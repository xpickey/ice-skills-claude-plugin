# Questioning Frameworks Reference

This file contains the nine frameworks a B2B enterprise software practitioner must be fluent in. Each framework is described with its origin, its mechanics, the stage of the deal or account lifecycle where it is strongest, and enterprise software examples that illustrate it in practice. Use this file whenever a framework selection decision is needed, or whenever the user asks about any specific framework by name.

## 1. SPIN Selling

**Origin.** Neil Rackham, synthesizing analysis of more than thirty-five thousand B2B sales calls. The most rigorously researched questioning framework in the professional selling literature.

**Mechanics.** Four question types used in sequence: Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-Payoff.

*Situation questions* establish current state — which ERP is running today, how many legal entities consolidate, where the data warehouse sits. Keep Situation brief; buyers find it boring and the seller can often prepare the answers before the call through desk research.

*Problem questions* surface pain points — where does the close break down, what manual work concentrates where, what has been tried that did not work. This is the pivot point of the conversation.

*Implication questions* extend each problem into downstream consequences — if the close stays at ten days, what does that cost in working capital, auditor comfort, board responsiveness, finance-team attrition. Implication is the step most sellers skip, and the single highest-leverage improvement available to most teams.

*Need-Payoff questions* invite the buyer to articulate the value of the solution themselves — if the close ran in three days, what would your team do with the recovered week, how would the CFO's Board pack change. Need-Payoff is the moment the buyer sells the solution to themselves.

**When strongest.** Discovery conversations, especially the second or third meeting when context is established and pain is ready to be deepened. SPIN is the core discovery spine around which every other framework orbits.

**Enterprise software example.** For an Oracle Fusion Cloud ERP pursuit: *Situation* — "which Fusion modules are live today and which remain on E-Business Suite?"; *Problem* — "where in the monthly close does the hybrid architecture cost you the most time?"; *Implication* — "if that double-handling continues through your IPO preparation next year, what does your auditor likely say?"; *Need-Payoff* — "if finance worked on a single cloud platform by Q3, what would the first win be for you personally?"

**Discipline.** Resist jumping from Situation to Solution without completing Problem and Implication. Most losing calls skip that middle; most winning calls spend more time there than anywhere else.

## 2. MEDDPICC

**Origin.** Jack Napoli and the PTC sales team of the 1990s, evolved by the broader B2B sales community. Now a de facto qualification standard across enterprise software.

**Mechanics.** An eight-letter qualification scorecard — not a conversation framework, but the private discipline the seller applies after every customer interaction.

- **M** — *Metrics.* Quantified business outcomes the buyer expects ("close days from ten to three", "procurement cycle time down thirty percent").
- **E** — *Economic Buyer.* The single person who can release budget unilaterally. Usually the CFO or CEO. Most losing deals never reached the Economic Buyer.
- **D** — *Decision Criteria.* The rational criteria on which the vendor will be judged — functional fit, TCO, implementation risk, local support.
- **D** — *Decision Process.* The sequence and calendar of steps the committee will follow — scoring, shortlist, BAFO, Board approval.
- **P** — *Paper Process.* Legal and procurement workflow — MSA, DPA, e-bidding rules for government, audit sign-off.
- **I** — *Identify Pain.* Top-ranked pain in the buyer's own words. If the buyer cannot articulate a pain that is urgent and expensive, there is no deal.
- **C** — *Champion.* Internal advocate with authority, credibility, and personal motivation to see you win. Not every contact is a Champion.
- **C** — *Competition.* Named vendors, incumbent platforms, the option to do nothing, and internal build.

**When strongest.** After every customer interaction, as a private five-minute scorecard update. Any letter scored below 5/10 becomes the questioning agenda for the next conversation.

**Enterprise software example.** After a discovery call with a Thai manufacturer evaluating SAP S/4HANA, the AE scores: M — 7 (three quantified metrics confirmed); E — 4 (met Finance Director but not CFO); D-Criteria — 6; D-Process — 3 (Board calendar unknown); P — 5; I — 8; C — 6 (Finance Director is supportive but has not been tested); C-Competition — 7 (Oracle and Infor named). The next meeting's agenda is driven by the Economic Buyer and Decision-Process gaps.

**Discipline.** Treat MEDDPICC as a questioning backlog, not a checkbox. Every low-confidence letter is a question yet to be asked.

## 3. Sandler Pain Funnel

**Origin.** David Sandler, The Sandler Training organization. One of the oldest and most replicated probing techniques in B2B selling.

**Mechanics.** A six-layer sequenced probe that takes a surface complaint and drills to emotional and financial pain.

- Layer 1 — "Can you give me a specific example?"
- Layer 2 — "How long has this been going on?"
- Layer 3 — "What have you tried to fix it?"
- Layer 4 — "What was the impact when those attempts did not work?"
- Layer 5 — "How much do you think this is costing?"
- Layer 6 — "How do you feel about that cost?"

**When strongest.** When the buyer says something vague ("our current system is slow"), the Pain Funnel is the fastest path to knowing whether the statement is worth anything. Also the best diagnostic for whether a deal is real: if the buyer cannot pass Layer 3, pain is not serious; if they cannot pass Layer 5, the Economic Buyer will not sign.

**Enterprise software example.** Buyer says "reconciliation is painful." Layer 1: "Can you walk me through the last reconciliation that went badly?" Layer 2: "How long has this been an issue?" Layer 3: "What has your team tried?" Layer 4: "What happened when those fixes did not hold?" Layer 5: "Roughly what is this costing in time and headcount?" Layer 6: "When the Board asks about it, how do you feel going into that conversation?"

**Discipline.** Do not skip layers. Each layer earns the right to ask the next.

## 4. Challenger Sale

**Origin.** Matthew Dixon and Brent Adamson, CEB research (later Gartner). The research found that in complex B2B deals, the highest-performing sellers were not relationship builders but Challengers — sellers who teach the customer something new, tailor the message to the individual, and take control of the conversation.

**Mechanics.** The signature move is the *provocative question* — a reframe grounded in domain knowledge that challenges a comfortable assumption the buyer did not know they had.

**When strongest.** Once per meeting, at the moment the conversation has established context and the seller has earned standing. Used well, it is the most memorable moment of the meeting and the reason the seller is remembered at shortlist time.

**Enterprise software example.** "Your three closest peers in the F&B sector have closed in four days for the last eighteen months; your ten-day close is now the outlier. What would it mean for your cost of capital if your lenders started ranking you below them on financial transparency?"

**Discipline.** Provocative questions require data behind them. An unprepared Challenger question sounds arrogant and ends the conversation. Use sparingly — one per meeting is the ceiling.

## 5. Five Whys

**Origin.** Taiichi Ohno, the Toyota Production System. The simplest and most underrated questioning tool.

**Mechanics.** Ask "why?" five times in sequence, each time drilling from symptom toward root cause.

**When strongest.** When a surface problem statement is vague, when the seller suspects the stated reason is not the real reason, or when the buyer offers a convenient explanation too quickly.

**Enterprise software example.** "The close takes ten days." → *Why?* — "Intercompany reconciliation is manual." → *Why?* — "Legal entities use different charts of accounts." → *Why?* — "Growth was through acquisition and never rationalized." → *Why?* — "The last transformation paused in 2022." → *Why?* — "The Board lost confidence in the previous vendor." The real agenda for this conversation is a vendor-failure reset, not a cost-of-close pitch.

**Discipline.** Five Whys works only with the permission of the buyer. Frame it softly in Thai contexts: "may I ask you to walk me one layer deeper on this?"

## 6. Socratic Method

**Origin.** Plato's dialogues; the ancient technique of teaching through questions rather than statements. In enterprise software, the advisor's highest-status move.

**Mechanics.** Rather than telling the buyer the right answer, the advisor asks a sequence of questions that lead the buyer to the answer themselves. The buyer then owns the conclusion.

**When strongest.** When the advisor needs the buyer to own a decision — implementation phasing, scope discipline, change-management investment, executive sponsorship. Also strong in QBRs and renewal conversations where the CSM wants the Economic Buyer to articulate value without being told.

**Enterprise software example.** Instead of "you should phase the implementation," run: "If we attempt everything in one go-live, what risks do you see? How have previous programs of that scale landed in your organization? If we phased by function, what would Phase 1 need to include to demonstrate value? Who would be unhappy with that phasing, and why?" The buyer arrives at phasing on their own.

**Discipline.** Socratic questioning is the difference between a vendor and a trusted advisor. It requires patience and refusal to shortcut to an opinion.

## 7. Strategic Selling (Miller Heiman)

**Origin.** Robert Miller, Stephen Heiman, Tad Tuleja. Sometimes called the Blue Sheet methodology.

**Mechanics.** Structures a buying committee into four roles, each asked different questions because each measures success differently:

- *Economic Buyer.* Asked about business outcomes, strategic fit, risk.
- *User Buyer.* Asked about day-to-day work, friction, time.
- *Technical Buyer.* Asked about architecture, integration, security, compliance.
- *Coach.* Asked about politics, history, what the committee will actually do.

**When strongest.** In any pursuit where the buying committee is five or more people, or in any Thai enterprise where hierarchy makes role-tailored questioning mandatory.

**Enterprise software example.** In a Fusion Cloud pursuit at a Thai bank: the CFO (Economic Buyer) is asked about regulatory capital and cost-to-income; the Head of Finance Operations (User Buyer) is asked about reconciliation friction and month-end overtime; the CIO (Technical Buyer) is asked about OCI data sovereignty and integration to the core banking system; the internal program-office lead (Coach) is asked about the Board's appetite and the history of the previous two vendor selections.

**Discipline.** An Economic Buyer asked a User Buyer question gets frustrated; a User Buyer asked an Economic Buyer question gets defensive. Map roles before the meeting.

## 8. Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD)

**Origin.** Clayton Christensen, refined by Bob Moesta, Tony Ulwick, and others. The interview technique of choice for solution consultants.

**Mechanics.** Rather than asking what the user wants the software to do (which yields a feature list), JTBD asks what job the user is trying to get done (which yields an outcome). The canonical opener is the *switching moment*: "Walk me through the last time you hired a new system to do this job. What happened on the day you decided the old way was not working?"

**When strongest.** Solution design workshops, RFP response preparation, product roadmap conversations during QBRs.

**Enterprise software example.** Instead of "what do you need in an EPM system?", ask "walk me through the last Board pack you produced; what would you have wanted to do differently if the tools had cooperated? What is the job you are hiring EPM to do — help me consolidate in two days instead of five, or help me forecast with confidence the CEO will trust, or something else?"

**Discipline.** Protect against the most common failure mode of enterprise software design — building exactly what the user asked for and missing what the user needed. The JTBD output is a clean job statement ("help me close confidently in four days so I can spend the saved week on forward-looking analysis") that becomes the design brief.

## 9. Design Thinking — How Might We and laddering

**Origin.** Stanford d.school, IDEO, IBM Enterprise Design Thinking, SAP AppHaus. Two specific questioning techniques are indispensable in solution workshops.

### How Might We (HMW)

Transforms a problem statement into a design brief. "The close takes too long" becomes "how might we compress the close to four days without adding finance headcount." The reframe unlocks solution generation because *how might we* is the grammatical form that invites proposals, while the original problem invites only commiseration.

**Discipline.** Every major pain surfaced in discovery should be rephrased as an HMW before the solution workshop begins.

### Why / What / How laddering

Moves a conversation vertically along the problem-solution stack.

- *Why* moves up toward purpose.
- *How* moves down toward mechanism.
- *What* stays level and surfaces artifacts.

**Enterprise software example.** The user requests a "dashboard." Ladder: *Why* — "what decision will the dashboard drive?"; *For whom* — "who reads it, at what cadence?"; *What* — "what three numbers would make it succeed or fail?"; *How* — "how often will it be refreshed and from what sources?"; *How will we know* — "what behavior change would tell us it is working?" A feature request becomes a design specification in a single conversation.

## Framework selection quick reference

| Stage | Primary framework | Supporting frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Prospecting / first meeting | SPIN-Situation + one Challenger | Strategic Selling (Coach) |
| Discovery | SPIN (full funnel) | Sandler Pain Funnel, Five Whys, Strategic Selling |
| Qualification (private) | MEDDPICC | — |
| RFP response | JTBD + Strategic Selling | SPIN on ambiguous requirements |
| Solution design workshop | JTBD + Design Thinking HMW + laddering | Socratic on scope decisions |
| Demo / Proof | Discovery-driven scene scripting + Need-Payoff | Implication on gaps |
| Negotiation | Open questions + silence | Socratic on trade-offs |
| Close / Handoff | Confirmatory + commitment | MEDDPICC-refresh |
| Onboarding | Baseline + adoption probes | Sandler on friction |
| QBR | Accountability against Metrics | Socratic on forward-looking |
| Renewal | Champion health + risk-surface | MEDDPICC-refresh |
| Expansion | JTBD for new problem areas | Challenger on peer benchmarks |
| Escalation / Churn risk | Direct truth-seeking | Sandler Layer 6 (feeling) |
