# Design Thinking Complement for Questioning

Read this file during solution-design workshops, customer envisioning sessions, co-creation, or any stage where the questioning purpose has shifted from *qualifying a pain* to *designing a solution*. Design Thinking adds four assets that extend the Pre-Sales / Solution Consultant toolkit beyond SPIN/MEDDPICC: empathy mapping, point-of-view (POV) statements, stakeholder personas, and the JTBD interview script. Each translates raw discovery answers into a shape the implementation team can act on.

## Where Design Thinking fits in the questioning lifecycle

Design Thinking does not replace SPIN or MEDDPICC; it follows them. SPIN surfaces the pain; MEDDPICC qualifies the deal; Design Thinking translates the qualified pain into a designable solution. The skill's questioning agenda shifts accordingly.

The typical flow in an enterprise software engagement:

1. AE-led discovery calls surface two or three quantified pain points (SPIN + Sandler).
2. MEDDPICC qualification confirms the deal is real.
3. Pre-Sales / SC runs a solution workshop where the Design Thinking toolkit takes over.
4. Output of the workshop feeds the demo script, the proposal, and (later) the implementation backlog.

In this middle phase, the questioning character changes: fewer probing "why" questions about pain, more generative "how might we" questions about solution possibilities, with the customer as co-designer rather than interviewee.

## Stakeholder persona card

A persona card is a one-page synthesis of a stakeholder type that the solution must serve. For enterprise software engagements, four to seven personas is typical — covering Economic Buyer, Technical Buyer, User Buyer(s), Champion, and sometimes Influencer and End User.

Each persona card carries: name and role, primary goals, primary frustrations, success metrics, typical day, constraints, and key questions for the solution to answer. Populate each card through a dedicated questioning pass.

### Questions to populate a persona card

*Role and authority.* "What is this person responsible for, what are they measured on, and what decisions can they make without escalation?" / "Who do they report to, and what does their manager expect from them on this initiative?"

*Day-in-the-life.* "Walk me through a typical week for this person. Which recurring meetings do they attend, and which systems do they touch every day?"

*Primary frustrations.* "What in this role causes the most rework, the most overtime, or the most unnecessary escalation?" / "What does a bad week look like for this person, and what typically caused it?"

*Success criteria.* "What does good look like for this person twelve months from now, in their own language? How would they know the solution worked?"

*Constraints.* "What are the 'never-do' constraints for this person — policies, audit requirements, regulatory rules, internal politics — that the solution must respect?"

*Channel and style.* "How does this person prefer to consume information — live demos, written briefings, peer testimonials, analyst reports? And how formally do we need to engage?"

### Persona card template

```

- Name convention: [e.g., "Khun Somchai, Finance Director"]
- Reports to: [EB role]

- Key question the solution must answer for this persona:
```

## Empathy mapping — Say / Think / Do / Feel

The empathy map is a four-quadrant canvas that captures what a stakeholder says out loud (the rational layer), what they think privately (the unspoken layer), what they do (observed behaviour), and what they feel (the emotional layer). It is best used after the first two or three discovery sessions, when enough raw material exists to populate all four quadrants.

The questioning discipline for each quadrant:

*Say.* Direct quotes from the discovery calls. "Walk me through what you actually said in that conversation with your CFO about the close."

*Think.* Probed through follow-up questions that invite the unspoken. "And behind the scenes, what is the concern that you haven't voiced to the Board yet?" / "What would you not want the vendor to hear you say?"

*Do.* Observed behaviour or workarounds. "When the system doesn't give you what you need, what do you actually do instead?" / "Can I watch you do a day-three close step end-to-end?"

*Feel.* Named emotions rather than positions. "How do you feel when that escalation lands on your desk at 10 pm during close week?" / "What is the moment in the cycle where stress peaks?"

An empathy map that populates all four quadrants produces a stakeholder understanding that a pure SPIN discovery often misses. It is particularly powerful in Thai contexts where the *think* and *feel* quadrants must often be surfaced in private one-on-one channels rather than in the group workshop.

## Point-of-View (POV) statements

A POV statement is a tight synthesis of the discovered insight in the form: "[User / persona] needs [need, in verb form] because [surprising insight]." The discipline is in the last clause — the *because*. If the insight is obvious, the POV is not ready. If the insight is surprising even to the user themselves, the POV is ready.

### Questions to produce a POV statement

*On the user.* "Of all the stakeholders we've mapped, who is the person whose unmet need most needs to be solved first, and why them?"

*On the need.* "What is this person actually trying to get done, in their own verbs, not ours?"

*On the insight.* "What did we learn about this person that we didn't know before this workshop — something that, if we hadn't discovered it, our solution would have missed?"

### Examples

*Weak POV (insight is not surprising):* "The Finance Director needs a faster close because the current close is slow."

*Stronger POV (insight is specific and surprising):* "The Finance Director needs to be able to certify the month-end numbers to the CFO before the fifth business day, because the Board now receives a pre-read on day six and her team has been working weekends to make that window — she is losing senior talent because of it."

The second version generates a different demo, a different proposal, and a different implementation conversation than the first. POV quality is the single highest-leverage move in the solution workshop.

## How Might We (HMW) question generator

Once the POV is stable, the ideation phase opens with HMW questions. The HMW is a deliberately open question that invites divergent solution ideas without constraining them yet. The discipline is in the altitude — too narrow and the HMW pre-commits to one solution ("how might we automate the reconciliation screen?"), too broad and it generates nothing useful ("how might we improve finance?").

### HMW altitude calibration

From any POV, derive three to five HMWs at progressively different altitudes:

1. *The tight HMW* — close to the named pain. "How might we certify month-end numbers by day five?"
2. *The adjacent HMW* — reframes the job. "How might we change what 'certify' means so less manual work is required?"
3. *The upstream HMW* — moves the problem earlier. "How might we reduce the number of manual entries that need certifying in the first place?"
4. *The stretch HMW* — reframes the goal entirely. "How might we replace the monthly certification cycle with continuous close?"

Run ideation on all four altitudes. The best solutions often live at the *adjacent* or *upstream* level — tight HMWs produce small improvements; stretch HMWs produce transformation; the middle two produce the practical, sellable, implementable wins.

### Thai-calibrated HMW

In Thai workshop contexts, HMWs work well because they are collective in form ("we" rather than "you") and invitational rather than accusatory. They give every workshop participant — senior and junior — a safe way to contribute an idea without placing anyone in a position of disagreement.

## JTBD interview script

The Jobs-To-Be-Done interview probes for the moment the customer decided to "hire" a system (or a process, or a vendor) to do a job. The script is distinctive because it asks about a specific past decision rather than a general preference, which surfaces far more actionable information.

### The JTBD opener and follow-ups

*Opener.* "Walk me through the last time you decided to bring in a new system to do [the job]. Start from the moment you first thought something needed to change."

*Follow-up 1 — First Thought.* "What was happening at the time that made you first think something needed to change?"

*Follow-up 2 — Passive Looking.* "What did you do first? Who did you talk to? What did you read?"

*Follow-up 3 — Active Looking.* "When did you move from thinking about it to actually evaluating options?"

*Follow-up 4 — Deciding.* "What were the candidates in your consideration set, and how did each one drop out?"

*Follow-up 5 — Ongoing Use.* "Looking at that decision today, knowing what you now know, would you make the same decision? What would you change?"

*Follow-up 6 — The non-obvious criterion.* "Was there any criterion that was important to you personally — not in the RFP, not in the business case — that you would not have been comfortable raising publicly?"

### Why JTBD is powerful

It surfaces the *actual* buying criteria rather than the stated ones. It reveals the anxiety that drove the purchase — a different and more actionable signal than the stated need. It gives the Pre-Sales team a language the buyer recognizes, because it is the language of their own recent experience.

## Value Proposition Canvas

The Value Proposition Canvas is Alex Osterwalder's two-sided canvas that maps the solution's *products/services*, *pain relievers*, and *gain creators* against the customer's *jobs*, *pains*, and *gains*. Populate it after personas and POVs are stable.

### Questions to populate the customer side

*Customer jobs.* "What is this person actually trying to get done — the functional job, the emotional job, the social job?" (Functional: close the books. Emotional: be trusted by the CFO. Social: be seen by peers as modern.)

*Customer pains.* "What goes wrong when this person tries to do the job today? What obstacles, frustrations, and risks recur?"

*Customer gains.* "What would the person gain — in substantive outcomes, emotional relief, reputation — if the job were done better?"

### Questions to populate the solution side

*Products and services.* "Of our capabilities, which ones are relevant to this customer's jobs?"

*Pain relievers.* "Which of our capabilities specifically remove or reduce one of the named pains?"

*Gain creators.* "Which of our capabilities produce one of the named gains that the customer actually wants?"

A Value Proposition Canvas where the two sides do not match is a pre-sold proposal-disaster. The workshop has caught it in time.

## Workshop agenda templates

### 90-minute discovery workshop

- *0–10 min* Opening, introductions, agenda-setting.
- *10–25 min* Persona card completion for the Economic Buyer and Champion.
- *25–60 min* Process walkthrough using JTBD opener plus SPIN follow-ups.
- *60–80 min* Empathy-map populate (four quadrants).
- *80–90 min* Summarize three candidate POV statements; agree who will confirm which with whom.

### Half-day envisioning workshop

- *0–30 min* Opening, personas recap, POV finalization.
- *30–60 min* HMW generation across four altitudes (tight, adjacent, upstream, stretch).
- *60–120 min* Ideation on the top two HMWs using Crazy 8s or 6-3-5 Brainwriting.
- *120–180 min* Convergence using Impact-Effort matrix; select top three ideas.
- *180–210 min* Storyboard the top idea (six panels).
- *210–240 min* Value Proposition Canvas draft; next-steps.

### One-day solution sprint

- *Morning* Personas, POVs, HMWs.
- *Afternoon* Ideation, convergence, storyboard, value case draft.
- *End of day* Executive readout (ten slides) generated live.

Each of these templates is a questioning agenda as much as it is a workshop agenda. Every timed block has a primary question that drives it.

## Handoff protocol

When the Design Thinking questioning phase completes, the output feeds three downstream artifacts:

1. *The demo script* — each demo scene opens with a question tied to the POV and closes with a Need-Payoff.
2. *The proposal* — structured around the Value Proposition Canvas, with each pain reliever and gain creator tied to a named capability.
3. *The implementation backlog* — populated from the HMW ideation, with each idea that survived convergence becoming a backlog item with acceptance criteria.

The handoff is not complete until all three artifacts have been drafted and reviewed against the personas, POVs, and Value Proposition Canvas. Skipping the review is the most common source of post-signature scope disputes.

## Integration with the primary questioning frameworks

Design Thinking extends rather than replaces the primary frameworks:

- *Empathy mapping* extends SPIN's *Situation* and *Problem* questions into the *think* and *feel* layers.
- *POV statements* extend *Implication* by naming the consequences in first-person user language.
- *HMW questions* open the solution space that Need-Payoff starts to hint at.
- *JTBD* deepens discovery by asking about a specific past decision rather than a general preference.
- *Persona cards* provide the scaffolding for MEDDPICC's *Decision Criteria* and *Decision Process* work.

The skilled Pre-Sales Consultant blends the two toolkits without friction — starting with SPIN on a discovery call, bringing Design Thinking into the workshop, and returning to SPIN for the commercial conversation.
