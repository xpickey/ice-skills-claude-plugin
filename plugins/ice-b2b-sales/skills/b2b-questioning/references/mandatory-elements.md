# The Ten Mandatory Elements of a Great Question

Use this file whenever producing, scoring, or revising a question. A question that carries all ten elements does commercial work; a question missing two produces less; a question missing five produces nothing and often damages trust.

## 1. Purpose

Before the question leaves the mouth, the practitioner must be able to complete the sentence "I am asking this to ___" with one clear outcome: confirm a hypothesis, surface a pain, identify a stakeholder, quantify an impact, transition the conversation, test a commitment. Questions without purpose produce conversation without direction, and experienced buyers feel it within ten minutes.

*Diagnostic test.* Pause before asking. If the purpose is unclear, the question is not ready.

## 2. Tense

A question is located in past, present, or future, and the three tenses do different jobs.

- *Past tense* — surfaces evidence and history. "What happened the last time you ran a consolidation?"
- *Present tense* — surfaces current state and pain. "How does the close run today?"
- *Future tense* — surfaces vision and buying criteria. "What would success look like twelve months after go-live?"

A conversation that stays in one tense will miss two-thirds of the picture. A disciplined discovery traverses all three per topic.

## 3. Scope

The question must be pitched at the right altitude for the moment. Too broad ("tell me about your business") produces a brochure answer. Too narrow ("what version of Fusion are you on?") closes down the conversation.

*Diagnostic test.* The opening word plus the qualifier usually reveals scope. "Walk me through how the month-end close runs from day one to day ten" has altitude, specificity, and a landing zone.

## 4. Specificity

A specific question outperforms a vague question by a factor of ten. "What are your challenges?" yields nothing useful. "Where in the eight-day close process does the team work the longest hours?" yields the answer that unlocks the demo.

Specificity requires preparation — the practitioner must know enough about the domain to ask at the right resolution. For Thai enterprise contexts, specificity often includes regulatory or local references (BoT circular, Revenue Department guideline, PDPA clause) that signal domain depth.

## 5. Neutrality

The question must not contain its own answer. "Don't you find manual consolidation frustrating?" has already told the buyer what to say. The buyer either agrees politely (giving nothing useful) or pushes back defensively (losing trust).

Neutral phrasing gives the buyer room to surprise the seller, which is where the useful information lives.

*Rewrite test.* If the question contains "don't you", "wouldn't you", "isn't it true that", or any version of these, rewrite.

## 6. Cognitive load

A good question is answerable in the time available without forcing the buyer to think in public for ninety seconds. If the expected answer is longer than sixty seconds, precede the question with context and break it into two.

The CFO in a forty-five-minute meeting cannot be asked "describe your finance transformation strategy" cold; they can be asked "given the Board's cost-of-capital pressure, what is the top finance priority for the next twelve months?"

## 7. Sequencing

Questions do not stand alone; they arrange into a funnel — broad to narrow, past to present to future, Situation to Problem to Implication to Need-Payoff. A brilliant question asked in the wrong sequence produces nothing because the buyer is not ready for it. A mediocre question in the right sequence produces insight.

See the funnel-sequencing discipline below.

## 8. Follow-up

A question is not finished when the answer arrives; it is finished when the practitioner has probed at least once into the answer. Probing moves are usually "tell me more about that", "what did you mean by ___", or a Five Whys extension.

Novices collect answers; masters mine them. When drafting questions, always draft at least one follow-up probe per primary question.

## 9. Silence

After the question is asked, hold the silence for at least three seconds beyond the point of discomfort. Most high-value information in a discovery call arrives in the second pass — after the buyer has given a polite first answer, paused, and then continued when the silence invites them to.

Sellers who rush to fill silence with their own voice lose this information every time. In Thai business contexts, where pauses are culturally longer, three seconds often extends naturally to five or seven.

## 10. Documentation

Every material question and material answer should be captured — in the CRM, in workshop minutes, in account notes. Questions that are asked and forgotten are asked again by the next vendor meeting, and the buyer notices.

Disciplined documentation of prior questions is the invisible infrastructure of a long-running enterprise account.

## Funnel sequencing discipline

The standard sequence used across SPIN, Sandler, and consultative selling is the funnel. Traversed once per topic; a complex discovery conversation may traverse it three or four times across different business processes.

1. *Broad opening questions* — set context and let the buyer frame the agenda.
2. *Problem questions* — surface pain in the buyer's own words.
3. *Implication questions* — quantify and extend the cost of the pain.
4. *Need-Payoff questions* — invite the buyer to articulate the value of the solution.

Two mistakes collapse the funnel:

**Inverting.** Starting with a closed specific question ("do you use Oracle or SAP today?") before establishing context. The buyer feels interrogated rather than consulted.

**Skipping.** Jumping from Situation to Need-Payoff without doing the Problem and Implication work. The buyer has not accepted that the pain is worth solving and hears the Need-Payoff question as sales pressure.

Both mistakes are common and both are avoidable by conscious funnel discipline.

## Scoring rubric

When reviewing a draft question or a transcript, score each question on a 1–5 scale against each of the ten elements. A question scoring 4+ on every element is ready; a question scoring 3 on any element needs revision; a question scoring 2 or less on three or more elements should be discarded and rewritten.

| Element | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Purpose | No clear purpose | Vaguely relevant | Purposeful but generic | Specific purpose | Strategic purpose tied to MEDDPICC/SPIN |
| Tense | Mismatched to moment | Single tense | Mixed uncertainly | Deliberate tense | Tense plays a role in insight |
| Scope | Wrong altitude | Too broad or too narrow | Adequate | Well-calibrated | Calibrated + anchored to a known fact |
| Specificity | Generic | Mildly specific | Industry-specific | Customer-specific | Customer+process-specific |
| Neutrality | Contains answer | Subtly leading | Neutral | Neutral and inviting | Opens unexpected answers |
| Cognitive load | Impossible in time | Too heavy | Manageable | Appropriate | Perfectly sized |
| Sequencing | Out of order | Isolated | Fits funnel | Advances funnel | Advances + sets up next question |
| Follow-up | None planned | Generic probe | One probe ready | Two probes ready | Probes mapped to likely answer branches |
| Silence | Rushed delivery | Short pause | 3-second hold planned | 3-5 second + observation | Comfortable with 7-10 seconds |
| Documentation | Not captured | Captured in memory | Captured in notes | Captured in CRM with tag | Captured + next-meeting-agenda updated |

Present the rubric output with specific rewrite suggestions for any question scoring below 4 on any element.
