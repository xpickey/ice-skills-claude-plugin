# Role-Specific Playbooks

This file contains stage-by-stage questioning agendas for the three primary roles in enterprise software: Account Executive (Sales), Solution Consultant (Pre-Sales), and Customer Success Manager (Post-Sales). Read the relevant section when the user's role and stage are identified in Step 1.

## Table of contents

- Account Executive playbook — prospecting through close and handoff
- Pre-Sales / Solution Consultant playbook — discovery workshop through design validation
- Customer Success Manager playbook — onboarding through renewal and escalation

## Account Executive playbook

The AE owns the commercial relationship from first prospect conversation through signature and handoff to delivery. Questioning agenda organized around the six stages of an enterprise software deal.

### Prospecting

The first contact is not the moment for deep discovery; it is the moment to earn thirty minutes on the calendar. Keep the questioning agenda narrow and focused on relevance.

Confirm two things only: (1) the prospect has the pain the solution addresses, and (2) the prospect is senior enough that the first meeting will not be wasted. Frame questions as curiosity about a public signal — an earnings call comment, a regulatory filing, a leadership change.

Example: "I noticed your Q4 commentary flagged working-capital pressure; is treasury optimization a live priority on your roadmap for this year?"

### Discovery

The most important stage and where the largest AE-skill gap typically shows. A proper discovery runs sixty to ninety minutes, covers three to five business processes, traverses the SPIN funnel on each, and closes with confirmed next steps.

Opening move is almost always a Situation question wide enough to let the buyer set the agenda: "help me understand where the finance transformation journey stands for you today". Pivot to Problem when the buyer names their first friction; immediately probe with a Sandler Pain Funnel sequence.

The discovery call is not finished until the AE has:

1. At least three quantified pain points.
2. Identified the Economic Buyer (or the path to them).
3. Named the Decision Process calendar.

If any of these three are missing, another discovery call must be scheduled before any demo or proposal work.

### Qualification

Private work done after discovery using MEDDPICC as the scorecard. Each letter is scored on 0–10 confidence, and any letter below 5 becomes the questioning agenda for the next customer interaction. Deals that score below 40 total should be deprioritized until the score rises or the pursuit is dropped.

Discipline: ask the hard questions rather than guess. "Do we know the Economic Buyer is X?" is answered by asking the Champion, "besides the CFO, who else has budget authority on a commitment of this size?"

### Demo and Proof

Demos collapse most often because they were scripted before discovery was complete. A demo that answers questions the buyer did not ask fails regardless of scripting elegance.

The AE's questioning role is to build the demo script from confirmed pain points — every scene opens with a question tied to discovery ("you mentioned intercompany reconciliation takes two days; let me show you what that looks like in Fusion") and closes with a Need-Payoff ("if that ran in minutes rather than days, what would your team do with the time back?").

If a POC is required, the success criteria must be extracted in discovery, written down, and countersigned.

### Negotiation

Questions designed to surface what the buyer actually values versus what they are positioning. Open questions such as "help me understand which of these commercial terms is most important to you" generate more information than closed price questions.

Silence after a price push is the single highest-leverage move in the entire negotiation stage. Never break silence after offering a price; a buyer who breaks silence first almost always does so with useful information.

### Close and Handoff

The final AE questioning agenda is to brief delivery fully on what was sold and why. A disciplined handoff meeting uses structured questions to transfer knowledge:

- What were the three top pain points?
- Who is the Economic Buyer and what do they measure?
- What are the success metrics the customer expects?
- Who is the Champion and what is their motivation?
- What risks did we agree to manage?

An AE who cannot answer these sold a deal that delivery will struggle to execute, and the account will churn at renewal.

## Pre-Sales / Solution Consultant playbook

The SC translates sales conversation into implementable design. Questioning is more technical, longer in cycle, and more workshop-driven than the AE's.

### Solution discovery workshop

After the AE has confirmed a qualified opportunity, the SC runs a half-day to multi-day workshop focused on process-level discovery. JTBD interview technique is the backbone: customer walks through the current state of each process end-to-end, noting moments of friction, workarounds, and data touchpoints.

Every process area (Record-to-Report, Procure-to-Pay, Order-to-Cash, Hire-to-Retire) has a standard question template the SC works through. Mandatory output: a process map, a pain register, and a set of design hypotheses to test.

### Fit-gap analysis

Test the preferred solution against discovered requirements. Questioning discipline: ask "does this process work for you as configured out of the box, or is there a specific scenario that breaks it?" for every major process.

Gaps are prioritized through implication questions — "if this gap is not closed, what is the impact on the business case?" A gap that no one can tie to the business case is a gap the customer does not truly need closed. Scope discipline depends on surfacing this.

### RFP response

When an RFP lands, the SC's first move is not to answer but to ask. A pre-response questioning session — formally as a bidder's conference or informally through the AE — tests the RFP's assumptions.

"Question 47 asks about multi-currency consolidation across seventeen entities; can you help us understand how the consolidation process works today so our response is precise?" Answers inform the response; unanswered questions are flagged as risks in the submission.

### Demo scripting and tailoring

Before each demo, the SC confirms three things: the business process the demo will follow, the stakeholders who will be in the room, and the key question each stakeholder needs answered. A demo without this preparation is generic, and generic demos lose.

### Design validation

Late sales cycle or early implementation, validation sessions test the proposed solution against the customer's own business. Hypothesis-confirmation questions: "if we design the chart of accounts this way, what breaks in your monthly Board pack? who would object to that design and why?"

Validation questions protect against the most expensive failure in implementation — a solution that passes sales-cycle review and fails post-go-live reality.

## Customer Success Manager playbook

The CSM owns the relationship from go-live through every subsequent renewal and expansion. Questioning agenda designed to surface adoption friction early, surface expansion opportunities early, and maintain intimacy of understanding that the sales team built at signature.

### Onboarding and first 90 days

In the first ninety days after go-live, the CSM replaces the AE and SC as the primary questioning relationship and confirms that the success metrics promised at signature are being measured.

Direct process-focused questions:

- "How is the close running at month one versus the baseline before?"
- "Where are the users getting stuck?"
- "Which of the agreed success metrics do we have clean baseline data for today?"

Every unanswered question is a follow-up commitment.

### Adoption reviews

Monthly or bi-monthly, a structured adoption review focused on usage data and user sentiment. The questioning loop is a forced-honesty sequence: "the usage data shows fifty percent of finance users have not logged in in the last thirty days; what is that telling us? what would it take to get adoption to eighty percent? what can we offer to accelerate?"

A CSM who shies away from the adoption question is the leading indicator of churn.

### Quarterly Business Review (QBR)

The single most important customer interaction of the year. A QBR is not a status report; it is a structured conversation about the health of the business case.

Mandatory four questions:

1. Looking back at the success metrics committed at go-live, where are we?
2. What has worked better than expected, and what has worked less well?
3. What new priorities have emerged in your business since we last met?
4. How is the Economic Buyer measuring the success of this investment today?

The QBR that does not ask these four questions is wasted calendar time.

### Renewal

Renewal questioning starts six months before the renewal date, not six weeks. Confirm the renewal case — budget, sponsor, success metrics, competition — and surface any risk early enough to mitigate.

- "Is there anyone new in the finance leadership who has not met us yet?"
- "What are they likely to ask about this contract?"
- "If we were to present a renewal case to the Board next quarter, what story would it tell?"

### Expansion and cross-sell

Expansion opportunities come from customers who have proven value in one area and are curious about adjacents. Forward-looking questions:

- "Now that the close is running in four days, where does your team want to focus next?"
- "What business problems are on the CFO's agenda that we have not talked about?"
- "If you could point this same team at one more problem, what would it be?"

### Escalation and churn risk

When signals flash — a drop in usage, a missed success metric, a leadership change, a sudden silence from the Champion — the CSM's questioning agenda becomes a risk triage.

Direct, uncomfortable questions:

- "Are we still the right partner for you?"
- "What would it take for you to consider switching?"
- "Who inside your organization is skeptical of the current platform, and can I meet them?"

These questions are hard to ask and harder still to hear the answer to, but they are the only questions that catch churn early enough to reverse.
