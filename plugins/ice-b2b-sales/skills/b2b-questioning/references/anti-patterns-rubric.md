# Anti-Patterns, Character Traits, and Self-Assessment Rubric

Read this file when reviewing a user's draft questions, scoring a call transcript, or helping the user diagnose why their discovery is not producing insight. It contains three linked resources: the twelve character traits of a master questioner, the fifteen questioning anti-patterns with corrective discipline for each, and a self-assessment rubric the user can apply to their own recent conversations.

## The twelve character traits of a master questioner

Character and skill are distinct. A practitioner can know every framework and still ask bad questions because the character is not calibrated to the work. When coaching a practitioner on questioning skill, return to character first if the frameworks are not landing.

### 1. Curiosity

Real curiosity is the practitioner's genuine interest in the buyer's business, context, people, and constraints — the fuel that keeps a conversation alive beyond the scripted agenda. Performative curiosity ("interesting…") is detected within minutes; authentic curiosity produces questions that could not have been prepared in advance because they respond to the specific answer just given.

*Development.* Read industry analyst reports, customer annual reports, and regulatory filings before every meeting. Curiosity compounds on substrate.

### 2. Patience with silence

The three-second hold after a question is the single most undertrained and highest-leverage discipline in consultative selling. Most practitioners break silence in under one second; the second sentence of a buyer's answer — the one that contains the actual insight — never arrives.

*Development.* Record own calls. Count the silence after questions. If the average is under three seconds, deliberately over-correct in the next three meetings to build tolerance.

### 3. Empathy

The ability to feel what the buyer is feeling — the quarterly pressure, the Board scrutiny, the restless team, the career risk riding on the transformation. Empathy changes question phrasing from audit to advocacy.

*Development.* Before every meeting, write two sentences: "what is this person's week actually like?" and "what are they hoping to avoid?" Then review these immediately after the meeting against what was observed.

### 4. Business acumen

The ability to connect a tactical answer to a P&L implication. A Procure-to-Pay cycle time is not a procurement metric; it is working-capital efficiency, supplier negotiating leverage, and balance-sheet shape. The practitioner who can make that connection out loud earns a seat at the executive table.

*Development.* Read the buyer company's 10-K (or Thai equivalent — SET filings, annual reports, MD&A). Translate three operational findings into executive-grade impact statements.

### 5. Domain knowledge

Deep fluency in the technology, regulatory environment, and implementation patterns that define the deal. In Thailand, this includes Revenue Department e-Tax requirements, Bank of Thailand circulars, PDPA nuances, New GFMIS workflow for public sector, and Oracle/SAP/NetSuite specific configuration patterns.

*Development.* Specialize. Generalists lose in enterprise software. Depth in one or two domains (Record-to-Report + Treasury, Procure-to-Pay + Supply Chain, Hire-to-Retire + Workforce Planning) produces ten times the question quality of shallow breadth.

### 6. Emotional intelligence

The real-time capacity to read the room — who has disengaged, who is posturing for a senior colleague, who is sending a subtle signal, who has gone quiet. Questions must be calibrated to what is happening in the room, not only to what was planned.

*Development.* After each meeting, name three emotional signals noticed (posture shift, interrupted sentence, deferential pause). Over time, the practice sharpens the sensor.

### 7. Humility

The willingness to be corrected, to ask a simple question after a complex one, to not know and to say so. Humility is the social precondition of curiosity — a practitioner who cannot say "I don't know" also cannot ask a question that admits not knowing.

*Development.* In every meeting, deliberately admit one thing unknown. Over time, this stops feeling risky and starts feeling powerful.

### 8. Discipline and preparation

Every material meeting is preceded by written preparation: five inputs clarified, the question set drafted, the MEDDPICC delta identified, the expected objections anticipated, the three-second silence rule internalized.

Practitioners who "wing it" in enterprise meetings consistently produce worse outcomes than practitioners who prepare for thirty minutes. The discipline is habitual, not inspirational.

*Development.* Build a pre-meeting template. Use it for every external meeting of material size for ninety days. The habit stabilizes.

### 9. Active listening

Hearing what was said, what was meant, what was not said, and what the buyer hesitated before saying. Active listening is not the absence of speaking; it is the full presence of attention with deliberate non-interruption and visible reflective cues.

*Development.* Practice paraphrasing. After a buyer finishes a paragraph, restate it in one sentence and ask if the restatement is accurate. This both confirms understanding and slows the conversation down productively.

### 10. Cultural sensitivity

In Thai contexts specifically, the ability to calibrate kreng jai, face, hierarchy, and bunkhun in every question delivered. In cross-cultural contexts, the ability to shift register between Thai-calibrated and Western-direct without losing the thread of the conversation.

*Development.* Read `references/thai-context.md` before high-stakes meetings. Practice the register table with a Thai-speaking colleague.

### 11. Courage

The willingness to ask the question that feels uncomfortable — the Economic Buyer question the Champion would prefer not to answer, the churn-risk question the CSM is worried about, the Why-Didn't-You-Pick-Us question after a lost deal. Courageous questions are the only questions that produce the information the practitioner actually needs.

*Development.* In every meeting, identify in advance the one question that feels hardest to ask. Ask it — with appropriate calibration. Record whether the answer was useful. Over time, courage becomes pattern recognition of payoff.

### 12. Objectivity

The discipline to hear the answer the buyer actually gave rather than the answer the practitioner hoped to hear. Confirmation-bias collection — hearing agreement where there was polite deflection — is the single most common cause of deal-loss in late-stage enterprise software.

*Development.* After each meeting, review notes with a neutral colleague who can push back on the interpretation. Over time, self-correction on confirmation bias becomes native.

## The fifteen questioning anti-patterns

Each anti-pattern is named, diagnosed, and paired with the corrective discipline. When reviewing a call transcript or a draft question set, run through the list — a question set with three or more of these anti-patterns is not ready.

### 1. Interrogation

*Pattern.* A rapid-fire sequence of closed questions ("do you use X? how long? how many users? what budget?") that feels to the buyer like a due-diligence checklist.

*Diagnosis.* The buyer answers in short, reluctant monosyllables and visibly disengages. No vocabulary is being surfaced and no follow-ups are generating.

*Corrective discipline.* Switch to one open question and let it run. "Walk me through how the month-end close actually runs from day one to day ten" replaces six closed questions and produces ten times the information.

### 2. Brochure dump

*Pattern.* The practitioner asks a Situation question and then, when the buyer pauses, fills the silence by explaining the product. The "question" becomes a disguised pitch.

*Diagnosis.* Most of the airtime is being spent by the seller. The buyer has stopped offering information.

*Corrective discipline.* Silence the inner sales pitch. Count to five after every question before saying anything. The buyer will fill the space if the practitioner does not.

### 3. Leading loop

*Pattern.* Every question is phrased in a way that suggests its desired answer. "Wouldn't you agree that…", "Isn't it the case that…", "Don't you find that…"

*Diagnosis.* Answers are all "yes, that's right" with no elaboration. Buyer has realised they can just agree politely.

*Corrective discipline.* Rewrite every question starting with "don't you" or "wouldn't you" into an open neutral form. "Wouldn't you agree consolidation is painful?" becomes "what does the consolidation process look like today?"

### 4. Assumption shortcut

*Pattern.* The practitioner assumes they know what the buyer's problem is based on industry or persona and skips the discovery.

*Diagnosis.* The demo or proposal lands on a problem the buyer did not actually have, and the deal stalls.

*Corrective discipline.* For each assumed problem, ask a validating question. "We often hear CFOs of listed Thai manufacturers say consolidation takes two weeks — how does it look for you?" lets the buyer confirm or correct.

### 5. Feature-probe

*Pattern.* Questions about whether the buyer uses a specific technical feature before the business problem has been established. "Do you use lockbox processing?" before understanding how the collections process actually works.

*Diagnosis.* The buyer treats the question as a technical screening and answers narrowly, missing the business conversation.

*Corrective discipline.* Always precede a feature probe with a business-process question. "Walk me through how collections works today" surfaces the context; "and within that, are you using lockbox or doing this manually?" probes specifically.

### 6. Orphaned question

*Pattern.* The practitioner asks a question, gets an answer, and moves on to the next unrelated question without probing.

*Diagnosis.* Answers feel like a survey rather than a conversation. The most valuable information — the second-pass detail — is never surfaced.

*Corrective discipline.* Draft one follow-up probe per primary question. After every answer, ask "tell me more about that" or "what did you mean by X" before moving on.

### 7. Seniority mismatch

*Pattern.* Asking a CFO an operational question they should not have to answer ("how long does daily reconciliation take?"), or asking a Finance Manager a strategic question they are not authorized to answer ("what is the Board's cost-of-capital thesis?").

*Diagnosis.* The senior stakeholder defers to their subordinate; the junior stakeholder hedges or declines.

*Corrective discipline.* Map each question to the right seniority before the meeting. Strategic questions to strategic personas; operational questions to operational personas.

### 8. Premature close

*Pattern.* A trial-close question is asked before the pain has been quantified. "If we could solve this, would you be interested in talking next steps?" after a single problem statement with no implication work.

*Diagnosis.* The buyer gives a soft yes that does not advance the deal; or the buyer pushes back and the practitioner has nothing to recover with.

*Corrective discipline.* The trial close is earned only after the implication work is done. If the cost of the pain has not been quantified, the trial close is premature.

### 9. Confirmation-bias collection

*Pattern.* The practitioner hears what they hoped to hear rather than what the buyer said. A polite "we'll take a look" becomes "they're interested"; a hedged "we'll see" becomes "we're making progress."

*Diagnosis.* MEDDPICC score is consistently higher than reality; the deal slips and the practitioner is repeatedly surprised.

*Corrective discipline.* Review meeting notes with a neutral colleague. Rewrite the interpretation in the buyer's own language. If the buyer did not use the word "committed," do not write "committed" in the CRM.

### 10. Silence-breaker

*Pattern.* The practitioner asks a good question and, after two seconds of buyer silence, rushes to rephrase, clarify, or add context. The first answer never arrives.

*Diagnosis.* The conversation lives entirely on the seller's first attempt; the real information that would have come on the buyer's reflection is never captured.

*Corrective discipline.* Internalize the three-second rule. Count to three in your head (in Thai contexts, count to five). Do not speak until the count completes or the buyer speaks.

### 11. Assumption-laden phrasing

*Pattern.* Questions that assume a prior answer. "Given that your close is slow, what's driving that?" when the buyer never said the close was slow.

*Diagnosis.* The buyer either politely accepts the frame (and you now cannot trust their answer) or pushes back ("actually our close is fine") and the discovery stalls.

*Corrective discipline.* Re-read every question out loud. If it contains an assumption about the buyer's state that has not yet been stated by the buyer, rewrite.

### 12. One-sided discovery

*Pattern.* The practitioner asks all the questions; the buyer never gets to ask theirs. The meeting feels like an interview of the buyer.

*Diagnosis.* The buyer's questions — which contain their real concerns — are never surfaced. The proposal ends up answering questions the buyer did not ask.

*Corrective discipline.* Build in two or three points in every meeting where you explicitly invite the buyer's questions: "before I ask the next one, what questions has this raised for you?"

### 13. Thai-register miscalibration

*Pattern.* A question that is well-formed in English is delivered directly to a Thai senior stakeholder without softening, causing face-loss or forcing polite fiction.

*Diagnosis.* The stakeholder's answer is shorter and more formal after the question than before; the energy in the meeting drops; the Champion looks uncomfortable.

*Corrective discipline.* Read `references/thai-context.md`. For every hard question, draft the softened Thai-calibrated phrasing before the meeting. Rehearse with a Thai-speaking colleague if stakes are high.

### 14. Unprepared Challenger

*Pattern.* Attempting a Challenger-style provocative question without sufficient preparation. The "reframe" is based on shallow industry knowledge and the buyer — who knows their industry better — dismisses it.

*Diagnosis.* Credibility dropped in the meeting. The Champion's body language shifted. Subsequent questions land less well.

*Corrective discipline.* A Challenger move earns its place only with deep preparation — a teaching insight grounded in research the buyer has not seen. If the preparation is not there, stay with SPIN and skip the provocation.

### 15. Undocumented conversation

*Pattern.* Material questions and answers are exchanged and not captured. The next meeting re-asks the same questions. The buyer notices and trust erodes.

*Diagnosis.* Second and third meetings re-cover ground. Multiple practitioners ask the same questions. The buyer begins to answer more briefly to save time.

*Corrective discipline.* Immediately after every meeting, capture in CRM: the three highest-value questions asked, the answers, the follow-up commitments, the MEDDPICC delta. Make this the final twenty minutes of the meeting block.

## The self-assessment rubric

Use this rubric to score a recent meeting or a draft question set on a 1–5 scale across eight dimensions. A total below 24 indicates material coaching need; a total of 32 or above indicates competent practice; a total of 36 or above indicates mastery.

| Dimension | 1 — Novice | 2 — Learning | 3 — Competent | 4 — Strong | 5 — Masterful |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Preparation | No written prep | Rough mental notes | Written agenda | Full five-input prep | Pre-meeting brief shared with team |
| Framework discipline | No framework used | One framework, inconsistently | SPIN funnel applied | Multiple frameworks integrated | Framework choice is deliberate and commented on |
| Question quality | Mostly closed / leading | Mixed quality | Mostly open + neutral | Ten elements consistently present | Questions produce surprise insights |
| Silence discipline | Rushes every pause | Some pauses held | 3 seconds most of the time | 3-5 seconds deliberate | Comfortable with 7-10 seconds |
| Active listening | Interrupts | Waits but thinks ahead | Paraphrases sometimes | Paraphrases deliberately | Captures unspoken signals |
| Thai calibration | None applied | Inconsistent register | Basic softening | Full four-dimension calibration | Uses Thai language moments deliberately |
| Courage | Avoids hard questions | Asks hard questions badly | Asks hard questions with preparation | Asks hard questions with calibration | Seeks out hard questions as diagnostic signal |
| Documentation | Notes in memory only | Rough personal notes | CRM updated same day | CRM + team brief | CRM + team brief + next-meeting agenda updated |

*How to use.* After each material meeting, score the meeting on each row. Track the eight-row score over time. The rows where the score is consistently 3 or below are the coaching agenda.

## Transcript review protocol

When the user provides a call transcript for review, follow this protocol to produce a structured critique:

**Step 1 — Count the questions.** Identify every question asked by the practitioner. Number them.

**Step 2 — Classify by type.** For each question, label the type (Open, Closed, Probing, Clarifying, Reflective, Hypothetical, Implication, Leading, Funnel, Confirmatory, Provocative) and the framework origin (SPIN-S/P/I/NP, MEDDPICC-letter, Sandler-layer, Challenger, Socratic, 5-Whys, JTBD, Design-Thinking).

**Step 3 — Score against the ten mandatory elements.** For each question, use the 1–5 rubric in `references/mandatory-elements.md`. Flag questions scoring below 4 on any element.

**Step 4 — Flag anti-patterns.** Check the transcript against the fifteen anti-patterns. Name each anti-pattern spotted with the timestamp or line number.

**Step 5 — Measure silence.** If the transcript contains timing, note the average silence-after-question. If it is under three seconds, flag as a discipline gap.

**Step 6 — Note what was not asked.** Equal weight to what was missed: Economic Buyer not identified, Decision Criteria not probed, Competitor presence not checked, Champion motivation not surfaced.

**Step 7 — Produce three next questions.** Based on the gaps found, produce the three highest-value questions the practitioner should ask in the next meeting, with framework tag, persona, and Thai calibration.

**Step 8 — Deliver the summary.** Structure the output as: "what was strong", "what was missed", "the three anti-patterns to correct", "the next three questions". Keep the tone coaching, not punishing. The practitioner needs to return to the next meeting confident, not bruised.
