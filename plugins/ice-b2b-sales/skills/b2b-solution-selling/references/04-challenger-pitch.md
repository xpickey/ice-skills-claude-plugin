# Reference 04 — Challenger Sale: Commercial Teaching Pitch

**Use when:** Differentiating against an entrenched incumbent. Reframing a buyer who is comparing on features or price. Building a first-meeting deck that earns the next meeting. Coaching a seller who is being out-positioned despite a better product.

Source: Dixon & Adamson, *The Challenger Sale* (2011) and *The Challenger Customer* (2015), based on CEB / Gartner research on 6,000+ reps.

---

## A. The Challenger insight

Across the five sales-rep profiles studied (Hard Worker, Relationship Builder, Lone Wolf, Reactive Problem Solver, Challenger), **Challengers won ~40% of complex deals; Relationship Builders won 7%.**

The Challenger wins because in complex enterprise deals — like cloud ERP, EPM, transformation services — the customer often **does not know what they do not know**. The Challenger teaches them, then offers a path.

## B. The three core capabilities

1. **TEACH for differentiation** — bring an insight the customer could not have got anywhere else
2. **TAILOR for resonance** — pitch the same insight differently to CFO, CIO, COO, Plant Manager
3. **TAKE CONTROL of the sale** — manage the process, push back on bad ideas, drive decision velocity

## C. The Commercial Teaching pitch — six steps in order

This is the structure for a first-meeting deck or executive briefing. It works because it earns the right to talk about your product *only at the very end*, after the buyer's worldview has shifted.

### Step 1 — Warmer (establish credibility, NOT discovery)
**Goal:** Show you understand their world without asking obvious questions.

Bad: "So tell me about your business…" (waste of EB time)
Good: "We've worked with three Thai food-processing groups in the THB 5–20bn revenue band who consolidated SAP / Oracle / NetSuite ERPs after acquisition. Two went live in 14 months; one took 22 months. The difference was data governance from week one. Before I share what I think you should consider, can I check — does that pattern look familiar?"

### Step 2 — Reframe (challenge the current thinking)
**Goal:** Introduce a new lens. Make the buyer think "I had not considered that."

"Most of our peers start with a 'lift and shift' migration. Our data shows that approach takes 30% longer and costs 40% more than a 'land and expand' design — going live on core GL + AP first, then layering on consolidation and planning. The reason: data quality issues surface earlier, scope is contained, and finance teams build muscle memory before they hit the hard modules."

### Step 3 — Rational Drowning (quantify the cost of inaction)
**Goal:** Make the cost of doing nothing tangible — in the customer's own numbers.

"At your current 18-day close, your finance team is working ~600 person-hours per close that could be doing analysis. That is roughly THB 10M/year fully loaded. If we add the deferred treasury decisions — at 60% forecast accuracy you are over-borrowing approximately THB 200M average daily balance — the real annual cost of the status quo is THB 14–18M. That is roughly the cost of the cloud licence over three years."

### Step 4 — Emotional Impact (story that makes it real)
**Goal:** Move from numbers to a story the buyer remembers.

"We had a Thai listed retail group — 6 brands, 8 entities — close at 19 days. They missed their bank covenant filing once. The covenant breach was minor; the lender repriced 25 bps anyway. The CFO's bonus was tied to debt cost — that one slip cost him ~THB 3M personally and triggered a board review of finance leadership. Six months later he was 'reassigned'. Their close is now 6 days. The new CFO talks about that 19-day close every quarterly review."

### Step 5 — A New Way (criteria for success — NOT product yet)
**Goal:** Establish the buying criteria. The customer agrees to *what* they need, before you say *who* provides it.

"Companies who do this well share four habits:
1. Cleanse data 6 months before go-live, not 6 weeks
2. Train finance users 6 weeks before go-live, not the week before
3. Run parallel for 5 days, not 30
4. Freeze customisation at 70% of requirements; do the last 30% in phase 2

If you adopt these criteria, your selection conversation looks completely different — you are no longer comparing modules, you are comparing *partner ability to deliver outcomes*."

### Step 6 — Your Solution (now and only now)
**Goal:** Show how you uniquely enable the new way.

"Oracle Cloud Financials' pre-built data models cover ~80% of what you need without configuration — that is why companies optimising for the four habits above pick Oracle ~65% of the time, per Gartner. Our delivery team has done six similar consolidations in Thai listed groups. Here is how we would sequence yours…"

## D. Tailoring the same pitch to different stakeholders

Same six-step pitch, different content per audience:

| Audience | Reframe emphasis | Rational Drowning | Emotional Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| CFO | Cost of capital, covenant risk | Borrowing cost, audit fees | Peer CFO career story |
| CIO | Technical debt, talent risk | DBA recruitment cost, security exposure | Peer CIO post-incident story |
| COO | Process velocity, customer impact | Inventory write-down, OTIF miss | Peer COO supply-chain disruption |

## E. Mobilizers — who you target inside the buying group

From *The Challenger Customer*. Not all stakeholders move deals forward equally.

**Good Mobilizers — invest here:**
- **Go-Getter** — bold, change-energised, first to champion. Risk: pushes too fast.
- **Teacher** — proactive educator; brings rigour. Earns peer trust.
- **Skeptic** — hybrid; demands evidence; once committed, fully committed.

**Poor Mobilizers — manage but do not rely on:**
- **Guide** — risk-averse; "let's do it the right way"; never decides.
- **Friend** — likes you; will not fight for you.
- **Climber** — backs the winner once obvious; useless during evaluation.
- **Blocker** — sees you as threat (often the legacy-system admin whose role is at risk).

**Strategy:** Find Mobilizers, give them ammunition (data, peer stories, business cases), let *them* drive cross-functional consensus. CEB research: sellers who target Mobilizers are 31% more likely to be top performers.

## F. Constructive tension (how to push back without breaking trust)

Challenging must feel like *collaboration in pursuit of truth*, not *vendor versus customer*.

| Customer says | Weak vendor reply | Challenger reply |
|---|---|---|
| "We want heavy customisation" | "Of course, we can do that." | "Custom GL structures can work, but they raise go-live risk by ~25% and make every upgrade painful. Three Thai customers chose standard GL last year and saved THB 8–15M each in rework. What specific business requirement is making customisation feel essential? — let's see if there's a standard path." |
| "We need a 90-day parallel run" | "OK." | "90-day parallel costs about THB 4M and pushes wrap-up by 3 months. Customers who did 5 days with strong cutover comms cut cost ~80% and went live cleanly on day 6. Can we explore the 5-day path before committing to 90?" |
| "Workday already has a feature for this" | "Let me check with product…" | "It does — and our customers who evaluated both told us they chose us because [specific reason tied to *the new way*]. Want me to connect you with their controller for a 30-minute call?" |

**The DNA of constructive tension:** always grounded in data or peer evidence; respects buyer autonomy ("that's your call, here's the trade-off"); positions as "pursuing the right outcome together"; never personal.

## G. Thai / Asia cultural calibration (mandatory)

Challenger as written can sound aggressive in Thai culture. **Calibrate using these rules:**

1. **Lead with humility, not assertion.** Say "Our peers have learned…" not "You are wrong to think…"
2. **Challenge through questions, not statements.** Say "May I respectfully ask why you are leaning toward lift-and-shift?" not "Lift-and-shift is the wrong approach."
3. **Never publicly contradict a senior buyer.** If you must push back, do it 1:1 in a follow-up message, not in front of their team.
4. **Anchor in third-party evidence.** Thai buyers respect Gartner, Deloitte, peer references — not the seller's opinion alone.
5. **Build the relationship credibility *first* (kreng-jai), then earn the right to challenge.** A Challenger without relationship capital is labelled "arrogant outsider" within one meeting.

Cross-reference `08-thailand-context.md` before delivering any Challenger pitch in Thailand.

## H. Output-format guidance

When the user asks for a Challenger pitch, deliver:

1. **The full 6-step pitch** filled in with the buyer's specifics — Warmer / Reframe / Rational Drowning / Emotional Impact / New Way / Your Solution
2. **Tailored versions** for the 2–3 most important stakeholders (table form per section D)
3. **A Mobilizer map** — who in the buying group is a Go-Getter / Teacher / Skeptic vs Friend / Guide / Blocker, and the strategy for each
4. **2–3 constructive-tension scripts** — anticipated objections and how to push back
5. **A Thai cultural calibration note** if Thai buyer (lead with humility, anchor in evidence, never publicly challenge)

Then offer the next component: "Want me to roll this into a 1-page CFO briefing using SCQA, or build the quantified business case behind the Rational Drowning section?"

## I. Common failure modes

- **Reciting features as the "Reframe".** Reframe must challenge the buyer's *thinking*, not list product capabilities.
- **Skipping Emotional Impact.** Buyers remember stories, not statistics. Always include a peer story.
- **Saying "your solution" too early.** If you mention your product before step 6, you have lost the Challenger discipline.
- **Aggressive tone in Thai context.** A Challenger in Bangkok must sound like a respectful peer-advisor, not a Wall Street pitch. Calibrate or lose the deal.
