# Reference 08 — Thailand / Government / SOE Context Layer

**Use when:** Any deal in Thailand. Any deal involving Thai government ministries, departments, agencies, SOEs, public universities, or BOI-promoted entities. Any deal involving family-controlled Thai conglomerates. Any time the user mentions e-GP, GFMIS, สตง., NACC, PACC, Procurement Act 2560, BOI, or Thai listed groups.

This reference modifies *every other reference in this skill.* Western methodology (Challenger especially) sounds aggressive in Thailand and breaks deals. This reference provides the cultural, regulatory, and procurement calibration that turns a generic enterprise sale into a Thailand-winning sale.

---

## A. The five Thai cultural concepts every seller must internalise

### 1. Kreng-jai (เกรงใจ)
A blend of consideration, deference, and reluctance to impose. A Thai buyer who is uncomfortable with your proposal will rarely say "no" directly — they will go quiet, postpone, or send a junior to give vague feedback.

**Implication for selling:**
- Silence is data. A 2-week silence after a meeting often means "no" or a problem they cannot raise directly.
- Always provide a face-saving exit ("If the timing is not right, we can revisit in Q3 — I just want to make sure we are aligned").
- Never put a senior buyer on the spot in a group meeting.

### 2. Naa (หน้า) — face
Public dignity is paramount. Causing someone to lose face — especially a senior — destroys the relationship.

**Implication for selling:**
- Never publicly contradict a senior buyer, even when they are factually wrong
- If you must push back, do it 1:1 in a follow-up message, framed as "I want to make sure I understood correctly"
- Praise publicly, correct privately

### 3. Bun-khun (บุญคุณ) — debt of gratitude
A favour given creates a long-term obligation. Champions in Thailand are often Champions because of accumulated bun-khun, not just rational ROI.

**Implication for selling:**
- Invest in small, generous gestures over time (a peer-CEO introduction, a relevant industry article, a thoughtful introduction)
- Patience compounds; quarterly relationship investment matters more than quarterly campaign blasts

### 4. Sanuk (สนุก) — making things enjoyable
Thai professional culture values pleasant working relationships. A "transactional" foreigner who treats every meeting as a sales call burns out fast.

**Implication for selling:**
- The dinner / golf / Songkran investment is real work, not extracurricular
- Humour, warmth, and genuine interest in the person open doors that data and slides never will

### 5. Hierarchy (ผู้ใหญ่ — phu-yai)
Age, seniority, and title carry weight. A junior with technical authority still defers to a senior generalist in public.

**Implication for selling:**
- Match seniority on each side — your CEO meets their CEO, your CFO meets their CFO
- Sending a junior to meet a senior buyer is an insult unless the junior is being formally introduced

## B. The Thai family-business and conglomerate map

Most large Thai enterprises are family-controlled even when listed. The visible org chart is not the real decision map.

| Group type | Decision pattern |
|---|---|
| **Family principal-led (e.g., CP, ThaiBev, Central, BTS, KBank legacy)** | Family principal sets strategic direction; professional CEO executes; large IT decisions almost always go to the principal |
| **Multi-generational with professional CEO (e.g., SCG, PTT, AOT)** | Professional management has high autonomy within strategic envelope set by board / Crown / family |
| **MNC subsidiary** | Local CFO / CIO has signing authority within group framework agreement; major decisions go to regional HQ (Singapore / Tokyo / KL) |
| **State Enterprise (SOE)** | Decisions formally rest with board, but ministerial / political alignment is the unwritten rule |

**The "shadow buying centre":** for family-controlled groups, map both the visible buying centre AND the shadow buying centre (family principal, family-trusted advisor, family lawyer/accountant). The professional manager will not say "I need to check with the Chairman" — but every gate will have a 2-week pause where exactly that happens.

## C. The Thai government / SOE procurement landscape — the regulatory bedrock

Selling to Thai government / SOE / public universities is *not* a standard B2B sale. It is governed by:

### Procurement Act B.E. 2560 (2017) — พรบ.การจัดซื้อจัดจ้างฯ พ.ศ. 2560
The master statute. Mandatory for all government, SOE, and grant-receiving entities. Defines:
- Allowed methods: e-bidding, e-market, selection, specific method
- Thresholds for each method
- TOR drafting requirements and accountability
- Bid committee composition and rotation
- Appeal procedures
- Disbarment ("ทิ้งงาน") consequences

### Ministerial Regulation (กฎกระทรวง) and supplementary regulations
Operational rules for thresholds, special cases, and method selection.

### e-GP (Electronic Government Procurement)
The mandatory online procurement platform. All bids above threshold must be processed through e-GP. Vendor must be registered, qualified, and in good standing.

### GFMIS (Government Fiscal Management Information System) and the New GFMIS Thai
The integrated financial / procurement / budget system. PO, AP, payment, and budget control all flow through GFMIS. Knowing how a customer's budget code, fund source, and PO type behave in GFMIS is essential pre-sales work.

### สตง. (State Audit Office of Thailand) and PACC / NACC oversight
Every government procurement is subject to ex-post audit. Findings can lead to project cancellation, vendor disbarment, and personal criminal liability for the project owner. The seller's TOR must be **defensible to สตง. on its face.**

### Cybersecurity Act, PDPA, and Cloud Security Standard 2567
Sector-specific compliance overlays. Cloud deployments to government / CII (Critical Information Infrastructure) require additional approvals and standards conformance. Cross-reference `legal-it-thailand-cloud` skill.

## D. The pre-TOR vs post-TOR rule (the most important sentence in this reference)

> **The seller's influence ends when TOR is published.** All consultative selling (discovery, education, vision-shaping) must happen pre-TOR. Once the bid is open, the seller may only conform to spec; relationships do not unlock the bid committee.

Translated:

| Phase | Seller activity | What works |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-TOR (months -12 to -2)** | Discovery, education, demo, reference visits, vision-shaping, suggested capability requirements that *fit your differentiators* | Consultative selling, Challenger pitch (calibrated), business case, executive workshops |
| **TOR drafting (months -2 to 0)** | Project Owner drafts TOR; you may be invited to comment; multiple vendors may also comment | Quietly ensure your differentiators map to TOR scoring criteria, BUT TOR must remain vendor-neutral on its face |
| **TOR published** | All vendors see same TOR; price + scoring submission window opens | Conform exactly. No "creative interpretation". Defensibility on every page. |
| **Bid evaluation (committee anonymous, rotated)** | Committee scores; cannot be lobbied | Wait. Lobbying = disqualification + criminal exposure. |
| **Award decision** | Lowest qualified bidder usually wins (e-bidding) or highest-scored (selection method) | Either you set up the win pre-TOR or you did not |
| **Appeal window** | Losing vendors may appeal | Your job: be unappealable — every TOR requirement clearly met, every score clearly defensible |
| **Contract signature** | PO issued via GFMIS / e-GP | Operational handover begins (see `07-handover-closure.md`) |

A seller who only shows up after TOR is published has already lost. A seller who shapes the project's vision pre-TOR — by being the trusted advisor for 12+ months — wins before the bid opens.

## E. The 8 anti-corruption guardrails (Thailand-specific)

NACC and PACC investigations have intensified since 2017. The seller's behaviour is part of the customer's audit risk. Discipline:

1. **No undisclosed gifts above public-officer threshold (THB 3,000 per occasion / THB 30,000 per year aggregated)** to government officers
2. **No facilitation payments — ever** (Foreign Corrupt Practices Act / UK Bribery Act apply to MNCs; NACC applies to Thai entities)
3. **No "consulting fees" to family of officials** (NACC red flag #1)
4. **No "agent commissions" without registered local entity, transparent contract, and audit trail**
5. **All entertainment within company policy and properly logged** (a single dinner above threshold is reportable)
6. **All references and case studies cleared with customer compliance** (mentioning a government client without permission triggers complaints)
7. **TOR comments must be in writing through Project Owner channel** — no informal back-channel
8. **In doubt: ask compliance.** A delayed deal is recoverable; an NACC finding is not.

## F. Calibrating the methodology to Thai context

| Methodology | Default Western form | Thai calibration |
|---|---|---|
| **SPIN** | Implication questions can be sharp ("What is the cost of inaction?") | Soften: "If we look at this together, what do you see as the main risk of waiting?" Use peer-anchored phrasing. |
| **Pain Sheet** | Direct quantification of pain in person's KPI | Quantify in business terms first ("THB to the company"), then offer to discuss personal-KPI implication 1:1 with the relevant individual |
| **9-Box** | Confirm column closes with explicit "do you agree?" | Close with "Have I understood correctly?" — invites a face-saving correction without forcing a commitment |
| **MEDDPICC** | Coach the seller to drive 14-day Champion actions | Coach the seller to *enable* the Champion's action through bun-khun and patience; aggressive pressure on a Thai Champion backfires |
| **Challenger** | Reframe assertively, push back hard | Reframe through questions and peer evidence ("Three peers have learned…"); never assert "you are wrong"; never publicly challenge a senior |
| **Stakeholder map** | Power × Interest with Western roles | Add: family principal, family advisor, ministerial alignment for SOE, BOI condition for promoted entities |
| **Business case (SCQA)** | Crisp, tight, declarative | Same structure, but soften the "Complication" and "Question" — frame as opportunity rather than threat where possible |
| **Handover** | Crisp 10 artefacts; AD steps back | Same 10 artefacts, but AD remains visible at every steering committee for relationship continuity (face-of-the-vendor model) |

## G. The Thai listed-group context (SET / mai)

For SET-listed Thai groups, additional layers:

- **SEC and SET disclosure requirements** — large IT investments may require board approval, disclosed materially
- **Audit committee independence** — IT control gaps surface here; remediation timelines become compelling events
- **Family vs minority-shareholder dynamics** — large IT spend with related parties draws scrutiny
- **CG (corporate governance) ranking** — Thai CG ranking influences institutional investment; weak IT controls hurt ranking

## H. The BOI and special-zone context

For BOI-promoted entities and EEC / SEZ companies:

- **BOI promotion conditions** often include digital / e-invoicing / data localisation requirements — these become compelling events
- **EEC (Eastern Economic Corridor)** companies may have additional procurement preference rules
- **CIT (Corporate Income Tax) holiday** ends typically in 5–8 years; major IT investment decisions cluster around the holiday-end transition

## I. Output-format guidance

When the user asks for any deliverable involving a Thai customer, append:

1. **A 1-paragraph cultural context** — kreng-jai, face, hierarchy implications for this specific deal
2. **A shadow buying-centre map** if family-controlled — visible vs shadow stakeholders
3. **A pre-TOR / TOR / post-TOR phase map** if government / SOE — what activity is allowed in each phase, with dates
4. **A compliance guardrail check** — gifts, references, agent payments, TOR back-channel
5. **A regulatory overlay note** — Procurement Act 2560, Cybersecurity Act, PDPA, Cloud Security Standard 2567 (whichever apply)

## J. Common failure modes

- **Treating silence as agreement.** Silence in Thailand often means polite disagreement. Probe gently.
- **Public Challenger pitch in front of Thai senior.** "You are wrong about lift-and-shift" said in front of a CFO and the board destroys the deal in one sentence.
- **Showing up after TOR published.** If you are first introduced post-TOR, you are filing paperwork, not selling.
- **Ignoring the family principal.** Two-week pauses at every gate; deal slips two quarters; closes after the family meeting.
- **Mistaking Project Owner's friendliness for committee influence.** Project Owner can shape pre-TOR; cannot influence the bid committee at all.
- **Compliance shortcut "to grease the wheels".** One NACC investigation ends the firm's government franchise for a decade.
- **Western SCQA delivered to Thai EB without softening.** Same structure, but the "Complication" and "Question" need cultural framing — often as "opportunity" rather than "threat".
- **Sending an MNC regional VP to a Thai EB without local pairing.** The EB will defer politely, then make the real decision with the local representative they trust afterwards. Always pair foreign senior with the local Account Director.
