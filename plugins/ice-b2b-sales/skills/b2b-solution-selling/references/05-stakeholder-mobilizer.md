# Reference 05 — Stakeholder Map + Mobilizer Targeting

**Use when:** Any deal with more than one buyer (i.e. all enterprise deals). Diagnosing a single-threaded deal. Planning who to multi-thread next. Preparing a Champion-enablement pack. Coaching a seller who has been "blocked in IT" or "stuck in procurement."

CEB / Gartner research on 5,000+ B2B buyers shows the average enterprise software decision now involves **6.8 stakeholders** across 4–5 functions. Sellers who win consistently target Mobilizers, not Coaches; and they multi-thread early, not late.

---

## A. The Power × Interest grid (start here)

For every named stakeholder, plot two axes:

- **Power**: ability to say *yes* (1) or *no* (1) — formal authority + informal influence
- **Interest**: how much this initiative affects their personal KPIs / role / political position

|  | **Low Power** | **High Power** |
|---|---|---|
| **High Interest** | Keep informed — give them air-time, do not let them feel ignored (they will undermine quietly) | **Manage closely** — these are your Mobilizers, Champions, and Blockers |
| **Low Interest** | Monitor — minimal investment, but watch for shifts | Keep satisfied — usually the EB or a senior approver who must not be surprised |

The deal is won or lost in the **High Power × High Interest** quadrant. Map every name there with: role, KPI, posture (Champion / Coach / Friend / Skeptic / Blocker), and last-touch date.

## B. The 7 Mobilizer types (Challenger Customer)

From Adamson, Dixon, Spenner & Toman, *The Challenger Customer* (2015), based on CEB research on 700 B2B buyers across the consensus journey.

### Good Mobilizers — invest here

| Type | Recognise them by | How to deploy them |
|---|---|---|
| **Go-Getter** | Bold, ambitious, change-energised. First to push for action. Frustrated by status quo. | Give them peer success stories and a clear next step. Risk: they push too fast and trigger antibodies — pace them. |
| **Teacher** | Proactive educator. Brings rigour, evidence, frameworks. Earns peer trust through credibility. | Equip with white papers, ROI models, peer references. They will run your sales process internally for you. |
| **Skeptic** | Demands evidence; pushes back hard. Once committed, fully committed and influential. | Show your homework — sensitivity analysis, risk matrix, reference call. Win the Skeptic and the consensus follows. |

### Talkers (but do NOT drive consensus)

| Type | Recognise them by | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Friend** | Likes you, returns calls, gives "intel". Will not fight for you, will not stick neck out. | Useful for information; never confuse with Champion. Manage politely, allocate minimal time. |
| **Climber** | Backs the winner once obvious. Tracks who is "in" with the EB. Useless during evaluation. | Do not invest until consensus is forming, then they jump on for the ride. |
| **Guide** | Risk-averse. "Let's do it the right way." Loves process, hates decisions. Never says no, never says yes. | Manage with patience. Give them the procedural clarity they need — Decision Process map, Paper Process plan — but do not rely on them to drive. |

### Blockers — must be neutralised

| Type | Recognise them by | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Blocker** | Sees you as threat. Often the legacy-system admin / consultant whose role is at risk. Sometimes a senior who backed the incumbent and would lose face if displaced. | Surface the threat openly. Find win-win (training, role expansion, advisory). If unfixable, escalate to Champion + EB to neutralise. |

**CEB finding:** Sellers who target Mobilizers are **31% more likely** to be top performers than those who target friends and "gatekeepers."

## C. Champion vs Coach vs Friend (the test that matters)

| | Champion | Coach | Friend |
|---|---|---|---|
| Has power | Yes | No | No |
| Has influence | Yes | Some | No |
| **Has done something for you in last 14 days** | **Yes — uncomfortable for them** | Sometimes — gives intel | No |
| Useful for | Driving the deal | Information, navigation | Coffee. That is all. |

**Champion litmus test (run weekly):** "What did the Champion do for me this week?" Examples of real Champion actions:
- Got you a meeting with the EB
- Defended your budget in a finance review
- Sent your business case to the CFO unprompted
- Killed a competitor's demo from the agenda
- Pushed back on Procurement's TOR draft to remove a blocker clause

If the answer is "nothing" twice in a row, you have a Coach (or worse, a Friend) — not a Champion. Find a real Champion or de-risk the deal in CRM.

## D. The 6 personas in an enterprise software buying centre

Every deal has these roles. Sometimes one person plays two; sometimes two people play one. Map them explicitly.

| Persona | What they care about | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|
| **Economic Buyer (EB)** | ROI, strategic fit, risk to their tenure | If you have not met EB by week 6, deal is yellow. |
| **Technical Buyer** | Architecture fit, security, integration, total cost of ownership | Often becomes Blocker if not engaged early — incumbent vendor is their friend. |
| **User Buyer** | Day-to-day usability, training burden, change-management impact | Their resistance kills go-live, even if EB signs. |
| **Coach / Champion** | Gets credit if it works, exposed if it fails | Validate: are they powerful enough to take the risk? |
| **Procurement** | Defensible TOR, compliance, price, risk transfer | In Thai government / SOE: Procurement Act 2560 audit-defensibility is paramount. |
| **Legal / Risk / Compliance** | Liability cap, IP, data protection, indemnity | Engage in week 4, not week 24. |

For a 6-stakeholder deal, plan **at least 4 distinct relationship threads**. Anything less is single-threaded.

## E. Multi-threading — why and how

**Why:** A single-threaded deal collapses if your one contact is reassigned, fired, blocked, or simply goes on holiday. Gartner research: deals with **3+ active relationships** are 2.5× more likely to close than single-threaded deals.

**How (the 30-60-90 plan):**
- **Days 0–30**: Champion + Technical Buyer + User Buyer (the working level)
- **Days 30–60**: EB introduction (Champion-led), Procurement introduction
- **Days 60–90**: Legal, peer EB-to-EB call (your CFO ↔ their CFO), board / steering committee touch

Each touch should leave behind something the stakeholder owns (a one-pager, a peer reference, a question they will ask at the next internal meeting). Stakeholders who own something defend it.

## F. The Champion enablement pack

A Champion is only as effective as the ammunition they carry to internal meetings. Build them this pack:

1. **One-page executive summary** in their boss's language (SCQA — see `06-business-case-bigfour.md`)
2. **Quantified business case** — best / base / worst with sensitivity (CFO-grade)
3. **Peer reference list** — 3 comparable customers, 1 with a peer-CFO willing to take a 30-minute call
4. **Competitive battlecard** — answers to "Why not SAP / Workday / Microsoft / status quo"
5. **Risk register + mitigations** — addresses the Skeptic before the Skeptic asks
6. **Implementation roadmap** — phases, milestones, customer ownership clearly marked
7. **FAQ for the buying committee** — anticipated objections + your answers, in their language

The pack should be co-created with the Champion, not delivered to them. Their fingerprints on it = their commitment to it.

## G. Thai / Asia stakeholder calibration

Thai buying centres differ from Western models in three important ways.

### 1. Hierarchy and face

The most senior person in the room may not be the most influential — but they *cannot be embarrassed*. Run all decisions past your Champion before any group meeting. Never surface a disagreement in front of a senior person; raise it 1:1 afterwards.

### 2. Family-business and conglomerate dynamics

In Thai family conglomerates (CP, Central, ThaiBev, SCG, etc.), the real EB is often the family principal — even if a hired CEO appears on the org chart. You may need to map a "shadow buying centre" that includes:

- Family principal(s) — strategic direction, ultimate yes/no
- Non-family CEO / CFO — formal decision execution
- Family-trusted advisor (consultant, lawyer, accountant) — gatekeeper to the principal

The professional manager will not say "I need to check with the Chairman" — but they will pause for 2 weeks every time. Plan for it.

### 3. Government / SOE buying centres

| Role | Who | Engagement window |
|---|---|---|
| Project Owner (เจ้าของโครงการ) | Director-level user | Pre-TOR — heavy education |
| TOR Committee (คณะกรรมการ TOR) | Cross-functional, anonymous | Pre-TOR — through Project Owner only |
| Procurement Committee (คณะกรรมการพิจารณาผล) | Anonymous, rotated | Cannot influence — TOR conformance only |
| สตง. / Internal Audit | External | Build defensibility into proposal |
| EB equivalent | Permanent Secretary / Director-General | Pre-TOR strategic alignment |

**The seller's influence ends when TOR is published.** All consultative selling must happen pre-TOR. Once the bid is open, conform exactly to spec; relationships do not unlock the committee. See `08-thailand-context.md`.

## H. Output-format guidance

When the user asks for a stakeholder map, deliver:

1. **A table of named stakeholders** with: name, role, KPI, persona (EB / Tech / User / Champion / Procurement / Legal), Mobilizer type (Go-Getter / Teacher / Skeptic / Friend / Climber / Guide / Blocker), posture (Champion / Coach / Friend / Skeptic / Blocker), power 1–5, interest 1–5, last-touch date
2. **A 2×2 Power × Interest grid** with names plotted
3. **A multi-thread plan** — for the next 30 days, who will the seller meet (or have the Champion introduce), what question, what desired commitment
4. **A Champion proof-point check** — what did the Champion do in the last 14 days? If nothing, what action will the seller request this week?
5. **A Blocker neutralisation plan** if a Blocker is identified — surface, win-win offer, escalation path
6. **A Thai cultural note** if Thai buyer — face management, hierarchy respect, family-principal mapping

Then offer the next component: "Want me to roll this into a Champion enablement pack, or score the deal on MEDDPICC now that the buying centre is mapped?"

## I. Common failure modes

- **Single-threaded deal disguised as multi-thread.** Five names in CRM, but only one has been met in the last 30 days. The litmus test: when did each stakeholder last spend ≥30 minutes with you?
- **Mistaking Procurement for the Blocker.** Often Procurement is doing their job (defensibility, risk transfer); they are a Guide, not a Blocker. Engage them as a partner with a clean Paper Process plan.
- **Treating the Friend as the Champion.** Friend gives intel, never fights. Run the 14-day action test.
- **Ignoring the User Buyer.** Deal signs; go-live fails because users were never consulted; reference is poisoned forever.
- **Engaging Legal in week 22 instead of week 4.** Paper Process discovery in week 22 = deal slips a quarter, every time.
- **Not mapping the family principal in Thai family-business deals.** Deal stalls inexplicably for 2–3 weeks at every gate. Map the shadow buying centre from day 1.
