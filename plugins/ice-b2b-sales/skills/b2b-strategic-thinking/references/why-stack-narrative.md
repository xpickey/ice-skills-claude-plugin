# The Five-Why Strategic Narrative ("Why-Stack")

Enterprise software deals are won and lost on whether the buyer can answer five questions in front of their executive sponsor — *in the buyer's own words.* The Why-Stack is the discipline of ensuring those five answers exist, hang together, and are armed with proof.

This is the spine of every executive-grade narrative this skill produces.

---

## The five questions

| # | Question | Buyer is asking | Seller must give |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Why Change?** | "Is the status quo really broken?" | A pain quantified — what it costs to do nothing, in $/risk/lost-opportunity terms |
| 2 | **Why Now?** | "What forces a decision this cycle vs. next year?" | A compelling event — regulatory deadline, contract expiry, capacity wall, market window |
| 3 | **Why Invest?** | "Is this the best use of scarce capital and management bandwidth?" | A business case — TCO, ROI, payback, risk-adjusted, defensible to CFO/audit |
| 4 | **Why Us?** | "Why this vendor / partner over alternatives, including 'do nothing'?" | Differentiated value tied to *their* job — capability, proof, ecosystem, references in vertical |
| 5 | **Why Stay?** | "How do we make sure the value sticks and grows?" | Adoption plan, success metrics, governance, expansion roadmap, CSM partnership |

The order matters. Why-Us before Why-Change is a pitch. Why-Change before Why-Us is a strategy.

---

## Why this works

Most sellers are excellent at Why-Us (their product) and weak at Why-Change and Why-Now (the customer's situation). Most buyers are the opposite — they are drowning in Why-Change and Why-Now and can't articulate Why-Us in their own words.

The Why-Stack closes that gap by **arming the champion** with a story they can tell *without the seller in the room.* This is the single highest-leverage thing a strategic thinker does.

---

## Building each Why

### Why Change?
- Anchor in **a quantified pain**, not a feature gap. ("$4.2M in inventory write-offs last FY" beats "no demand sensing.")
- Cite **the customer's own words** wherever possible — discovery transcripts, CFO commentary, board minutes.
- Surface **the cost of inaction** as a number with a confidence range.
- For Thai government / SOE buyers: anchor in **policy alignment** (Thailand 4.0, digital government, e-GP) alongside operational pain — defensibility matters more than novelty.

### Why Now?
- Identify the **compelling event** — without one, this is a stall pretending to be a deal.
- Compelling events are usually one of: contract expiry, regulatory/audit deadline, capacity wall, M&A integration timeline, leadership change with a mandate, end of fiscal year discretionary budget.
- For Thailand specifically: BOI promotion sunsets, e-Tax invoicing requirements, PDPA enforcement actions, fiscal-year budget cycles for government, Lunar New Year cash-flow patterns for ethnic Chinese family businesses.
- If no compelling event exists, **say so**. Don't invent one. The strategic move may be to manufacture urgency through a paid pilot or burning platform reframing.

### Why Invest?
- Build the case using the **Value Engineering Logic Tree** (see `references/frameworks-library.md`): Revenue ↑ + Cost ↓ + Cash ↑ + Risk ↓ → driver → metric → lever → capability → proof.
- Use **customer-specific baselines** wherever possible. Industry benchmarks are tells of laziness.
- Show **best-case / base-case / worst-case** with assumptions, so the CFO can stress-test it.
- Mark **CapEx vs. OpEx**, **cash timing**, and **payback period** explicitly. For Thai government buyers, also flag budget category (งบลงทุน vs. งบดำเนินงาน) and fiscal-year fit.
- Include **risk of inaction** as a quantified line, not a footnote.

### Why Us?
- Differentiate on **outcomes the buyer cares about**, not on capability check-marks.
- Pair every claim with a **proof unit**: case study with metric, named reference, certification, partner ecosystem, vertical IP.
- Be explicit about **where you don't fit** — strategic thinkers volunteer their gaps, which makes the rest of the narrative more credible.
- For Thai/APAC: name the **local delivery team**, **local language support**, **local government experience**, **local partner ecosystem** — these are decisive in mid-market and government buys.

### Why Stay?
- Treat this as **part of the first sale**, not an afterthought. Adoption defines whether expansion is possible.
- Articulate the **success metric** the customer will measure 6/12/24 months out, who measures it, and the cadence of review.
- Name the **CSM playbook**, **business-review rhythm**, and **expansion sequence** (capability → site → entity → region).
- For Thailand: anchor in **multi-year relationship horizon** with named senior sponsor on both sides. Transactional vendors are dismissed in mid-market and SOE buys.

## Proof-unit discipline

Every Why must be **armed**. A proof unit is a discrete, citable piece of evidence:
- Customer quote (with attribution)
- Quantified case study (with named metric)
- Reference customer (with sector, size, region)
- Certification or compliance proof (ISO 27001, SOC 2, PDPA-aligned)
- Analyst recognition (Gartner, Forrester) — be honest about category fit
- Internal data (e.g., "92% of customers achieve X in 90 days")

A Why without proof units is a slogan. A slide deck full of vendor-asserted claims with no proof units is a lose.

## Output formats

### Long-form (Executive Brief / Board Paper)
A full one-pager per Why, with proof units and assumptions. Pair with a 1-page executive summary that gives the headline of each Why and the integrated recommendation.

### Champion-arming format
A 1-page "what to tell your CEO" for the Champion to use internally. Five sections (one per Why), each 2-3 sentences, written in the Champion's voice and language.

### Slide format (5-slide deck)
One slide per Why. Headline = the Why claim. Body = 3 supporting points. Footer = proof units cited. This is the executive deck, not the demo deck.

### Why-Stack worksheet
See `assets/why-stack-worksheet.md` for the fillable template the model should produce when the user wants to draft a new Why-Stack from scratch.

## Diagnosing a weak Why-Stack

| Symptom | Probable cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "We need to show more product" | Why-Us bloated, other Whys empty | Re-balance — at least one slide per Why |
| Champion can't repeat the story back | Language is the seller's, not the customer's | Use customer transcripts to rewrite |
| CFO pushes back on the business case | Why-Invest uses generic benchmarks | Rebuild with customer-specific baselines |
| Deal stalls in legal / procurement | Why-Now wasn't real | Find the compelling event or reframe to a paid pilot |
| Buyer says "this isn't a priority right now" | Why-Change wasn't quantified enough | Surface a sharper cost of inaction |
| Customer churns post-go-live | Why-Stay was an afterthought | Make adoption part of the original case |

## Calibration to Thai/APAC + Government

Two adjustments matter most:

**For government / SOE / SMART PAO contexts:**
- Why-Change must align to a **policy mandate** (Thailand 4.0, BCG Economy, Cloud-First, OAG audit findings).
- Why-Invest must fit **annual budget cycles** and pass **e-GP/GFMIS justification** standards.
- Why-Us must include **local references**, **MOF-registered status**, **e-bidding track record**, **knowledge transfer commitment**.
- Why-Stay must include **knowledge transfer to officials** and **independence from a single vendor** as explicit commitments.

**For Thai mid-enterprise SME family businesses:**
- Why-Change must respect the **bunkhun** the founder feels toward incumbent vendors — never disparage; reframe the moment.
- Why-Invest must speak in **cash terms** (monthly cash impact) more than NPV/IRR.
- Why-Us must include **local Thai-speaking team**, **owner-relationship continuity**, **family-business reference**.
- Why-Stay must include **multi-generational legacy** language ("the platform your kids will inherit").
