# Anti-Patterns

The 12 failure modes that turn strategic-thinking work into noise. Catch these in your own drafts before delivery; flag them when reviewing the user's work.

Each entry has the **symptom**, the **why-it-fails**, and the **fix**.

---

## 1. Framework drape

**Symptom:** A 2x2 or canvas pasted on a problem that didn't need it. The framework appears before any thinking has been done.

**Why it fails:** A framework without insight is decoration. Buyers and executives see through it; the seller looks junior.

**Fix:** Ask "what would this analysis say in plain prose?" If you can answer in 2 sentences, deliver that. If the framework genuinely sharpens the thinking, keep it; otherwise drop it.

---

## 2. List-itis

**Symptom:** Bullet pile with no headline, no recommendation, no priority order.

**Why it fails:** Forces the reader to do the synthesis you were paid to do. Senior readers stop reading.

**Fix:** Lead with a one-sentence answer. Then 3-5 reasons in MECE form. Lists belong inside reasons, not as the whole answer.

---

## 3. Why-Us before Why-Change

**Symptom:** Recommendation, deck, or email opens with "We are [X capability]" before establishing why the buyer should care.

**Why it fails:** Buyers don't care about the seller until the seller has earned the right to be considered by demonstrating they understand the buyer's situation.

**Fix:** Re-sequence. Open with Why-Change anchored in the buyer's words. Earn into Why-Us.

---

## 4. Missing Why-Now

**Symptom:** Strong Why-Change, strong Why-Us, no compelling event. The deal slides quarter to quarter.

**Why it fails:** Without urgency, even good buyers stay where they are. "Important but not urgent" is a no.

**Fix:** Find the compelling event — regulatory, contract, capacity, leadership — or honestly admit there isn't one and recommend a paid pilot or burning-platform reframing.

---

## 5. Vendor-asserted business case

**Symptom:** ROI numbers built from vendor benchmarks, "industry averages," and round-number multipliers. No customer-specific baselines.

**Why it fails:** The CFO sees through it in 30 seconds. Credibility leak that contaminates the whole proposal.

**Fix:** Build the case from the customer's own data wherever possible. Mark assumptions explicitly. Show best-case / base-case / worst-case.

---

## 6. Single-threaded deal masquerading as a relationship

**Symptom:** All the seller's information comes through one person; that person is a Champion; nobody on the buyer side has tested the recommendation independently.

**Why it fails:** When the Champion leaves, gets reorganized, or goes quiet, the deal evaporates. Common in Thai family businesses where one ally is mistaken for the buying committee.

**Fix:** Map the power network (see `references/frameworks-library.md` Power Map). Establish at least three relationships at different levels and functions. Run the "if [Champion] left tomorrow, what happens?" test.

---

## 7. Generic "best practices" pitch

**Symptom:** Slide-ware about how "leading companies in your industry" do something. No customer-specific insight. No local context.

**Why it fails:** Sounds like a stock pitch. Buyer's takeaway: "they don't actually know us." Especially deadly in Thai mid-market and government.

**Fix:** Replace generic claims with named customer-specific observations. If you can't, do more discovery before the next meeting.

---

## 8. Over-confident recommendation

**Symptom:** Strong language ("we strongly recommend…") with weak evidence underneath. No "what would change my mind."

**Why it fails:** Senior readers calibrate confidence. Over-claiming relative to evidence makes them distrust the rest.

**Fix:** State confidence honestly (high/medium/low). Mark assumptions. Name the disconfirming evidence and how you'd know.

---

## 9. Missing reversibility plan

**Symptom:** Recommendation reads like a final answer with no monitoring, no tripwire, no checkpoint.

**Why it fails:** Strategy lives in time. Decisions need to be revisited. Without a reversibility plan, the recommendation is brittle.

**Fix:** Add: leading metrics that prove the choice right, tripwire metrics that suggest it's wrong, owner of monitoring, cadence, and the cost of reversing by quarter.

---

## 10. Disparaging the incumbent

**Symptom:** Comparison to the existing vendor leans on attack ("their architecture is outdated", "they don't really understand X").

**Why it fails:** In Thai contexts especially, the incumbent often carries bunkhun. Attacking them attacks the buyer's prior judgment. Even in Western contexts, it sounds insecure.

**Fix:** Acknowledge the incumbent's contribution. Position your value as **additive** or as a fit for a **new chapter**. Let the customer choose to displace.

---

## 11. Defaulting to a single output format

**Symptom:** Every output looks the same regardless of audience — same length, same structure, same depth.

**Why it fails:** A board paper is not a deal-review note is not a champion-arming brief. One-size-fits-all wastes the reader's time.

**Fix:** Default to the audience's altitude. CFO needs cash and TCO; CIO needs architecture and risk; Champion needs a story they can retell. See SKILL.md "Adaptive output format" for the mapping.

---

## 12. Cultural tone-deafness

**Symptom:** Recommendation is technically right but ignores face, hierarchy, bunkhun, or Thai/APAC decision norms. Often shows up as: forced urgency, Western directness, dismissal of relationship cost, single-meeting close expectations, or treating a Thai owner-led deal like a US committee buy.

**Why it fails:** Loses deals that look winnable on the spreadsheet. Damages the seller's standing for the next deal.

**Fix:** Run every Thai/APAC recommendation through the calibration filter (`references/thai-apac-calibration.md`). Add a "Relationship & Face" section to account/deal recommendations. Use longer time horizons. Acknowledge incumbents. Provide face-saving narratives for "pass" or "delay" calls.

---

## Bonus anti-patterns to watch for

- **The "interesting" trap:** observations that don't drive a decision. Apply the so-what test.
- **Sandwich-of-views:** "on the one hand… on the other…" with no recommendation. Take the position.
- **Assumption laundering:** an inference written as a fact. Mark every inference as such.
- **Buried trade-off:** recommendation reads like a free lunch. Name what's given up.
- **Confidence theater:** corporate-bold language replacing actual rigor. Calibrate honestly.
- **Backfit reasoning:** picking evidence that supports the gut answer. Inventory disconfirming evidence too.

---

## Self-check before delivery

Before sending any output produced by this skill, scan for these 12 patterns. If any apply, revise. The cost of revision is minutes; the cost of shipping a flawed strategic recommendation is a deal, an account, or a relationship.
