# Segment Strategy Bridge

This skill operates across three buyer segments that demand different strategic moves: **Government / Public Sector**, **Large / Tier-1 Enterprise**, and **Mid-Enterprise / SME**. The frameworks are the same; the application differs.

This reference is the bridge: it tells the model **what changes per segment** so the recommendation lands.

---

## Quick orientation

| Dimension | Government / SOE | Large Enterprise | Mid-Enterprise / SME |
|---|---|---|---|
| Decision unit | Committee + policy | Steering committee + C-suite | Owner + CFO |
| Cycle length | 12-24 months | 6-18 months | 3-9 months |
| Procurement | e-GP / e-bidding / TOR | Structured RFP | Direct negotiation |
| Risk posture | Audit-defensive | Strategic-ambitious | Cash-cautious |
| Reference need | MOF-registered, local SOE | SET-100, regional MNC | Peer family business |
| Compelling event | Fiscal year, policy mandate | M&A, group strategy, ESG | Generational change, BOI, IPO |
| Vendor preference | Local + proven | Global brand + local team | Local + relationship |
| Pricing sensitivity | Lowest-compliant-bid bias | TCO over price | Cash flow over TCO |
| Lock-in tolerance | Low (forced multi-vendor) | Moderate (architecture-aware) | High (single-throat trust) |
| Decision style | Defensible | Differentiated | Personal |

## Government / Public Sector

### Strategic frame
The buy is defensible by design. Every decision must survive:
- An OAG (Office of the Auditor General) review
- An SAO (State Audit Office) audit
- Public scrutiny, sometimes years later
- Political-cycle reversal

### Recommended strategy moves
1. **TOR shaping is the strategy.** Most government deals are decided in TOR. If you didn't shape the TOR, you're competing for second place.
2. **Reference architecture matters more than product features.** Map to Thailand 4.0, Digital Government Architecture, BCG Economy.
3. **Multi-vendor / open standards are a feature, not a weakness.** Lock-in is a procurement red flag.
4. **Knowledge transfer is a contract obligation, not a value-add.** Plan for it from day 1.
5. **Local references > global references.** Even one strong Thai SOE reference outweighs three Fortune 500s.
6. **Compliance posture is non-negotiable.** PDPA, Cloud Security Standard 2567, ISO 27001 — and you must show it, not claim it.

### Why-Stack tilts
- **Why-Change:** policy mandate + operational pain (in that order)
- **Why-Now:** fiscal year + ministerial directive + audit finding
- **Why-Invest:** budget category fit (งบลงทุน vs. งบดำเนินงาน), e-GP defensibility, multi-year continuity
- **Why-Us:** local references, MOF-registered, knowledge transfer commitment, partner ecosystem
- **Why-Stay:** government-grade SLAs, multi-year support, succession plan for officials

### Hand-offs
- `advisor-govt-gfmis` — for GFMIS, FM, e-LAAS, accrual accounting
- `govt-egp-gfmis` — for e-GP, e-bidding, procurement law
- `smart-pao-platform` — for อบจ. / PAO platform design
- `legal-it-thailand-cloud` — for PDPA, Cloud Security Standard 2567

### Anti-patterns to avoid
- Pitching innovation / "be first" to risk-averse government buyers
- Disparaging incumbents or other vendors in writing
- Promising what isn't TOR-aligned
- Treating government buys as transactional one-shots — they are relationships

## Large / Tier-1 Enterprise (PTT, SCG, CP, banks, telcos, listed conglomerates)

### Strategic frame
The buy is a **strategic transformation** wrapped in a software decision. The CFO + CIO co-sign; group procurement adds a layer; the executive sponsor is the deal.

### Recommended strategy moves
1. **Lead with the executive narrative.** Product capability is table stakes; strategic alignment with the group's 5-year plan wins.
2. **Match seniority to seniority.** A C-level customer needs C-level vendor sponsorship from day 1.
3. **Treat group procurement as a stakeholder, not an obstacle.** Co-design the commercial structure.
4. **Reference depth matters more than reference count.** A two-day site visit to a peer beats ten case studies.
5. **Architecture decisions are bets on the next 10 years.** Help the buyer see the optionality cost of each choice.
6. **Local delivery muscle is decisive.** The deal is won on confidence in local team strength.

### Why-Stack tilts
- **Why-Change:** group strategy alignment + competitive position vs. regional peers
- **Why-Now:** group strategy refresh, M&A integration, ESG mandate, leadership transition
- **Why-Invest:** TCO over 5+ years + strategic optionality + risk mitigation
- **Why-Us:** vendor strategic intent in the region + executive co-sponsorship + peer references
- **Why-Stay:** joint roadmap, named executive sponsorship, joint innovation lab

### Hand-offs
- `b2b-enterprise-sale-strategy` (Large Enterprise sub-strategy)
- `oracle-cloud-applications-consulting` for Oracle Fusion strategy
- `oracle-ebs-consulting` for EBS upgrade/migration
- `fin-tech-consulting` for FSI-specific opportunities
- `legal-it-thailand-cloud` for compliance posture

### Anti-patterns to avoid
- Sending a regional account exec to a meeting that needs a C-level
- Underweighting group procurement until the eleventh hour
- Generic "industry benchmarks" — Tier-1 buyers want their own data
- Pitching tactical wins when the buyer is thinking strategy

## Mid-Enterprise / SME (often Thai family business, Ethnic Chinese)

### Strategic frame
The buy is **owner-led, cash-conscious, and multi-generational**. The CFO advises; the owner decides. The relationship is the contract.

### Recommended strategy moves
1. **Earn the owner's trust before pitching.** Multiple visits, multiple meals, no agenda — then the deal.
2. **Reframe finance in cash terms.** Monthly cash impact wins; NPV loses.
3. **Position the platform as a legacy asset.** "What your kids will inherit" lands better than "5-year TCO."
4. **Local Thai-speaking team is non-negotiable.** Even with English-fluent owners, the comfort matters.
5. **Peer reference dinners beat case studies.** Connect them to a similar family business that bought from you.
6. **Respect incumbents.** The Thai accountant who built the homegrown system is owed respect.

### Why-Stack tilts
- **Why-Change:** generational change, IPO ambition, BOI promotion, growth plateau
- **Why-Now:** succession event, BOI sunset, year-end audit pain, family meeting decision
- **Why-Invest:** cash flow by month + payback in years (not NPV) + family legacy
- **Why-Us:** local team, peer references, owner-relationship continuity, "we'll be here in 10 years"
- **Why-Stay:** dedicated CSM, owner-level QBR cadence, family-business community

### Hand-offs
- `b2b-enterprise-sale-strategy` (Normal Enterprise / Mid-Market sub-strategy)
- `oracle-netsuite-consulting` — most common ERP fit for this segment
- `oracle-cloud-applications-consulting` — for IPO-track / multi-entity needs
- `fin-tech-consulting` — for SME lending / cash management opportunities

### Anti-patterns to avoid
- Sending a US-style structured pitch to an owner-led meeting
- Speaking only English when the room is bilingual
- Disparaging the incumbent accountant or homegrown system
- Applying enterprise governance frameworks as if they were the customer's reality

## Cross-segment principles

Some moves apply regardless of segment:

1. **Map the real decision unit** before recommending — title and authority diverge.
2. **Find the compelling event** — without one, the deal is a stall.
3. **Arm the champion** with a story they can repeat without you in the room.
4. **Lead with one strategic insight**, not three product slides.
5. **Be willing to walk away** — false-positive pursuits are the largest hidden cost in enterprise sales.
6. **Tie price to value**, not to competition.
7. **Plan for adoption from the first conversation** — Why-Stay is part of the original sale.

## When the segment is unclear

If the user hasn't named the segment, ask once with a sharp clarifier (or infer with confidence calibration):

> "Quick check — is this a government / SOE buy, a Tier-1 enterprise transformation, or an owner-led mid-market / family business? The frame shifts noticeably across them."

Then proceed with the appropriate calibration. Don't try to answer for all three — that produces mush.
