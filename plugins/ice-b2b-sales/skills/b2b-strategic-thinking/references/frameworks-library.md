# Strategic Frameworks Library

A curated, opinionated library of strategic-thinking frameworks for B2B enterprise software sales and solutioning. Each entry tells the model **what the framework is**, **when to reach for it**, **what shape its output takes**, and **the trap to avoid**.

The goal is not to memorize all frameworks. The goal is to recognize the **shape of the question** and pick the right tool — then explain *why* that tool fits, so the user can challenge or adjust.

## 1. Market & Industry Analysis

### Porter's Five Forces
- **What it is:** Structural analysis of an industry — buyer power, supplier power, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes, rivalry intensity.
- **When to reach for it:** Entering a new vertical, deciding whether to build a vertical practice, sizing a portfolio bet, briefing an executive on why margins compress in a market.
- **Output shape:** A 1-page table with each force rated H/M/L plus a one-sentence "so what" for the seller's strategy.
- **Trap:** Don't apply it at the deal level — it's a market frame, not an account frame. For Thai SOEs and government, "buyer power" is concentrated at one or two regulators; that distorts the analysis and must be called out.

### PESTEL
- **What it is:** Scan of Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal forces shaping a market.
- **When to reach for it:** Before a market-entry recommendation, when regulatory shifts (PDPA, Cloud Security Standard 2567, e-GP rules) affect deal viability, when an executive needs context on Thailand 4.0 / digital government policy direction.
- **Output shape:** Six short paragraphs (one per letter), each ending in a buyer/seller implication.
- **Trap:** Avoid generic "economic headwinds" prose. Be specific to the customer's segment (BOI-promoted manufacturer vs. PAO vs. Tier-1 bank).

### Three Horizons (McKinsey)
- **What it is:** Frame for balancing core defense (H1), emerging adjacent bets (H2), and option-creating moonshots (H3).
- **When to reach for it:** Portfolio strategy conversations, multi-year roadmap framing for executives, helping a CIO sequence ERP modernization vs. AI initiatives.
- **Output shape:** A 3-column table with current bets, capability needs, and metrics per horizon.
- **Trap:** Don't let H3 become a wishlist. Each H3 bet needs a falsifiable hypothesis and a kill criterion.

## 2. Competitive & Positioning

### Strategic Group Map
- **What it is:** 2-axis chart placing competitors by chosen dimensions (e.g., scope of capability vs. price). Reveals empty space and crowded clusters.
- **When to reach for it:** Differentiating against Oracle/SAP/MS/NetSuite/Odoo in Thailand, positioning a vertical SI practice, deciding which deals to walk away from.
- **Output shape:** A described 2x2 (or 2x3) plus a one-sentence "white space" call.
- **Trap:** Pick axes the *buyer* cares about, not the ones that flatter the seller's product.

### Blue Ocean (4 Actions)
- **What it is:** Eliminate / Reduce / Raise / Create framework to escape red-ocean competition by redefining the value curve.
- **When to reach for it:** When a deal turns into a feature war, when a new offering needs differentiation, when entering a contested government tender.
- **Output shape:** A 4-quadrant table populated for the offering, with one validation question per quadrant.
- **Trap:** Eliminating buyer-valued features to chase margin is the most common failure. Test every "Eliminate" against a real customer voice.

### Wardley Mapping
- **What it is:** Value-chain visualization on an evolution axis (genesis → custom → product → commodity). Exposes where to invest, automate, or outsource.
- **When to reach for it:** Solution-architecture decisions, build-vs-buy debates, pricing strategy, advising a customer on what to keep vs. move to SaaS.
- **Output shape:** A described map (since visuals are limited) plus 3-5 strategic moves with rationale.
- **Trap:** Don't over-engineer the map. The discipline of placing components on the evolution axis is the value, not the diagram itself.

## 3. Customer Value & Demand

### Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)
- **What it is:** Reframes products as "hires" to make progress on a job. Job statement: *When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome].*
- **When to reach for it:** Discovery synthesis, value proposition design, demo storyboarding, why-change framing for executives.
- **Output shape:** 3-7 job statements with priority and emotional/social/functional dimensions.
- **Trap:** Don't confuse a feature need with a job. "Wants a dashboard" is not a job; "wants to defend the close calendar to the audit committee" is.

### Value Proposition Canvas (Osterwalder)
- **What it is:** Pairs customer profile (jobs, pains, gains) with value map (products, pain relievers, gain creators).
- **When to reach for it:** Designing a Why-Us narrative, building a vertical solution package, refining a champion-arming slide.
- **Output shape:** Two side-by-side blocks with explicit fit lines.
- **Trap:** "Pain relievers" must be evidence-backed. Cite the proof unit (case study, benchmark, reference).

### Value Engineering Logic Tree
- **What it is:** Decomposition of business value: Revenue ↑ + Cost ↓ + Cash ↑ + Risk ↓ → driver → metric → lever → solution capability → proof.
- **When to reach for it:** Building a CFO-grade business case, defending TCO/ROI under scrutiny, anchoring price to value, justifying CapEx vs. OpEx in Thai government budget cycles.
- **Output shape:** A tree with each branch ending in a quantified metric and a proof source.
- **Trap:** Round numbers and "industry-average" multipliers are tells. Use customer-specific baselines wherever possible; mark assumptions explicitly.

## 4. Account & Deal Strategy

### Power Map / Stakeholder Map
- **What it is:** Visualization of decision-making power, influence flows, and relationship strength across an account.
- **When to reach for it:** Account planning, complex multi-stakeholder deals, government tenders with steering-committee overlays, recovery from a loss to a competitor's champion.
- **Output shape:** A described map (or table) with: Role · Power (H/M/L) · Stance (Champion/Supporter/Neutral/Skeptic/Blocker) · Coverage (who owns the relationship) · Next action.
- **Trap:** Don't conflate title with power. In Thai conglomerates, an in-law of the chairman with no formal title can be the real Economic Buyer.

### MEDDPICC Strategic Layer
- **What it is:** Strategic interpretation of MEDDPICC — not as a checklist, but as a question discipline: do we understand the *why* behind each letter?
- **When to reach for it:** Deal qualification, deal-review prep, forecast hygiene, deciding "fight or fold."
- **Output shape:** A table with each letter, status (Verified/Assumed/Missing), evidence source, gap, and next move.
- **Trap:** Filling in MEDDPICC from inference rather than from a Champion's words is the #1 self-deception in enterprise sales. Mark "Assumed" honestly.

### "Bowtie" Account Model
- **What it is:** Frame for full-funnel account strategy spanning land → adopt → expand → renew/advocate, with named owners at each stage.
- **When to reach for it:** Strategic account plans, GTM design for a named-account team, renewal and expansion planning with CSM partnership.
- **Output shape:** 5-column plan with goals, owners, milestones, risks, and metrics per stage.
- **Trap:** Don't optimize land at the expense of expand. The first deal sets the architecture for the next five years.

## 5. Solution & Product Strategy

### Capability Map
- **What it is:** Hierarchical decomposition of business capabilities (e.g., "Order to Cash" → "Credit Management" → "Risk Scoring") used to align solution scope to value.
- **When to reach for it:** Fit-gap workshops, multi-module ERP scoping, deciding where to integrate vs. extend, packaging a vertical accelerator.
- **Output shape:** A 2-3 level capability tree with maturity rating and target state per leaf.
- **Trap:** Don't confuse a capability (what the business does) with an application (the system that supports it). Capability stays stable; apps change.

### Operating Model Canvas
- **What it is:** Aligns process, organization, location, information, suppliers, and management system to strategy.
- **When to reach for it:** Transformation framing, change-impact assessment, helping a customer see that "buy ERP" is also "redesign ops."
- **Output shape:** Six-block canvas with the strategic intent on top.
- **Trap:** Don't present this *to* a customer cold. Use it internally to ask better discovery questions, then surface insights selectively.

### Tech Adoption S-Curve / Crossing-the-Chasm
- **What it is:** Adoption-lifecycle framing (innovators → early adopters → early majority → late majority → laggards) and the discontinuity between vision-buyers and pragmatist-buyers.
- **When to reach for it:** Positioning AI/automation initiatives in Thai mid-market, pacing innovation talk for risk-averse government buyers, sequencing a multi-year reference strategy.
- **Output shape:** A short narrative placing the buyer on the curve plus the matching message.
- **Trap:** A government-CIO buyer is almost never an innovator. Pitching "be first" to them is a credibility leak.

## 6. Strategy Execution & Change

### McKinsey 7-S
- **What it is:** Diagnostic framework for organizational alignment (Strategy, Structure, Systems, Shared values, Style, Staff, Skills).
- **When to reach for it:** Change-readiness assessment, post-merger integration scoping, explaining to a CEO why the ERP project is actually a leadership project.
- **Output shape:** Seven short sections, each with a current/target state and a gap.
- **Trap:** Avoid lecturing a customer with this model. Use it to inform your discovery.

### ADKAR
- **What it is:** Individual change journey: Awareness → Desire → Knowledge → Ability → Reinforcement.
- **When to reach for it:** Adoption planning, selling change-management services, framing "the second sale" (post go-live adoption).
- **Output shape:** Five rows mapped to roles (e.g., regional controller, branch manager) with intervention per stage.
- **Trap:** Don't propose ADKAR generically. Tie each stage to a measurable behavior change.

### RACI
- **What it is:** Responsibility assignment matrix (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed).
- **When to reach for it:** Project governance design, deal pursuit-team operating model, post-sale handover to delivery.
- **Output shape:** Compact matrix with task rows and role columns.
- **Trap:** Multiple "Accountable" entries on one row is the signal that ownership is unclear. Force exactly one A.

## 7. Decision-Making & Communication

### Pyramid Principle (Minto)
- **What it is:** Top-down communication structure: Answer first, then grouped supporting reasons (MECE), then evidence.
- **When to reach for it:** Executive emails, exec-sponsor meetings, business-case write-ups, deal-review summaries — *everything* that lands in front of a senior decision-maker.
- **Output shape:** Headline → 3 reasons → evidence per reason. Each reason is parallel in form.
- **Trap:** Burying the answer is the most common offense. If the headline doesn't survive deletion of every supporting bullet, rewrite it.

### MECE
- **What it is:** Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive — every option/category appears once, no gaps.
- **When to reach for it:** Whenever the model is tempted to write a list. Especially: buyer segments, value drivers, risks, options.
- **Output shape:** A list or table where the test "where would *this case* go?" has exactly one answer.
- **Trap:** Forcing MECE on a fundamentally fuzzy problem creates false precision. Call out fuzziness rather than hide it.

### Decision Matrix (Weighted)
- **What it is:** Options scored across weighted criteria; total score reveals the ranked recommendation.
- **When to reach for it:** Vendor down-select, deal pursuit go/no-go, build-vs-buy, sequencing initiatives in a roadmap.
- **Output shape:** Criteria column with weights, then one column per option with scores and weighted totals.
- **Trap:** The weights drive the answer. Make weights and reasoning explicit so the user can challenge them.

### 2x2 Prioritization (Effort/Impact, Reach/Confidence, etc.)
- **What it is:** Visual ranking on two axes, surfacing the upper-right "do now" quadrant.
- **When to reach for it:** Triage in pipeline reviews, feature-trade-off conversations, account portfolio decisions.
- **Output shape:** Described 2x2 with each item placed and a "do now / do next / defer / drop" call.
- **Trap:** A 2x2 with everything in the upper right is not analysis — it's avoidance.

## 8. Selection guide (which framework, when)

| Question shape | Reach for |
|---|---|
| "Should we play in this market / vertical?" | Porter Five Forces + PESTEL + Three Horizons |
| "How do we differentiate vs. [competitor]?" | Strategic Group Map + Blue Ocean + Value Prop Canvas |
| "What's the customer really hiring us to do?" | JTBD + Value Engineering Logic Tree |
| "Why are we losing / stalling this deal?" | Power Map + MEDDPICC strategic layer |
| "What does the long-term account plan look like?" | Bowtie Account Model + Capability Map |
| "Is the customer ready to absorb this change?" | 7-S + ADKAR + Operating Model Canvas |
| "How do I get the executive to say yes?" | Pyramid Principle + Why-Stack (see references/why-stack-narrative.md) |
| "Which option do we recommend and why?" | Decision Matrix or 2x2, anchored in MECE options |
| "Where do we keep building vs. buy / commoditize?" | Wardley Map |

When in doubt, default to the **smallest framework that exposes the trade-off**. A clean MECE list often beats a fancy 2x2.
