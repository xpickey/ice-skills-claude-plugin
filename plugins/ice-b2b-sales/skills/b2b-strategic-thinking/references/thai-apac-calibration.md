# Thai / APAC + Government Calibration

This reference is **default-on** for this skill. The user's primary operating context is Thailand, with extended reach across APAC. A strategic recommendation that is technically right but culturally tone-deaf is a strategic loss in this market.

The goal here is not to add ornamentation — it is to **shift the underlying recommendation** when the operating context demands it.

---

## The two non-negotiables

1. **Relationships are the strategy, not the lubricant for it.** In Thai enterprise, government, and family-business buys, the multi-year relationship arc *is* the deal. A one-quarter forecast view will mislead you here.
2. **Face and bunkhun are economic forces, not soft factors.** Underestimating face-loss risk costs deals. Underestimating bunkhun (the obligation a buyer feels to a person who helped them) loses to incumbents that look weaker on paper.

If your recommendation can't survive these two filters, rewrite it.

## Cultural concepts that change the recommendation

### Kreng jai (เกรงใจ)
The reluctance to impose, disagree openly, or cause discomfort. Implications:
- "Yes" often means "I hear you," not "I agree."
- Silence in a meeting is often disagreement, not consent.
- Hard objections will surface offline, not in the room.
- **Strategic move:** build offline channels (1:1 coffees, dinners) where the real conversation happens. Don't force decisions in formal meetings.

### Face / Nahb-nah-tee (นับหน้าถือตา)
Public standing, dignity, and the avoidance of public embarrassment. Implications:
- Never publicly contradict a senior in front of juniors.
- Don't ask a senior a question they can't answer well in public.
- A losing recommendation must include a face-saving narrative.
- **Strategic move:** privately preview hard messages with the senior before the meeting; let them appear to lead the consensus.

### Bunkhun (บุญคุณ)
The moral debt one feels to someone who has done them a kindness. Implications:
- An incumbent vendor that helped during a crisis carries deep bunkhun, regardless of current product fit.
- New vendors must respect, not attack, the incumbent.
- The right move is often to position as **complementary** to the incumbent, not as a replacement.
- **Strategic move:** acknowledge the incumbent's contribution; frame your value as additive; let the customer choose to displace if and when they're ready.

### Hierarchy / Phu Yai - Phu Noi (ผู้ใหญ่-ผู้น้อย)
The senior-junior relationship structures decision flow. Implications:
- The Economic Buyer is rarely the loudest voice; they speak last and decisively.
- Pre-aligning with junior champions is necessary but not sufficient — the senior's blessing matters more than the spreadsheet.
- **Strategic move:** map the hierarchy explicitly (see `references/frameworks-library.md` Power Map), and design the engagement to give the senior a clean, dignified path to "yes."

### Sanuk / Mai Pen Rai (สนุก / ไม่เป็นไร)
Lightness, ease, "no problem" — values that smooth interaction. Implications:
- Heavy-handed "burning platform" pitches land badly.
- A relaxed, confident posture wins more than urgency theater.
- **Strategic move:** carry a long-game posture even on this-quarter deals; let the customer feel the relationship is comfortable, not transactional.

## Buyer archetypes in Thailand

### Thai Government / SOE / Public Sector
- **Decision frame:** committee + policy alignment + audit defensibility
- **Procurement reality:** e-GP / e-bidding / TOR-driven; vendor selection criteria are pre-published and tightly scored
- **Why-Now drivers:** fiscal-year budget cycles (Oct-Sep), policy mandates (Thailand 4.0, Digital Government, BCG Economy), audit findings from OAG/SAO, ministerial directives
- **Decision criteria emphasized:** local references, MOF-registered status, knowledge transfer, no single-vendor lock-in, PDPA + Cloud Security Standard 2567 compliance
- **Decision criteria de-emphasized:** raw innovation, "first-mover" pitches, US/EU-only references
- **Strategic move:** invest in TOR shaping early, build steering-committee map, position for **defensibility under audit** before "best of breed"
- **Hand-off skills:** `advisor-govt-gfmis`, `govt-egp-gfmis`, `smart-pao-platform`, `legal-it-thailand-cloud`

### Thai Tier-1 Enterprise (PTT, SCG, CP, banks, telcos)
- **Decision frame:** strategic transformation + competitive parity with regional peers + executive ambition
- **Procurement reality:** structured RFP with weighted scoring; CFO + CIO co-sign; group-level procurement often overlays
- **Why-Now drivers:** group strategy refresh, M&A integration, ESG/sustainability mandates, regional expansion, leadership transitions
- **Decision criteria emphasized:** vendor financial strength, regional delivery muscle, executive sponsorship from vendor side, peer-group references (SET-listed, regional MNC)
- **Strategic move:** lead with executive-level sponsorship and a strategic narrative; let product fit be table-stakes; commit to local C-level engagement
- **Hand-off skills:** `b2b-enterprise-sale-strategy` (Large Enterprise sub-strategy), `oracle-cloud-applications-consulting`, `oracle-ebs-consulting`

### Thai Mid-Enterprise / Family Business / Ethnic Chinese SME
- **Decision frame:** owner-led + cash-conscious + multi-generational legacy
- **Procurement reality:** owner is the decision-maker; CFO/CIO advise but rarely decide; relationship with the salesperson matters
- **Why-Now drivers:** generational succession, IPO ambition, BOI promotion, ERP migration off legacy (often EBS, NetSuite, or Microsoft Dynamics on aging hardware)
- **Decision criteria emphasized:** monthly cash impact (not NPV), local Thai-speaking team, references from peer family businesses, owner-relationship continuity, "the platform my kids will inherit"
- **Decision criteria de-emphasized:** complex TCO models, multi-currency edge cases, US-style "best practices" pitches
- **Strategic move:** earn the owner's trust through patience and presence; reframe finance in cash terms; provide a peer reference dinner
- **Hand-off skills:** `b2b-enterprise-sale-strategy` (Normal Enterprise / Mid-Market sub-strategy), `oracle-netsuite-consulting`, `fin-tech-consulting`

### Thai Subsidiaries of MNCs
- **Decision frame:** local execution within a regional or global standard
- **Procurement reality:** decision often made at HQ (Singapore, Tokyo, US, Europe); local team executes and influences
- **Why-Now drivers:** global rollout cadence, regional consolidation, Thai compliance gaps (PDPA, e-Tax invoicing, withholding tax)
- **Decision criteria emphasized:** local Thai/Lao language support, statutory compliance localization, integration to global parent's ERP/EPM
- **Strategic move:** map the dual-track decision (HQ + local), arm the local team to influence HQ, deliver localization proof early
- **Hand-off skills:** `oracle-cloud-applications-consulting`, `oracle-netsuite-consulting`

## APAC extension (selective)

### Singapore
- More structured, English-first, less hierarchical than Thailand
- Government tenders highly competitive and process-rigid (GeBIZ)
- MAS regulations dominate financial services buys
- **Move:** lead with structured business cases; references from Singapore peers are decisive

### Vietnam
- Hierarchical and relationship-driven, similar in shape to Thailand
- Younger, faster-growing market; more risk-tolerance in private sector
- State-owned enterprise (SOE) buys mirror Thai government in process rigor
- **Move:** invest in local language and partner ecosystem; expect slower deal cycles than the velocity suggests

### Indonesia
- Largest APAC market by population; very hierarchical; relationship-driven
- Currency, language, and regulatory localization are non-trivial
- Sharia compliance matters in financial services
- **Move:** local partner mandatory; design for multi-island delivery realities

### Malaysia
- English-friendly, structured; balance of Bumi-policy and global multinational presence
- MITI / Bumiputra equity considerations affect partner structure
- **Move:** partner structure is the strategy; lead with localization

### Philippines
- English-first, Western-influenced decision style, strong BPO ecosystem
- Family-conglomerate buyers similar to Thai patterns
- **Move:** lean on US-style structured business cases; respect the conglomerate hierarchy

## Calibration patterns to apply

When this skill produces output for a Thai/APAC context, apply:

1. **Add a "Relationship & Face" section** to any account or deal recommendation, naming bunkhun, incumbent risks, and the Champion's standing.
2. **Translate financial cases into cash-by-month** alongside NPV/IRR for mid-enterprise.
3. **Add a "Defensibility" section** to any government recommendation, naming the audit, policy, and PDPA fit.
4. **Use a longer time horizon** (3-5 years) by default; quarterly thinking is a Western tic that misleads here.
5. **Acknowledge the incumbent** before proposing a displacement; never disparage.
6. **Provide a face-saving narrative** for any "pass" or "delay" recommendation that the customer can use internally.
7. **Map the steering committee** explicitly for any government deal — don't conflate the formal org chart with the real power map.
8. **Default to in-language assets** (Thai for Thailand-specific) when the user signals the audience is Thai-only.
