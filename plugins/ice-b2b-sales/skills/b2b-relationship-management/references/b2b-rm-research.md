## 1. Executive Summary

You already operate a strong sales-side skill stack (`b2b-enterprise-sale-strategy`, `b2b-questioning`, the `sales:*` family, `customer-support:*` family). What is **missing** is a unifying discipline that governs the **relationship itself** across the whole customer lifecycle — from first executive touch through renewal, expansion, advocacy, and (when needed) recovery.

A dedicated **B2B Relationship Management (B2B-RM)** skill is justified because relationship quality, not product quality, is the primary driver of net revenue retention (NRR) in enterprise software. McKinsey's research shows that providers with sophisticated value-realization and adoption journeys produce NRR roughly **seven percentage points higher** than peers with basic practices. BCG's Key Account Management work shows that mature KAM lifts revenue **5–10%**, margin **3–5 percentage points**, and cuts cost-to-serve **10–20%**. These gains are not accidents — they are the output of a deliberate relationship discipline.

This document defines what must go into your B2B-RM skill: scope, mandatory components, mandatory characteristics, foundational frameworks (BCG, McKinsey, SAMA, Gartner), Thai cultural overlay, and the Thai government/state-enterprise overlay you specifically called out.

## 2. Definition: What "B2B Relationship Management" Means in Your Context

In an Enterprise Software Solution business, B2B Relationship Management is the **end-to-end discipline of designing, building, governing, and growing strategic, multi-stakeholder, multi-year relationships with enterprise customers**, with the explicit goals of:

1. Winning the first deal on **value**, not price.
2. Realizing the promised business outcomes during implementation.
3. Becoming the **trusted advisor** rather than a replaceable vendor.
4. Compounding the relationship into renewals, expansions, references, and co-innovation.
5. Surviving — and recovering from — the inevitable escalations, churn risks, and political shifts on the customer side.

It is **distinct from** transactional selling (one-off deals), customer support (ticket resolution), and customer success (product adoption only). It is the **connective tissue** that makes all three work together.

## 4. Mandatory Components (The "What" — Must-Have Capabilities)

These nine components are the non-negotiable building blocks of the skill. Each becomes either a section in `SKILL.md` or a dedicated reference file in `references/`.

### 4.1 Account Intelligence Layer
A single source of truth on the customer that the skill maintains and refreshes. Includes corporate profile, financials, strategic priorities, competitive context, regulatory exposure, prior history with us, install base, contract footprint, and known initiatives. **Without this, every other component is guessing.**

### 4.2 Stakeholder & Power Mapping
A structured org chart of the customer's buying committee — Gartner now puts the average B2B buying committee at **6–10 decision-makers** — annotated with role, influence, sentiment toward us, sentiment toward incumbent/competitors, personal motivations, and our coverage status. Anchored on three roles: **Champion, Economic Buyer, Technical Evaluator.** Layered with RACI for any active workstream.

### 4.3 Strategic Account Plan (SAP)
The single document that codifies the relationship strategy for a given account. Per SAMA's 7-step value co-creation model, it covers: customer profile → needs analysis → unique value definition → account objectives → strategy & action plan → execution governance → opportunity capture loop. Must include a **white-space analysis** (what we sell vs. what they could buy from us) and a **share-of-wallet target**.

### 4.4 Engagement Cadence (QBR / EBR / Ad-hoc)
A rhythmic, governance-grade meeting structure. Quarterly Business Reviews (QBR) with operational stakeholders, Executive Business Reviews (EBR) annually or semi-annually with C-level. Each meeting has a mandatory agenda spine: strategic performance review → challenges/obstacles → collaborative goal-setting → value demonstration → next-step roadmap. Pre-meeting prep is non-optional: data pulled, deck shared in advance, internal stakeholders aligned.

### 4.5 Account Health Score & Risk Assessment
A composite indicator combining product adoption, support volume/severity, executive sentiment, financial health, contract status, NPS/sentiment, and stakeholder turnover. Drives a four-state classification: **Green / Yellow / Orange / Red**. Each state triggers a defined playbook. Health score is the early-warning system that prevents Red surprises.

### 4.6 Multi-Threading & Champion Development
Discipline of building **multiple, independent** relationships inside the customer (minimum 3+ contacts per active account) so that the relationship survives a champion's departure or reorg. Includes a Champion Development Plan: identify → enable → arm → reward → protect. Detects single-threaded accounts as a Yellow risk automatically.

### 4.7 Value Realization Tracking
Mechanism to convert pre-sale promises into post-sale evidence. Captures the **business case** at sale-time (the ROI / TCO / process-improvement claims), then tracks actuals quarter-by-quarter. Becomes the primary input to renewals and expansions and the primary defense against price-down pressure. McKinsey identifies value-realization as the #1 driver of the NRR advantage.

### 4.8 Renewal, Expansion & Reference Motion
Time-phased motion that starts **180 days before** any renewal or anniversary: re-baseline the value case, identify expansion white-space, secure executive endorsement, structure the commercial offer, and convert successful customers into references and co-marketing partners. Reference-conversion is itself a relationship deepening event, not just a marketing transaction.

### 4.9 Escalation, Recovery & Exit-Risk Management
Playbook for when things go wrong. Escalation triage, executive-to-executive recovery, war-room governance, communication discipline, root-cause documentation, and — when warranted — a graceful exit that preserves the option to return. Connects to your existing `customer-support:customer-escalation` skill but operates at the **relationship level**, not the ticket level.

## 5. Mandatory Characteristics (The "How" — Quality Attributes)

These six characteristics define the *style* the skill must adopt for every output it produces. They map to your role as a "transformation strategist and business advisor" and to your Thai + Government context.

### 5.1 Lifecycle-Integrated, Not Phase-Siloed
Every output must reference where the account is in its lifecycle (Prospect → Pursuit → Onboard → Adopt → Expand → Renew → Advocate / Recover) and the next two stages it is moving toward. Relationship management fails when each function (sales, delivery, CSM) optimizes only its own slice.

### 5.2 Cross-Functional by Design
Outputs must be readable and actionable by **all four user roles** (Sales Leader, KAM, BDM, Pre-Sales). Use a shared vocabulary, shared scoring, shared artifacts. Per BCG, KAM is "an enterprise-wide initiative involving not just the sales force" — the skill must reflect that.

### 5.3 Data-Driven and Evidence-Anchored
No assertion about an account without source. Every health score, every claim of executive sponsorship, every white-space opportunity must cite the artifact (CRM record, meeting note, public filing, system log) that supports it. McKinsey notes data privacy and compliance are now the **#1 customer concern** — same discipline applies to how we handle and cite customer data.

### 5.4 Culturally Intelligent (Thai-Calibrated by Default)
Every customer-facing artifact must be calibrated against the four Thai dimensions: **kreng jai** (deference, soften the ask), **bunkhun** (debt of gratitude, honor reciprocity), **face / นับหน้าถือตา** (never embarrass in public), **hierarchy / ผู้ใหญ่-ผู้น้อย** (right register for seniority). Thai is high-context — read what is **not** said. Research on Thai ERP adoption has even documented bunkhun being deliberately invoked as a change-management lever.

### 5.5 Outcome-Oriented, Not Activity-Oriented
Outputs are graded by what they **change in the relationship**, not by how many meetings/emails/QBRs they produce. Activity is the cost; outcome is the value. The skill must always answer "so what?" — what will be different about this account next quarter because of this artifact?

### 5.6 Trust-First, Service-Mind (จิตบริการ)
Reuse the philosophy already encoded in `b2b-enterprise-sale-strategy`: partnership and empathy precede commercial extraction. Trust compounds; transactions don't. This characteristic is what differentiates a "Senior Partner / Managing Director-level" advisor from a quota-carrying rep.

## 6. Foundational Frameworks the Skill Must Encode

The skill must be built on top of recognized frameworks so its outputs carry external authority. Five are mandatory.

### 6.1 BCG — Key Account Management (KAM)
Codify BCG's definition: KAM develops **strategic, not transactional** relationships with a limited number of customers for long-term, sustained, measurable business value. Embed BCG's **four common KAM mistakes** as anti-patterns:
1. Wrong accounts designated as "key" (highest-upside missed).
2. Too many key accounts relative to resources.
3. Key relationships drift back to transactional.
4. Discounts given without joint value creation.

Embed BCG's expected outcomes as success benchmarks: **+5–10% revenue, +3–5pp margin, –10–20% cost-to-serve.**

### 6.2 BCG — Account-Based Engagement (ABE)
Use BCG's evolution of ABM into ABE as the multi-threading and cross-functional orchestration model. Marketing, sales, service, and pre-sales are coordinated against the same target stakeholder list.

### 6.3 McKinsey — Customer Experience Building Blocks + NRR Advantage
Encode McKinsey's findings that **value realization and adoption journeys** are the dominant NRR lever (~7pp uplift). Encode the 2026 trend stack: **agentic AI for low-value interactions, humans on high-value relationships; data privacy & compliance as the #1 customer concern; preference for off-the-shelf or managed-service AI consumption (~80%).**

### 6.4 SAMA — 7-Step Value Co-Creation Framework
Use SAMA (Strategic Account Management Association) as the structural backbone for the Strategic Account Plan: Profile → Needs → Unique Value → Objectives → Strategy → Execution → Opportunity Capture. Position the skill so its output would map cleanly to a CSAM (Certified Strategic Account Manager) review.

### 6.5 Gartner — Buying Committee + Customer Health Score
Use Gartner's buying-committee research (6–10 stakeholders average; champion + economic buyer + technical evaluator as anchors) for the Stakeholder & Power Mapping component. Use Gartner's customer-success-platform health-score categories for the Account Health component.

## 7. Thai Cultural Overlay (Operationalized)

Generic global frameworks fail in Thailand if applied without cultural translation. The skill must hard-wire these operational rules:

- **Hard messages go private, not public.** A Red health score, a renewal at risk, a champion losing influence — these are surfaced in 1:1 conversation with the senior contact, never in a multi-stakeholder QBR. Public exposure causes face loss and ends the relationship.
- **Bunkhun is a balance sheet, not an emotion.** Track favors received and favors given. Honor reciprocity. A customer who once made a personal call to clear an internal blocker is owed a personal call when *they* are in trouble. The skill must remind users to log and honor these debts.
- **Hierarchy dictates register.** Outputs intended for ผู้ใหญ่ (senior contacts) use formal Thai register, are typically delivered by an equally senior person from our side, and follow protocol (advance briefing, agenda shared, no surprises). Outputs for working-level contacts can be more direct.
- **High-context — read the silence.** When a Thai stakeholder goes quiet, that **is** the answer. The skill must explicitly prompt users to ask "what was not said?" after every meeting, and to follow up privately rather than push for an answer in the room.
- **Relationship before business.** First-meeting agendas should leave 20–30% of time unstructured for relationship building. Going straight to commercial topics signals you are a vendor, not a partner.

## 8. Thai Government / State Enterprise Overlay

Per your stated emphasis on Thai government / state enterprise customers, the skill must encode this overlay:

- **Two-tier relationship.** You manage two simultaneous relationships: the **agency / SOE** (your customer) and the **regulatory ecosystem** (Comptroller General's Department, สตง., e-GP rules, GFMIS standards). Both must be healthy.
- **Long arc, not quarterly.** Thai government deals run **6–18 months** from first conversation to PO; relationships span **5–10 years** through TOR cycles, AMS contracts, and re-procurement. The Account Plan must use a 3-year minimum horizon.
- **Local presence is non-negotiable.** US Commerce confirms what every practitioner knows: a "reputable local representative with good access to the procuring agency" is the difference between winning and being shortlisted-but-lost. The skill must surface "do we have local access to this agency?" as a Day-1 health check.
- **Preferential treatment for incumbents.** The Thai system gives preference to suppliers who have already established commercial relationships. Translate this into the skill's strategy: protect the install base aggressively; assume any non-incumbent pursuit is uphill.
- **Mandatory deliverable triad.** Thai government proposals must include technology transfer + training + after-sales service. The skill must prompt for these three even in early account planning, not just at proposal time.
- **Relationship management has compliance implications.** Gifts, hospitality, and "informal" facilitation that are normal in some markets are PDPA/anti-corruption risks here. The skill must reference the existing `legal-it-thailand-cloud`, `govt-egp-gfmis`, and `advisor-govt-gfmis` skills for any compliance-sensitive guidance and never improvise on these topics.

## 11. Success Criteria (Project Workflow Step 1.6)

The skill is "successful" if, on representative test prompts, it produces:

1. **A Strategic Account Plan** that a SAMA-certified KAM would recognize as professional-grade and that a CFO would accept as a budget-justification document.
2. **A Stakeholder Power Map** that names a Champion, Economic Buyer, Technical Evaluator, and at least 3–5 other named stakeholders, each with influence rating, sentiment, and our coverage status.
3. **An Account Health Score** with explicit inputs, weighting, current state (G/Y/O/R), the top 3 risks driving the score, and the next 3 actions to improve it.
4. **A QBR or EBR agenda** that follows the mandatory spine (performance → challenges → goals → value → next steps) and is calibrated to Thai register for the named senior attendee.
5. **A Renewal Motion** that starts 180 days out, identifies expansion white-space, names the executive endorsement strategy, and defines the commercial structure.
6. **An Escalation Recovery Plan** that operates at the relationship level (not the ticket level) and includes executive-to-executive choreography.

Every output must:
- Cite its evidence source.
- Apply Thai cultural calibration where any customer-facing artifact is involved.
- Apply the Thai government overlay when the account is government / SOE.
- Cross-reference (not duplicate) the existing skill base.
