# Strategic Account Plan (SAP) — Reference

This is the canonical structure for a Strategic Account Plan in enterprise software. Built on SAMA's 7-step value co-creation framework, adapted to Oracle / SAP / Microsoft / NetSuite / Salesforce / Workday selling motions, and calibrated for Thai commercial and government accounts.

A SAP is the single document that codifies the relationship strategy for a designated account. It is **not** a deal sheet, not a pitch deck, and not a status report. It is the multi-year operating plan for the relationship.

## When to Build a SAP

Mandatory for any account flagged as Tier-1 (Strategic) or Tier-2 (Key). Recommended for any Tier-3 (Growth) account with credible expansion potential >= 3x current ARR within 24 months.

Not appropriate for transactional accounts — those are managed through the pipeline, not through SAPs.

## SAMA 7-Step Backbone

Every SAP follows these seven sections in order.

### 1. Customer Profile

The account as it actually is, not as we wish it were.

- Corporate identity: legal entity, parent, subsidiaries, ownership structure (family, MNC, state, listed)
- Industry and sub-industry
- Financial snapshot: revenue, growth, profitability, recent earnings calls (if listed)
- Strategic priorities (last 12 months public statements + internal intel)
- Major initiatives in flight or announced (digital transformation, M&A, new market, regulatory)
- Org chart at the C-level and the level below for the buying domain
- Our installed base inside this account: products, contracts, end-dates, ARR, AMS spend
- History with us: wins, losses, pauses, escalations, references, NPS
- Competitive landscape inside this account: incumbents, recent vendor decisions, known biases
- For Thai government / SOE accounts: regulatory ministry, oversight body, current 5-year plan alignment, e-GP track record

### 2. Customer Needs Analysis

What the customer is actually trying to do — in their words.

- Top 3 strategic objectives (from their planning docs, earnings calls, public commitments)
- Operational pains we are positioned to address
- Stated pains vs. observed pains (the gap is often where the value is)
- Trigger events: regulatory changes, leadership changes, M&A, IPO, public-sector budget cycles
- Buying committee composition and decision-making style (consensus / hierarchical / champion-driven)
- Cultural overlay (apply `thai-cultural-overlay.md`): how decisions actually get made here

### 3. Unique Value Definition

What we uniquely deliver to *this customer* that no one else credibly can.

- Three-part value statement: business outcome + quantified impact + differentiated proof
- Mapping of our solutions to their stated objectives (one-to-one)
- The "only-we" claim: what is true about us at this account that is not true of competitors
- Reference customers in the same industry / size / geography
- For government: incumbent advantage (ours or a competitor's), local-presence credibility, technology-transfer track record

### 4. Account Objectives (3-Year Horizon)

Where the relationship needs to be in 36 months. Use SMART format.

- Revenue objectives: ARR, services, AMS — by year
- Footprint objectives: products, modules, business units, geographies
- Relationship objectives: number of executive sponsors, board-level access, advisory board seats
- Strategic objectives: reference, co-innovation, joint case study, joint event
- Defensive objectives: incumbent displacement risk, contract concentration risk, key-person risk

For Thai government accounts use a minimum 3-year horizon (often 5) reflecting TOR cycles and AMS contract durations.

### 5. Strategy & Action Plan (12-Month View)

The plays we will run to move from current state to objectives.

Organize by stage of the lifecycle the account is in. For each play:

- Play name and one-sentence description
- Targeted stakeholder(s) and the engagement vehicle (executive briefing, design-thinking workshop, value workshop, exec dinner, joint roadmap session)
- Owner (named) + supporting cast (named)
- Timing (quarter)
- Success criterion (specific, measurable)
- Risk and mitigation

Typical play categories:
- Executive sponsorship establishment
- Champion development (see `champion-development.md`)
- Multi-threading expansion
- Value realization formalization
- Reference-readiness
- Renewal pre-positioning (see `renewal-expansion-motion.md`)
- Expansion / cross-sell
- Competitive defense

### 6. Execution Governance

How the plan stays alive, not just written.

- Internal cadence: monthly account review, quarterly leadership review, annual SAP refresh
- Customer cadence: QBR / EBR schedule (see `engagement-cadence-qbr-ebr.md`)
- Account team roster: KAM (lead), Solution Architect, CSM, Delivery Lead, Pre-Sales, Executive Sponsor (ours), Marketing Partner — with contact info and primary stakeholder coverage
- Health-score review trigger (see `account-health-score.md`)
- Decision rights: which decisions the KAM owns, which require Sales Leader, which require executive escalation

### 7. Opportunity Capture Loop

The mechanism for turning insight into pipeline.

- White-space analysis: matrix of our portfolio × customer business units / functions; cells classified as Sold / Active Pursuit / Identified Opportunity / White Space / Out of Scope
- Share-of-wallet analysis: estimated total addressable spend at this account on our category × current spend with us = current share-of-wallet %; named target % for end of horizon
- Pipeline build target: by quarter, by product line, by stage
- Trigger-event monitoring: which signals (leadership change, earnings, regulation, RFP) we will track and how

## Plan Length and Format

Tier-1 (Strategic): 15–25 pages. Reviewed quarterly with leadership. Refreshed annually.
Tier-2 (Key): 8–15 pages. Reviewed semi-annually. Refreshed annually.
Tier-3 (Growth): 4–8 pages. Reviewed annually.

Use `assets/account-plan-template.md` as the starting structure.

## Quality Checks Before Delivery

- Every section names specific people, not generic roles
- Every objective has a number and a date
- Every play has a named owner
- Cultural register is right for Thai-context accounts
- Government accounts include the regulatory-ecosystem second tier
- White-space analysis is actually filled in (not "TBD")
- The "so-what" is obvious within the first 30 seconds of reading

## Common Failure Modes

See `anti-patterns.md` for full treatment. Quick list:
- SAP written once and never refreshed → useless within 6 months
- Written by the KAM in a vacuum → no buy-in from delivery / pre-sales / CSM
- Reuses last year's wording with the year changed → customer can tell, and so can leadership
- Replaces strategy with activity ("we will hold 4 QBRs") — activity is the cost, not the value
- Treats the customer as a target, not a partner — kills it on contact in Thai culture
