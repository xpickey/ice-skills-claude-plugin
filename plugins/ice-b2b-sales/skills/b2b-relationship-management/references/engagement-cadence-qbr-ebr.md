# Engagement Cadence — QBR / EBR — Reference

The engagement cadence is the rhythm at which the relationship breathes. Without a deliberate cadence, contact collapses to "when something is wrong" — which trains the customer to associate us with problems and erodes the relationship over time.

A healthy cadence has three layers: working-level (weekly to monthly), business-level (Quarterly Business Review — QBR), and executive-level (Executive Business Review — EBR, semi-annual or annual). Each layer has a different audience, a different agenda, and a different decision rights envelope.

## Three-Layer Cadence Model

| Layer | Frequency | Audience (theirs) | Audience (ours) | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Working-level** | Weekly to bi-weekly | Project Owner, end-user lead | KAM / CSM / Delivery Lead | Operational health, ticket triage, near-term roadmap |
| **QBR (Quarterly Business Review)** | Quarterly | Project Owner + Director / VP layer + named functional leads | KAM + CSM + Solution Architect + Delivery Lead | Quarter-on-quarter value, 90-day plan, escalations, expansion seeds |
| **EBR (Executive Business Review)** | Semi-annual (Tier-2) / Annual (Tier-1) | Executive Sponsor (theirs) + C-1 stakeholders | KAM + Sales Leader + Executive Sponsor (ours) + Delivery Executive | Strategic alignment, multi-year roadmap, partnership health, bidirectional commitments |

Tier-1 Strategic accounts: working-level weekly, QBR quarterly, EBR semi-annual.
Tier-2 Key accounts: working-level bi-weekly, QBR quarterly, EBR annual.
Tier-3 Growth accounts: working-level monthly, QBR semi-annual, EBR annual or by trigger event.

## QBR — Quarterly Business Review

The QBR is the workhorse of the cadence. It is **not** a status report and it is **not** a sales pitch. It is a structured business conversation about how the partnership is performing against agreed outcomes and what we are doing together in the next 90 days.

### Standard QBR Agenda (60–90 minutes)

1. **Welcome and outcomes for today** (5 min) — what we want to walk out with
2. **Last-quarter review** (15 min) — what we said we would do, what actually happened, what we learned
3. **Value scorecard** (15 min) — quantified outcomes against the business case (see `value-realization-tracking.md`)
4. **Operational health** (10 min) — adoption, support, SLAs, escalations — green / yellow / red on each
5. **Roadmap and product updates** (10 min) — what is shipping that matters to them, what they should plan for
6. **Customer voice** (10 min) — their priorities for next 90 days, surfacing pain or aspirations
7. **Joint 90-day plan** (10 min) — 3–5 specific actions, named owners on both sides, due dates
8. **Open items and escalations** (5 min)

### QBR Quality Standards

- The customer talks at least 40% of the meeting time
- Every quantified claim is sourced and traceable
- Roadmap items are filtered for relevance to *this* customer — not a generic deck
- The 90-day plan has named owners on both sides — not "the team will…"
- Pre-read sent 48 hours in advance with the scorecard and proposed agenda
- Post-meeting summary within 24 hours with the agreed actions
- For Thai accounts: bilingual deck (TH/EN) or all-Thai depending on attendees; never default to English without checking

### Common QBR Failure Modes

- Becomes a sales pitch — kills trust; the customer stops bringing senior people
- Becomes a status report — the customer can read status; they came for a conversation
- No customer voice — we monologue for an hour and leave
- Recycles the same deck quarter after quarter — signals we are not paying attention
- Skips the value scorecard because numbers are uncomfortable — see `value-realization-tracking.md`

## EBR — Executive Business Review

The EBR is the higher-altitude conversation. It happens less often, with more senior people on both sides, and is structured around the *partnership* rather than the *product*.

### Standard EBR Agenda (90–120 minutes)

1. **Mutual welcome and strategic context** (10 min) — bidirectional updates on company direction
2. **Partnership scorecard** (20 min) — value delivered, value still to come, joint commitments, NPS / sentiment
3. **Their strategic priorities** (20 min) — what changed in their business since last EBR; what is on their executive agenda
4. **Our roadmap and strategic direction** (15 min) — where we are investing, what it means for them, where we want their input
5. **Joint multi-year roadmap** (20 min) — what we are building together over the next 12–36 months
6. **Strategic asks (both directions)** (10 min) — explicit, bidirectional: what we ask of them, what they ask of us
7. **Wrap and named follow-ups** (10 min)

### EBR Quality Standards

- Senior on senior — the executive sponsor on our side **must** attend; if they cancel, reschedule the EBR rather than send a substitute
- Held at the customer's office or a neutral venue — not on a screen by default for Tier-1
- Pre-read goes to the executive sponsor 1 week in advance, with a 1-page exec summary
- Strategic asks are made — an EBR with no asks signals we have no ambition for the relationship
- For Thai SOE / government EBR: protocol-aware seating, gift-giving etiquette, photo opportunity if appropriate, formal title use throughout
- Post-EBR thank-you note from our executive sponsor to theirs within 48 hours

### EBR Failure Modes

- Cancelled or postponed at the customer's request more than once → relationship-level Yellow flag
- Our executive sponsor sends a delegate → signals we don't take them seriously; their executive sponsor will reciprocate
- Generic content — same EBR deck used for three different accounts → fatal
- No strategic asks — we hold an executive's calendar for 90 minutes and leave with nothing committed

## Trigger-Event Cadence (Outside the Schedule)

A healthy KAM also runs unscheduled touches when the customer's world changes. Trigger events worth a proactive call:

- Their earnings release (if listed) — within 48 hours
- Leadership announcement (CEO, CFO, CIO change) — within 48 hours
- M&A announcement — within 24 hours
- Public-sector budget cycle / 5-year plan announcement (Thai government) — within 1 week
- Industry event our exec attended — share what we heard, what it means for them
- Competitor announcement that affects them — proactive POV
- Major product release on our side — pre-brief the champion before public launch

Trigger-event touches are short (15-minute calls or 200-word emails) but signal that we are paying attention.

## Cadence as a Health Signal

Cadence drift is itself a health signal:

- Customer reschedules QBR more than once → Yellow
- Customer attends QBR but Director / VP layer is absent → Yellow
- Working-level cadence collapses (no contact for 30+ days on Tier-1) → Yellow
- EBR cancelled with no reschedule date → Orange

When cadence slips, do not just rebook — diagnose. The slippage is usually upstream of a bigger problem.

## Thai Cultural Calibration

- For senior Thai stakeholders, in-person beats video; same city beats remote; long-term sponsorship beats episodic visits
- For Thai government EBR: agenda and pre-read in Thai; bilingual handout if needed; formal opening (gracious thanks for the time, recognition of seniority); avoid pushing for decisions in the meeting — let them deliberate offline
- For Thai family conglomerates: the most senior person may not say much — read body language; the chairman's nod is the decision; do not "sell" them, treat them as the wisdom in the room
- Gift-giving: appropriate for Lunar New Year, Songkran, Chinese New Year (for ethnic-Chinese-Thai families), end-of-year; modest, branded only if the customer initiates the practice
