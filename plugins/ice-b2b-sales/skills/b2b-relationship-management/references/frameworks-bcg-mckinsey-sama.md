# Foundational Frameworks — BCG, McKinsey, SAMA, Gartner — Reference

This skill is a synthesis of four foundational research streams. Understanding the underlying frameworks helps the practitioner know *why* the playbook says what it says, and lets them adapt with judgment when the situation does not fit the standard pattern.

This file is the reference for the theoretical backbone. The other reference files apply these frameworks to specific operational disciplines.

## 1. BCG — Key Account Management (KAM) and Account-Based Engagement (ABE)

### BCG's Four Common KAM Mistakes

BCG's research on KAM at industrial and B2B-software companies identifies four recurring mistakes that cost companies 5–10% of revenue uplift:

1. **Wrong account selection** — companies designate strategic accounts based on current revenue rather than future value-creation potential
   - Symptom: a "Tier-1" account list that mirrors last year's revenue, with no expansion white-space, while genuinely high-potential accounts are managed transactionally
   - Fix: tier accounts by *future* potential — share-of-wallet expansion possible, multi-product attach possible, reference / advocacy value, strategic-fit with portfolio

2. **No clear value proposition for KAM** — KAM treated as a service tier rather than a value-creation discipline
   - Symptom: the customer cannot articulate what KAM means in practice; KAM activity = more meetings
   - Fix: KAM must produce *jointly-defined value* — co-innovation, structured business reviews, executive access, multi-year roadmap alignment

3. **Lack of disciplined execution** — strategic account plans written and shelved
   - Symptom: SAP refreshed once a year; not used in weekly account team meetings; not visible to leadership
   - Fix: SAP becomes an operating document with monthly review, named owners on every play, leadership cadence

4. **Misaligned organizational capabilities** — KAM as a sales role, with delivery / CS / product treated as separate orbits
   - Symptom: KAM owns the relationship but cannot influence delivery quality or product priorities for their accounts
   - Fix: cross-functional account team with shared accountability for the account's outcome

### BCG Account-Based Engagement (ABE)

ABE extends Account-Based Marketing (ABM) by adding deep personalization, executive-level engagement, and account-team coordination across marketing, sales, and customer success. Key ideas:

- **One account, one team, one plan** — marketing, sales, CS aligned around the account, not the function
- **Hyper-personalization** — content, events, and asks tailored to the named account
- **Executive-to-executive engagement** — orchestrated, not opportunistic
- **Measurement by account outcomes**, not by activity volume

## 2. McKinsey — CX Building Blocks and Net Revenue Retention

### McKinsey's Three CX Building Blocks

McKinsey's research on B2B customer experience identifies three building blocks that account for the majority of CX outcome variance:

1. **Operational excellence in moments that matter** — onboarding, first value, escalation handling, renewal — small set of touchpoints that disproportionately shape perception
2. **Trust-building behaviors** — transparency on issues, proactive communication, follow-through on commitments
3. **Strategic partnership orientation** — understanding the customer's business, contributing beyond the product, helping them succeed broadly

### Net Revenue Retention (NRR) Advantage

McKinsey's research on B2B SaaS shows companies with strong customer success disciplines achieve ~7 percentage points higher NRR than peers — a structural compounding advantage.

Key NRR drivers from the research:
- **Adoption depth** — customers using more of what they bought
- **Value realization documentation** — proven ROI as the foundation of expansion conversations
- **Champion development** — internal advocates who drive expansion and renewal
- **Multi-year contract structure** — commercial structure that locks in renewal momentum
- **Cross-functional account ownership** — sales, CS, product all accountable for retention

## 3. SAMA (Strategic Account Management Association) — 7-Step Value Co-Creation

### SAMA's Strategic Account Plan Structure

SAMA codified the canonical structure of a Strategic Account Plan over decades of practitioner research. The 7-step framework:

1. **Customer Profile** — who they actually are (financial, strategic, organizational, historical)
2. **Customer Needs Analysis** — what they are actually trying to do, in their words
3. **Unique Value Definition** — what we uniquely deliver to this customer
4. **Account Objectives** — multi-year SMART objectives
5. **Strategy & Action Plan** — 12-month operating plan with named plays
6. **Execution Governance** — internal and customer cadence
7. **Opportunity Capture Loop** — white-space, share-of-wallet, pipeline build

## 4. Gartner — Buying Committee and Customer Health Score

### Gartner's Buying Committee Research

Gartner's research on B2B buying behavior consistently shows:
- **Average buying group size** in enterprise software: 6–10 people; growing
- For complex enterprise / regulated buys: often 12–20 people including influencers
- **Buying committees do not buy** — they build consensus through internal dynamics; a single advocate is necessary but not sufficient
- **Three roles always matter**: Champion (internal advocate), Economic Buyer (budget holder), Technical Evaluator (architecture / security / integration)

### Gartner's Customer Health Score Framework

Gartner has codified the customer health score as a composite indicator combining usage, sentiment, financial signals, and relationship indicators. Key ideas:

- **Composite, not single-metric** — single metrics (NPS alone, usage alone, ticket volume alone) over-simplify
- **Leading and lagging indicators together** — early-warning value comes from leading signals
- **Action-triggering, not reporting** — score's purpose is the playbook it triggers
- **Tier-differentiated cadence** — strategic accounts refreshed monthly, growth accounts quarterly

## 5. MEDDPICC and Sales Methodology Heritage

The deal-stage discipline that informs champion / economic-buyer / technical-evaluator identification draws heavily on MEDDPICC — the enterprise sales methodology refined over decades:

- **M**etrics — quantified business outcome
- **E**conomic Buyer — who can say yes
- **D**ecision Criteria — how they will evaluate
- **D**ecision Process — how the decision actually gets made
- **P**aper Process — procurement / legal / signature path
- **I**dentified Pain — the underlying business pain
- **C**hampion — internal advocate
- **C**ompetition — alternatives being considered

This skill embeds MEDDPICC discipline into stakeholder mapping and account planning. For active deal-stage qualification, route to the `b2b-questioning` skill which contains the full MEDDPICC + SPIN + Sandler treatment.

## How to Apply the Frameworks Together

The frameworks are complementary, not competing. A typical Tier-1 account engagement uses:

- **SAMA** for the account plan structure
- **BCG** for account selection / tiering and ABE coordination
- **McKinsey** for value-realization discipline and CX moments-that-matter
- **Gartner** for stakeholder mapping and health-score composition
- **MEDDPICC** for active-deal qualification within the account
- **Thai cultural overlay** as the operating layer over all of the above

When in doubt, use this priority order:
1. Cultural fit first — a framework misapplied culturally is worse than no framework
2. Customer-defined success metrics second — frameworks are means, not ends
3. Discipline of execution third — a B+ framework executed weekly beats an A framework refreshed annually
4. Sophistication last — do not import framework complexity the customer cannot use
