# Renewal & Expansion Motion — Reference

In SaaS / subscription enterprise software, ~70% of customer lifetime value is *post-acquisition* — renewals, AMS, expansion, cross-sell. Yet most sales organizations invest 70% of their effort in net-new acquisition. The renewal-and-expansion motion is the discipline that closes that gap.

The core insight: **renewal is not a transaction at month T-90, it is the cumulative result of every interaction since signature.**

## The Three Renewal Truths

1. **The renewal is won (or lost) by month 6 of the contract** — the customer's experience of value during the first half of the term sets the renewal posture; the last 90 days are mostly procedural confirmation
2. **Surprise is the enemy** — surprises at month T-60 (sudden objections, unannounced procurement changes, unfamiliar new stakeholders) signal we have lost relationship visibility
3. **Expansion conversations belong in QBRs, not in renewal cycles** — bundling expansion into a renewal negotiation gives away leverage and signals we did not earn the expansion separately

## Renewal Lifecycle (T-180 Model)

| Time | Activity | Owner |
|---|---|---|
| **T-180** | Renewal readiness assessment: health score, value realization status, stakeholder map currency, contract review | KAM + CSM |
| **T-150** | Internal renewal strategy: predicted price posture (uplift / hold / discount), expansion attach plan, risks | KAM + Sales Leader |
| **T-120** | Customer conversation: signal renewal intent, surface known concerns, agree process | KAM with Champion |
| **T-90** | EBR / QBR with explicit renewal framing — value scorecard, multi-year roadmap, renewal proposal directionally | KAM + Exec Sponsor |
| **T-60** | Formal renewal proposal delivered with options (term, scope, pricing tiers) | KAM |
| **T-45** | Negotiation, redlines, internal approvals on both sides | KAM + Legal + Procurement (theirs) |
| **T-15** | Final signature workflow | KAM + Procurement (theirs) |
| **T-0** | Contract effective; transition to next-cycle account plan | KAM + CSM |

If at T-120 the customer has not engaged with the renewal conversation: Yellow flag. At T-60 with no engagement: Orange. At T-30 with no engagement: Red.

## Renewal Posture Decision

By T-150, the account team must agree on a posture:

| Posture | When to apply | Example move |
|---|---|---|
| **Premium uplift (5–15%)** | Strong value realization, high adoption, no competitive threat, expansion happening | Annual CPI + value-share uplift, multi-year discount in exchange |
| **List hold** | Healthy account, no compelling case for uplift, no churn risk | Standard renewal at current pricing |
| **Modest discount (single-digit)** | Yellow-state account; value lagging case; competitive pressure named | Discount in exchange for multi-year commitment, expansion attach, or reference rights |
| **Significant restructure** | Orange / Red state; possible scope contraction; customer in financial distress | Right-size scope, possibly temporary discount with milestone-based recovery |
| **Walk-away ready** | Red state with no recovery plan working; bad-fit account; reputational risk | Plan a graceful exit that preserves the relationship for future return |

The posture is internal language — never communicated to the customer in those terms.

## Expansion Attach — Done Separately

Expansion (more modules, more business units, more users, more services) should follow its own motion, not be bolted onto renewal.

### Expansion Trigger Sources

- **White-space mapping** in the Strategic Account Plan (`strategic-account-plan.md`) — proactive identification
- **Adoption thresholds** — module X is at 80% utilization, customer is asking for more capacity / additional capability
- **Trigger events** in the customer (M&A, new business unit, regulatory change, new exec mandate)
- **Value realization momentum** — strong proven ROI on Phase 1 → CFO will fund Phase 2
- **Customer-initiated** — they ask us; the highest-conversion source

### Expansion Sequence

1. **Discover** — discovery conversations with the relevant business owner (often a different stakeholder than the renewal owner)
2. **Quantify** — joint business case for the expansion, modeled in the same format as the original
3. **Propose** — separate proposal, separate signature path where possible
4. **Decouple from renewal** — explicit message to customer: "this is independent of the renewal; we want this to stand on its own merits"

### When Expansion Should Be Bundled with Renewal

Only when the customer asks for it (multi-year commercial structure simplification) or when bundling delivers material customer value (e.g., a unified contract that simplifies their procurement). Otherwise, decouple.

## Cross-Sell vs. Up-Sell

- **Up-sell**: more of the same (more users, higher tier, additional capacity) — generally lower-friction, owned by KAM
- **Cross-sell**: a different product line / module — higher-friction, often involves a different solution architect or product specialist; KAM coordinates but does not necessarily lead the technical conversation

Cross-sell into a different business unit is essentially a new sale into the same logo — treat it with the discovery and pursuit discipline of a net-new opportunity, not the assumed familiarity of an account expansion.

## AMS / Subscription / Support Renewals (Thai Specifics)

For Thai accounts running Oracle EBS, Oracle Cloud, SAP, NetSuite, etc., the AMS / support renewal is a distinct workstream:

- AMS contracts are often annual, sometimes 3-year for government
- Government AMS renewals run through e-GP, with TOR refresh and possible re-bid
- Thai government AMS budget cycles often misalign with the global vendor's renewal anniversary — plan T-270 not T-180 for government accounts
- Local-presence requirement: AMS partners must demonstrate Thailand-based delivery capability
- AMS renewal price uplifts in government are politically sensitive — heavy documentation and value-realization evidence required to justify

## Common Failure Modes

- **First contact about renewal at T-60** — too late to influence posture; you are now negotiating defensively
- **Treating the renewal as a procurement event** — the customer's procurement is doing their job; we should be doing relationship work in parallel, not waiting on procurement to drive the conversation
- **Assuming the same stakeholders will renew** — sponsors leave, mandates shift, evaluation criteria change; re-discover before assuming continuity
- **Bundling expansion into renewal to "save time"** — gives the customer a single-decision veto on both
- **No multi-year strategic option** — without a multi-year option you cannot trade discount for term; you also signal a short-horizon partnership
- **Discounting reflexively when the customer pushes back** — push back politely first; ask why; sometimes they are testing, sometimes they have specific budget pain you can help with structurally

## Cultural Calibration

- **Thai commercial accounts**: renewal conversations are relationship-led; rushing the procedural part can offend; allow time for proper internal discussion on their side
- **Thai government accounts**: renewal is procurement-led by definition (e-GP, TOR, possible re-bid); the relationship work happens *before* the procurement opens, by helping shape the TOR and demonstrating prior performance
- **Thai family conglomerates**: renewal often goes to the chairman / family principal for sign-off even when delegated; a relationship touch at that level mid-cycle (not at renewal time) keeps the door open
- **Bunkhun reciprocity**: prior favors / flexibility on our side build credit that can be drawn down at renewal — but it is not transactional and cannot be cited explicitly
