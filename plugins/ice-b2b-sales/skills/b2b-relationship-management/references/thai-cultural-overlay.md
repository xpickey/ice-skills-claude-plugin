# Thai Cultural Overlay — Reference

This skill is built for enterprise software relationships in Thailand. Western relationship-management frameworks (BCG, McKinsey, SAMA, Gartner) are necessary but not sufficient — applied without cultural calibration they produce relationship damage that is invisible to outsiders and irreversible once done.

The overlay below is the operating layer that sits *on top of* every other reference in this skill.

## Five Core Cultural Concepts

### 1. เกรงใจ (Kreng Jai) — Considerate Reluctance

Reluctance to impose, inconvenience, or cause discomfort to others, especially seniors. It is **not** shyness or weakness — it is a positive social value.

**How it shows up in B2B**:
- Thai customers often will not raise a complaint directly; problems surface as silence, polite delays, or third-party comments
- A customer who is "considering options" may already have decided against us but does not want to deliver bad news directly
- Asking direct yes/no questions in front of others can create kreng jai paralysis — the senior cannot lose face by saying no, the junior cannot speak ahead of the senior

**Operating principle**:
- Read silence as data — escalating silence is a signal, not an absence of one
- Create indirect channels for bad news — 1:1 with the champion, "off-the-record" coffee, asking through a trusted intermediary
- Never put a customer in a position where saying yes/no publicly costs them face

### 2. บุญคุณ (Bunkhun) — Reciprocal Obligation

A debt of gratitude that creates lasting moral obligation. Bunkhun chains are the invisible infrastructure of Thai business relationships.

**How it shows up in B2B**:
- A senior who got their start under a mentor will defer to that mentor decades later
- A vendor who helped a customer through a crisis will be remembered and re-engaged years later
- Conversely, a vendor who pushed too hard during a hard moment will be quietly excluded forever
- Channel partners, family-business networks, alumni networks (Chulalongkorn, Thammasat, Assumption, military academies) are bunkhun-bearing relationships

**Operating principle**:
- Treat bunkhun as a balance sheet — small, well-timed favors accumulate; large favors with strings attached repel
- Help without invoicing it — supporting a customer through a non-commercial moment (sponsor change, audit pressure, personal life event) builds bunkhun
- Never call in bunkhun explicitly — to name the debt is to nullify it
- Track relationship history at the institution level — what we did for *whom* and *when* matters across generations of company leadership

### 3. หน้า / นับหน้าถือตา (Face)

Public dignity. Thai culture is high-context — what is said in front of others is encoded for face preservation.

**How it shows up in B2B**:
- A senior who is contradicted in a meeting will rarely show it but will adjust the relationship afterward
- Detailed disagreement is for 1:1; group meetings are for alignment
- Public presentations are theatre — the real conversation happened beforehand
- Pricing discussion in front of multiple stakeholders is uncomfortable; the principal usually wants to discuss in private

**Operating principle**:
- Hard conversations in private; easy conversations in public
- Never correct a senior in front of their juniors, even when factually correct
- Pre-wire — secure the senior's alignment before the group meeting; the meeting is the ritual of alignment, not the negotiation
- If a senior makes a mistake in front of others, find a way to make it right without highlighting it (re-frame, "build on", "exactly — and to add to your point...")

### 4. ผู้ใหญ่-ผู้น้อย — Hierarchy

Thai business hierarchy is not just organizational; it is moral. The senior has duties; the junior has obligations. Skipping the chain is a deep offense.

**How it shows up in B2B**:
- A KAM emailing a CEO without first informing the CEO's deputy is a serious breach
- A junior who is given a piece of authority by a senior speaks with the full weight of the senior — treat them accordingly
- The "real" decision-maker may be one or two layers above the title-based decision-maker
- Procurement and IT often have less authority than their Western counterparts; the business owner or executive sponsor often has more

**Operating principle**:
- Map the hierarchy explicitly — it is often invisible to outsiders
- Always brief the next layer down before going to the senior; brief the next layer up before bypassing them
- Honor the deputy / chief of staff / personal assistant — they are the gatekeepers and they remember
- For Thai government / SOE: ปลัด (deputy permanent secretary) and ผู้อำนวยการ (director-general) often hold real operational authority

### 5. จิตบริการ — Service Orientation

Genuine, not performative, service mindset. Customers in Thailand value vendors who feel like long-term partners and care visibly about their success.

**How it shows up in B2B**:
- "Vendor" is mildly negative; "partner" is positive; "trusted advisor" is the goal
- Visible care (proactive outreach with no agenda, remembering personal details, showing up for non-commercial moments) is rewarded
- Transactional behavior (only calling when there is a quota to make) is punished by gradual disengagement
- The vendor who shows up to the funeral, the wedding, the temple ordination becomes a permanent fixture in the relationship

**Operating principle**:
- Invest in non-commercial relationship moments — they are the foundation, not the icing
- Personal continuity matters disproportionately — KAM rotations every 12 months are corrosive in Thailand; aim for multi-year continuity
- Visible care must be authentic — performative service is detected and discounted

## Communication Calibration

### High-Context Communication
Thai communication is high-context: meaning is embedded in tone, position, what is *not* said, and the surrounding relationship — not just the literal words.

- "Yes" can mean "I heard you" without meaning "I agree"
- "We will consider" can mean "no, but politely"
- "It might be difficult" usually means "no"
- "Let me discuss with my team" sometimes means "I do not have the authority to say no but will get someone who does"
- Direct disagreement is rare and meaningful — when it happens, take it as significant

### Bilingual Working Norms
- Default to Thai for senior Thai stakeholders, even when their English is excellent
- Bilingual decks (TH/EN side-by-side) are appreciated for executive audiences
- Technical content can default to English; commercial / strategic content should be in Thai
- Numbers in Thai documents: comma vs. period conventions, Thai numerals on official documents
- Buddhist Era (BE) dating on official Thai documents (BE = CE + 543)

### Meeting Etiquette
- Wai (ไหว้) greeting — return appropriately for status; the more senior person's wai height defines the exchange
- Seating order matters — senior-most against the wall facing the door, descending by status
- Business cards offered with two hands; received with two hands; placed on the table not in pocket
- Shoes off in many homes and some traditional offices
- Avoid pointing with feet, touching heads, displaying soles of feet
- Buddhist religious sensitivity — be mindful of imagery, language, and symbols

### Gift-Giving
- Appropriate occasions: Lunar New Year (for ethnic-Chinese-Thai families), Songkran (April), Loy Krathong, year-end, ordination, wedding, funeral
- Gifts modest and tasteful; high-end branded gifts can offend (perceived as bribery); locally-resonant gifts (high-quality Thai craft, premium tea, books) work well
- For government / SOE: anti-corruption rules are strict — coordinate with Compliance; small symbolic gifts during festivals are usually acceptable but document them
- Reciprocity expected — accept gifts graciously and return them at the appropriate next moment

## Relationship Patterns by Customer Segment

### Thai Family Conglomerates
- Decision power often concentrated in the family principal even when delegated operationally
- "Old wealth" families operate on multi-generational time horizons; trust is built over years
- Family schools, military service connections, and multi-generational business relationships shape the decision pattern
- The chief of staff to the principal is often a more important relationship than the CFO
- Patience and continuity are the differentiators; quarterly aggression is read as desperation

### Thai Listed Corporates
- More Western-influenced governance, but Thai cultural overlay still applies
- IR / corporate development teams behave more like global peers
- Audit committee, independent directors, and SET listing rules add formal governance
- Expect formal RFP processes; relationship work happens before and around the RFP

### Thai SMEs and Mid-Market
- Owner-operator dynamics; the owner makes the call regardless of org chart
- Personal relationships dominate; KAM continuity is critical
- Decision cycles can be fast or glacial depending on the owner's mood and confidence
- Price-sensitive but loyalty-prone once trust is established

### Thai Government / SOE
- Covered separately in `thai-govt-soe-overlay.md`

### MNCs in Thailand
- The Thai country team operates under both global vendor-management norms and local relationship norms
- The Thai country GM may have less authority than their Western counterpart suggests; some decisions route to regional HQ in Singapore / Hong Kong
- Local KAM continuity matters even when the global account is managed globally — the day-to-day relationship is in Thailand

## Anti-Patterns (Thailand-Specific)

See `anti-patterns.md` for the full set. Specific Thai-cultural ones:

- **Pushing for a decision in a meeting** — read as disrespectful; Thai decisions are made offline
- **Treating "yes" as "agreement"** — see high-context note above
- **Bypassing the deputy / chief of staff** — relationship-ending in Thai SOE
- **Quarterly KAM rotation** — destroys the personal continuity that Thai relationships require
- **Aggressive "challenger" selling** — works in some Western contexts; usually backfires in Thailand
- **Naming and shaming** — even in escalations, attribution to a specific person is destabilizing
- **Public price negotiation** — pricing belongs in private 1:1
- **Assuming English fluency = preference for English** — many fluent English speakers prefer Thai for serious commercial conversation

## Operating Practice

For every relationship play in this skill (account plan, QBR, EBR, escalation, renewal):
1. Apply the framework as written
2. Re-read it with the Thai cultural overlay
3. Adjust register, channel, sequence, and pace
4. Test with a trusted local advisor before executing on a high-stakes account
