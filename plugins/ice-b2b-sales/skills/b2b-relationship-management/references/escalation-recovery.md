# Escalation & Recovery — Reference

How an account is recovered from a Red state determines whether it churns, becomes a steady customer, or — counterintuitively — becomes one of the strongest references in the portfolio. Customers who experienced a problem that was handled well report higher loyalty than customers who never had a problem at all. This is the "service recovery paradox" and it is a real, measurable effect.

The discipline is: **act fast, take responsibility, name a single owner, communicate over-frequently, and decide cleanly at the end.**

## When This Skill Section Triggers

- Account health score drops to Red (<50) — see `account-health-score.md`
- A Critical Single Signal fires (executive escalation lodged, public dissatisfaction, RFP issued for our category, competitor displacement announced)
- A Sales Leader or executive sponsor flags an account as "at-risk" regardless of score
- A delivery escalation crosses an internal threshold (P1 unresolved >X days, customer-CEO involvement, pattern of repeated SLA misses)
- The customer formally declines a reference call after previously agreeing

## The First 72 Hours

The first 72 hours after escalation set the recovery trajectory. Speed and posture matter more than the recovery plan itself at this stage.

### Hour 0–4: Acknowledge

- KAM personally calls the customer's senior sponsor — voice, not email
- Acknowledge the problem in their language, without justifying or deflecting
- Commit to a specific next contact within 24 hours with a recovery plan
- **Do not** propose solutions yet — at this stage they need to be heard, not pitched

### Hour 4–24: Mobilize Internal

- Sales Leader formally takes ownership at the executive level on our side
- Single named owner appointed for the recovery (usually Sales Leader, sometimes a dedicated Recovery Manager)
- War-room formed: KAM + CSM + Delivery Lead + Solution Architect + Executive Sponsor (ours) + Legal if relevant
- 24-hour standup cadence established
- Root-cause investigation kicked off in parallel with recovery planning (do not wait for RCA to start communicating)

### Hour 24–48: Plan

- Draft 14-day recovery plan with:
  - Acknowledged root cause(s) — even if preliminary
  - Concrete actions, named owners on our side, daily milestones
  - What we are asking of the customer (access, decisions, inputs)
  - How we will measure recovery (specific signals)
  - What "success" looks like at day 14 and at day 60
- Internal review: Sales Leader, Delivery Executive, and where appropriate Country Manager / GM signs off

### Hour 48–72: Communicate

- Formal communication to customer's executive sponsor — written, named, with the plan attached
- Includes: acknowledgment, owner, plan, cadence, escalation path on our side
- Schedule the next 4 weekly executive touchpoints right then — do not leave them as "we'll set up a meeting"
- Follow up the written communication with an in-person meeting where geography allows; for Thai accounts this is non-negotiable for Tier-1 escalations

## The 14-60-90 Recovery Framework

| Phase | Duration | Goal | Signal of success |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Stabilize** | Day 0–14 | Stop the bleeding; restore basic confidence | Customer agrees plan is credible; war-room cadence accepted; no further escalations |
| **Demonstrate** | Day 15–60 | Prove we are executing; deliver visible early wins | Two or more measurable improvements landed; customer's tone shifts; champion (if any) re-engages |
| **Decide** | Day 60–90 | Joint decision on recovery success or path forward | Account back in Orange or better; OR mutually agreed graceful exit; OR escalation to a higher-level intervention |

## Communication Discipline

The single most common recovery failure is communication drift. Once the immediate fire is out, the temptation is to pull back to normal cadence. Resist.

- **Daily** internal standup for first 14 days
- **Weekly** customer-facing status email throughout the 90-day window — same format every week, sent on the same day, even when there is "nothing new"
- **Bi-weekly** executive-to-executive call (our exec → their exec) for first 60 days
- **Single source of truth** — one document maintained by the Recovery Owner that captures status, actions, owners, dates; shared with the customer

The pattern that destroys trust: silence. Even bad news communicated clearly and on schedule preserves trust; silence destroys it.

## Recovery Plan Components

Every recovery plan is built from the same components — adjusted for the specific failure mode:

1. **Acknowledged root cause** — what specifically went wrong, in plain language
2. **Immediate stabilization actions** — what we are doing in the next 14 days to stop the harm
3. **Structural fix** — what we are doing in 30–60 days to ensure it does not recur
4. **Named accountability** — single owner on our side, escalation path defined
5. **Customer commitments** — what we need from them, by when, to enable recovery
6. **Measurement** — specific signals we will track to know we are recovering
7. **Decision point** — explicit agreement on when we will jointly decide if recovery is working
8. **Goodwill gesture** (when appropriate) — fee credit, dedicated resource, executive site visit, free expansion module — proportional to the harm

The goodwill gesture is judgment-dependent. Too small reads as insulting; too large reads as guilt and invites further demands. Calibrate with the Sales Leader and Legal.

## Escalation Severity Tiers (Internal)

| Tier | Definition | Required involvement |
|---|---|---|
| **E1** | Operational issue; recoverable at delivery layer | KAM + CSM + Delivery Lead |
| **E2** | Material relationship issue; sponsor concern; risk of contract impact | + Sales Leader + Solution Architect |
| **E3** | Executive escalation; risk of churn, public criticism, or formal complaint | + Executive Sponsor (ours) + Country Leadership |
| **E4** | Existential to the relationship; possible litigation, regulatory exposure, or strategic-account loss | + Country GM + Regional Leadership + Legal + (where relevant) Global Account Executive |

Escalation tiers can move both ways. A successful E3 recovery can de-escalate to E2 within 30 days. A mishandled E2 can escalate to E3 in a single bad meeting.

## The Decide Point — Day 60–90

By day 60, the war-room must answer one question: **is meaningful recovery happening?**

Three honest answers:

### (A) Yes — recovery on track
- Continue the plan; maintain cadence to day 90
- Begin transitioning out of war-room mode
- Document what worked for institutional learning
- Use the recovery itself as a relationship deepener — propose a joint case study (optional, customer-led)

### (B) No — but a path exists
- Re-scope the recovery plan; extend with a fresh 60-day window
- Consider replacing the named owner — sometimes the right move
- Possibly bring in external help (ours: a senior advisor, a delivery executive from another region; theirs: a third party they trust)
- Be honest with the customer about the reset — do not paper over

### (C) No — and no path exists
- Plan a graceful exit
  - Mutual termination terms negotiated in parallel
  - Knowledge transfer to whoever they choose next (helping them migrate is the strongest long-term move)
  - Honest debrief on what we got wrong — recorded internally
  - Protect the door for future return; today's churn is tomorrow's reference
- Inform leadership; conduct internal post-mortem within 30 days of exit

The hardest move is recognizing (C) early. Sales organizations are trained for relentless optimism, which prolongs failed recoveries and damages reputation. Better to exit cleanly than to drag a Red account through three more cycles of broken promises.

## Cultural Calibration

### Thai Commercial Accounts
- The first acknowledgment must be in person where possible — a phone call works in pinch, an email does not
- Apologies framed as our shortcoming are more readily accepted than apologies framed as system failures (preserve face by owning the mistake personally)
- Avoid blaming subcontractors or partners publicly even when accurate — internal accountability is fine, external blame reads as deflection
- Senior-to-senior contact carries disproportionate weight; if our country GM personally calls their CEO, the issue is heard differently than if it routes through KAM only

### Thai Government / SOE Accounts
- Escalations may have political dimensions on the customer side that we do not see — over-index on listening
- Formal written communication carries weight; an unsigned letter on company letterhead from our country GM matters
- Be careful with documentation — anything we put in writing may end up in audit, regulatory inquiry, or court
- Know the difference between a *complaint* (recoverable) and a *กรณี* (formal case) — once it becomes a formal case, our communication discipline must follow legal review
- Engage Legal early; engage Compliance if there is any chance of regulatory exposure (PDPA, สตง., ป.ป.ช.)

### Thai Family Conglomerates
- A senior family member's displeasure overrides any operational signal — listen for it directly or through trusted advisors
- Recovery often requires a face-to-face with the chairman or family principal — bring our country GM, not just account leadership
- Once the family principal accepts our recovery posture, the rest of the organization will follow; until then, no operational fix will land

## Common Failure Modes

- **Slow first response** — silence in the first 24 hours doubles the perceived severity
- **Defensive posture** — explaining why we are not at fault before acknowledging the customer's experience
- **Multiple owners** — committee-led recoveries fail because no one is accountable
- **Plan without cadence** — a beautiful 60-day plan that nobody updates weekly
- **Premature de-escalation** — declaring victory at day 30 because operational metrics improved while the relationship is still fragile
- **Recovery becomes a sales motion** — using the war-room to sell expansion is read as opportunism and destroys what little trust remains
- **No goodwill gesture, ever** — being technically correct ("our SLA was met") while the customer is hurting reads as cold and bureaucratic

## Output

There is no fixed template — every recovery plan is bespoke. Use the components list above as the structure. Maintain the live recovery doc as a single shared file (one source of truth) reviewed in every standup.
