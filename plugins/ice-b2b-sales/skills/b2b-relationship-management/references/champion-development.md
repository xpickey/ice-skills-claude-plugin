# Champion Development — Reference

A champion is the single highest-leverage relationship in any enterprise account. They sell for us when we are not in the room. They warn us before bad news becomes public. They open doors we cannot open ourselves.

But champions are not found — they are *developed*. And once developed, they have to be protected, because losing a champion is one of the fastest paths from healthy account to lost account.

## What a Champion Actually Is

A champion is **not** a friendly contact, **not** an end-user who likes the product, and **not** a sponsor who endorses the initiative. A champion meets all five of these criteria:

1. **Power** — has real influence on the decision, not just a seat at the table
2. **Pain** — personally feels the problem we are solving, or stands to gain personally from the outcome
3. **Vision** — believes our solution is meaningfully better than alternatives
4. **Capacity** — has the time and political capital to spend on advocacy
5. **Risk-tolerance** — willing to put their own credibility on the line for us

Many people have one or two of these. Champions have all five. If a contact is missing more than one, they are a Coach (information source) or Supporter (favorable, but not active), not a Champion.

## The Five-Stage Development Arc

Champion development is a sequence, not a single conversation. Skipping stages produces fragile champions.

### Stage 1 — Identify

Find candidates with high power and existing pain. Signals:
- Owns or is accountable for the outcome we are addressing
- Has spoken about the problem in internal forums
- Has tried to solve the problem before (often with a competitor or in-house build)
- Has a personal incentive (career, recognition, mandate from above) tied to the outcome
- Asks substantive, forward-looking questions in early meetings

**Anti-signal**: people who agree with everything we say but propose nothing themselves. Pleasant but inert.

### Stage 2 — Enable

Give them the language, evidence, and frame to talk about us internally.

- Personalized one-pager: their problem, our solution, the proof — written so they can forward it
- Reference customer story tailored to their industry / size / stage
- Quantified business case (ROI calculator, payback model) they can take into their internal meetings
- Talking points addressing the specific objections they will face from peers / boss / IT / procurement

**Test**: have they shared what we gave them with someone internally? If yes, they are activating. If no, they are receiving but not advocating.

### Stage 3 — Arm

Give them the tactical materials to drive the deal forward.

- Comparison sheet vs. the leading competitor, structured so they can defend it
- Technical FAQs anticipating IT / security / architecture questions
- Implementation timeline they can use to set internal expectations
- Reference call already arranged — do not make them ask for it
- Internal pitch deck they can adapt and use

**Test**: did they actually use the materials? Ask: "How did the meeting with [their CFO] go on Tuesday?"

### Stage 4 — Reward

Champions take personal risk to advocate for us. Reciprocate explicitly.

- Public recognition: case study, conference panel, podcast, customer advisory board seat
- Career-relevant exposure: introductions to other practitioners, thought-leadership co-authoring
- Inside access: pre-release product previews, executive briefings with our leadership
- For Thai-context: บุญคุณ accumulation matters; small, well-timed gestures (handwritten thank-you, mention to their boss, executive dinner with our exec) carry more weight than expensive gifts

The reward must be **personal** to the champion, not a generic customer benefit. A champion who advocates for us and gets nothing back will burn out by the next renewal cycle.

### Stage 5 — Protect

Champions become targets. Competitors will try to neutralize them. Their own organization may pressure them. Protect them.

- Never put them in a position where they have to defend us with information they do not have
- Warn them ahead of any product issue, price change, or roadmap pivot that affects them
- If they are in trouble internally, help quietly — a call from our exec to their boss can stabilize a wobbling champion
- If they leave the customer, **stay in the relationship with them** — they will be a champion at their next company too
- Never burn a champion to win a battle: do not cite them by name in a competitive situation without explicit permission, do not surprise them in a meeting, do not let our delivery team escalate without warning them

## Champion Health Indicators (Re-Used from Stakeholder Mapping)

A champion is "healthy" when all five are true:

1. **Power** — can actually influence the decision (not just an enthusiastic end-user)
2. **Influence trajectory** — rising or stable, not declining
3. **Personal stake** — has visibly tied their own credibility to this initiative
4. **Access** — can get us in front of the Economic Buyer when needed
5. **Activity** — currently doing things on our behalf without being asked

Loss of any one moves the champion to Yellow. Loss of two moves the relationship to Orange. Loss of the champion entirely (departure, demotion, reorg) moves it to Red until a successor is developed.

## Champion Succession Planning

Single-champion accounts are fragile. Always be developing a second.

- For every named champion, identify and begin developing a successor
- A successor should be at least two of: peer of the champion, direct report of the champion, peer-of-the-peer in another function
- The succession path should be transparent (the champion knows we are doing this and supports it) — secret succession kills trust
- For Thai accounts: succession is often hierarchical — the champion's most-trusted ผู้น้อย is often the natural successor; honor the bunkhun chain

## Champion Turnover Response

When a champion exits the customer:

**Within 24 hours**: outreach to the champion personally — congratulate / commiserate, ask for the inside story
**Within 48 hours**: assess who the natural successor is; identify all internal handover meetings
**Within 1 week**: secure an introduction from the departing champion to the successor or their boss
**Within 2 weeks**: hold a re-orientation meeting with the new contact — do not assume continuity
**Within 30 days**: re-score account health; downgrade if successor is not yet activated

Stay close to the departing champion at their next company. They will sponsor the introduction.

## Cross-Pattern: When the Customer Has No Champion

Sometimes there is no internal advocate, only a procurement-driven evaluation. This is a Yellow / Orange signal.

- Diagnose: is it that we have not yet developed one, or is the account culturally champion-resistant (highly procedural, committee-driven, government bureaucracy)?
- For champion-resistant accounts: shift strategy from champion-led to coalition-led — multi-thread aggressively, build a quorum of supporters rather than one strong advocate
- Thai government / SOE: rarely have a single champion in the commercial sense; the equivalent is a respected senior bureaucrat who carries the file forward — develop the same way but with extra patience for hierarchy

## Anti-Patterns

See `anti-patterns.md`. Specific champion-related ones:
- Treating an end-user as a champion because they like us
- Confusing a paid consultant or partner-of-record for a champion
- Outsourcing champion development to marketing (they can support, not replace)
- Burning out the champion by asking too often
- Going around the champion to their boss without telling them — relationship-ending move

## Output

There is no formal "champion plan" template — champion development is encoded into the Strategic Account Plan (`strategic-account-plan.md`) and Stakeholder Power Map (`stakeholder-power-mapping.md`) as named plays. The discipline is consistency, not paperwork.
