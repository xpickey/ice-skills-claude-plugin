# Strategic Thinking Frameworks — Pick the Right Tool

Use this catalog to select the right Strategic Thinking framework for the question. Strategy is about **choice** — where to play, how to win, what not to do. Every output must produce at least three options with explicit trade-offs.

## The Playing-to-Win Choice Cascade (default synthesizer)

Lafley & Martin's five linked choices. Use as the default spine for any strategy output:

1. **Winning Aspiration** — what success looks like
2. **Where to Play** — markets, segments, geographies, channels
3. **How to Win** — differentiated value proposition, moat
4. **Capabilities Required** — what we must be great at
5. **Management Systems** — how we reinforce and measure

Rule: the five are **linked**. If Where-to-Play changes, How-to-Win changes, and so on. Do not produce a half cascade.

## External Lens Frameworks — Know the Field

### Porter's Five Forces
Industry attractiveness. Five forces: rivalry, new entrants, substitutes, buyer power, supplier power. Use when assessing a vertical or market entry. For Thai market, add a sixth force: **government and regulatory posture**.

### PESTEL
Macro scan: Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal. Use for market-entry analysis and scenario planning triggers. For Thailand, include: BOI incentives, EEC corridor, Thailand 4.0, Digital Government mandate, PDPA, Cloud Standard 2567.

### Strategic Group Map
Plot competitors on two strategic dimensions (e.g., price vs scope). Shows positioning clusters and white space.

### 7 Powers (Hamilton Helmer)
Durable advantage audit. Seven moats: Scale Economies, Network Economies, Counter-Positioning, Switching Costs, Branding, Cornered Resource, Process Power. Use to stress-test whether your edge is defensible.

### Technology S-curves
Plot adoption maturity. Use to time bets: do not invest Horizon-3 capital in a commodity-curve tech, or Horizon-1 capital in an unproven one.

## Internal Lens Frameworks — Know Thyself

### VRIO
Valuable / Rare / Inimitable / Organized-to-exploit. Four-box audit of each capability. Only capabilities that pass all four are sources of sustained advantage.

### Value Chain (Porter)
Primary activities (inbound, ops, outbound, marketing, service) + support (firm infra, HR, tech, procurement). Diagnose which links create vs destroy value.

### Core Competency (Hamel & Prahalad)
Three tests: (1) provides potential access to many markets, (2) adds perceived customer benefit, (3) difficult to imitate. Use for long-term capability investment choices.

### Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton)
Financial / Customer / Process / Learning perspectives. Use as the strategy-to-execution bridge.

## Portfolio & Growth Frameworks

### Ansoff Matrix
Market Penetration / Market Development / Product Development / Diversification. Use for growth-vector choice when revenue plateau appears.

### BCG Growth-Share
Stars / Cash Cows / Question Marks / Dogs. Portfolio allocation on market growth × relative share. Simple but powerful for prune/invest decisions.

### GE-McKinsey 9-box
Industry attractiveness × Business strength, each on 3-point scale. Use when BCG is too simplistic (multi-factor assessment needed).

### Three Horizons (McKinsey / Baghai)
- **H1** — core business, maintain and extend (12 months)
- **H2** — emerging business, build (1–3 years)
- **H3** — future options, seed (3–5+ years)

Mandatory for every strategy output. Every initiative must be H1, H2, or H3 tagged.

### Blue Ocean / ERRC (Kim & Mauborgne)
- **Eliminate** — which factors competitors take for granted
- **Reduce** — what to reduce below industry standard
- **Raise** — what to raise above industry standard
- **Create** — what to offer that the industry has never offered

Use to find uncontested space.

## IT-Specific Strategy Frameworks

### Wardley Mapping (Simon Wardley)
**The most IT-native strategy tool.** Plot components on two axes: value chain (y, user need at top) × evolution (x: genesis → custom-built → product/rental → commodity/utility). Shows what to build, what to buy, what to partner on, and when. Essential for cloud/AI/platform strategy.

Use for:
- Cloud migration timing
- Build vs buy vs partner decisions
- Platform bets (which PaaS, which AI)
- Commoditization threats to existing offerings

### Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder)
Nine blocks: Customer Segments, Value Proposition, Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams, Key Resources, Key Activities, Key Partners, Cost Structure. Use for business model design and comparison.

### Lean Canvas (Maurya)
Variant of BMC for early-stage / new offerings. Replaces some blocks with Problem, Solution, Key Metrics, Unfair Advantage.

## Dynamic / Futures Thinking

### Scenario Planning (2×2)
Two critical uncertainties → four future scenarios. For each: wind-tunnel test your strategy. Produces strategies that are robust under multiple futures.

### Real Options
Investment decisions as financial options: delay, abandon, scale, switch. Use when uncertainty is high; do not force NPV precision on speculative bets.

### Cynefin (Snowden)
Problem-space diagnosis: Simple / Complicated / Complex / Chaotic / Disorder. Different domains require different approaches:
- Simple — best practice
- Complicated — good practice + experts
- Complex — probe-sense-respond (DT fits here)
- Chaotic — act-sense-respond
- Disorder — find out which domain you are in

### OODA Loop (Boyd)
Observe → Orient → Decide → Act. Competitive response cycle. Whoever runs this faster wins.

### Hedgehog Concept (Jim Collins)
Intersect three circles: what you are passionate about, what you can be best at, what drives your economic engine. The overlap is the focus.

## Decision & Communication Frameworks

### MECE Decomposition (McKinsey)
Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive. Every problem tree should be MECE.

### Pyramid Principle (Barbara Minto)
Answer first, then supporting arguments, then evidence. Executive-grade communication default.

### Hypothesis Tree
Top-level hypothesis → sub-hypotheses → evidence to test each. Enables hypothesis-driven work rather than boil-the-ocean research.

### Pre-mortem (Gary Klein)
Imagine the strategy failed. Work backward to find what killed it. Surfaces risks before they materialize.

## Framework Selection Heuristic

| Strategic question | Recommended framework |
|---|---|
| Should we enter this vertical? | 5 Forces + PESTEL + Market sizing |
| How defensible is our edge? | 7 Powers + VRIO |
| What is our portfolio balance? | Three Horizons + BCG or 9-box |
| What should our cloud/AI platform strategy be? | Wardley Map |
| How uncertain is this bet? | Scenario 2×2 + Real Options |
| How do we reposition vs competitors? | Strategic Group Map + Blue Ocean ERRC |
| What is our integrated strategy? | Playing-to-Win Choice Cascade |
| How do we translate strategy to execution? | Strategy Map + Balanced Scorecard + OKR cascade |
| What could kill this plan? | Pre-mortem + Red Team |

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- Single-answer strategy (strategy is choice; always produce ≥3 options)
- Hidden trade-offs ("what we are not doing" must be explicit)
- SWOT as a finished product (it is an input, not an output)
- False precision (do not pretend to forecast the unpredictable)
- Strategy without execution bridge (OKRs, roadmap, risk register mandatory)
- Copying a competitor's strategy (strategy is differentiation, not imitation)
