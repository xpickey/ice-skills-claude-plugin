# Porter's Five Forces Template

Michael Porter's framework for assessing industry attractiveness and the structural forces that shape profitability. Use as a macro scan before account strategy or market entry.

## The Five Forces

```
            Threat of
          New Entrants
                │
                ↓
Bargaining                      Bargaining
Power of     →  Industry   ←   Power of
Suppliers       Rivalry          Buyers
                ↑
                │
            Threat of
           Substitutes
```

## Force-by-Force Assessment

### 1. Industry Rivalry
How intense is competition among existing players?

Signals of HIGH rivalry:
- Many competitors of similar size
- Slow industry growth
- High fixed / exit costs
- Undifferentiated products
- Price wars, frequent discounting

Signals of LOW rivalry:
- Few competitors, clear hierarchy
- Growing market
- Differentiated offerings
- Stable pricing

Enterprise software example (Thai Oracle mid-market):
- Oracle, SAP, MSFT, Salesforce, NetSuite, Infor all competing
- Rivalry moderated by vendor specialization (Oracle vs SAP vs MSFT)
- Implementation partner tier creates sub-markets
- Overall: **MODERATE-HIGH**

### 2. Threat of New Entrants
How easily can new competitors enter?

Barriers to entry (HIGH barrier = LOW threat):
- Economies of scale
- Capital requirements
- Switching costs for customers
- Access to distribution / channels
- Government regulation / licenses
- Brand / reputation
- Proprietary technology or IP

Enterprise software example (Thai Oracle mid-market):
- Oracle partnership tiers (Elite / Select / Standard) are hard-won
- Certified consultants in short supply
- Existing incumbent relationships create switching cost
- **LOW threat** in the short term; **MODERATE** from hyperscalers bundling

### 3. Bargaining Power of Buyers
How much leverage do customers have?

Buyer power is HIGH when:
- Few large buyers, many sellers
- Product is undifferentiated / commoditized
- Low switching costs
- Buyers are price-sensitive
- Buyers can backward-integrate (DIY)

Enterprise software example:
- Mid-market buyers individually small, but concentrated with a few large SIs
- Oracle / SAP products are switchable with effort — moderate switching cost
- Thai buyers often highly price-sensitive
- DIY is growing (low-code, open-source)
- **MODERATE-HIGH**

### 4. Bargaining Power of Suppliers
How much leverage do your suppliers have?

Supplier power is HIGH when:
- Few suppliers, many buyers
- No substitutes for the input
- High switching cost to change supplier
- Supplier can forward-integrate

Enterprise software implementation partner example:
- Oracle / SAP / MSFT are the "suppliers" of software; very few, so power is HIGH
- Cloud hyperscalers (AWS / Azure / OCI / GCP) are concentrated
- Talent market (Oracle consultants) is tight
- **HIGH**

### 5. Threat of Substitutes
What alternatives could replace the category entirely?

Substitute threat is HIGH when:
- Alternatives offer similar value at lower cost
- Switching is easy
- Industry dynamics are shifting

Enterprise software substitute examples:
- Low-code / no-code platforms (Odoo, Notion, Airtable) for SME
- Build-vs-buy — custom AI / internal platform teams
- Outsourced BPO eliminating need for ERP
- Spreadsheets (yes, still)
- **MODERATE and rising** for smaller customers

## Synthesis — Industry Attractiveness

Score each force: Low (favorable) / Moderate / High (unfavorable).

**Net industry attractiveness:**
- If 4–5 forces are Low: attractive — invest
- If 3 forces are Low: acceptable — compete selectively
- If only 1–2 forces are Low: unattractive — niche or exit

## Implication for Strategy

Each force points to a strategic move:
- **High rivalry** → differentiate or find a niche (Where to Play)
- **Low barriers** → build a moat (7 Powers, switching cost, scale, brand)
- **High buyer power** → add stickiness, value-beyond-feature
- **High supplier power** → vertically integrate or diversify suppliers
- **High substitutes** → innovate, embed, or reposition

## Workshop Run (90 min)

1. Define the industry scope precisely (20 min) — "we are analyzing [segment] in [geography]"
2. Score each force with evidence (50 min) — 10 minutes per force
3. Synthesize and name strategic implications (20 min)

## Common Pitfalls

- **Too broad industry definition** — "enterprise software" is not an industry; "Thai Oracle Fusion mid-market implementation" is
- **No evidence** — scores based on opinion; require data / quotes
- **Static analysis** — add a "where this force is moving" arrow for dynamic assessment
- **No strategic implication** — the analysis is worthless without "therefore we should..."

## Quality Bar

- Industry scope is specific
- Each force has at least 3 pieces of evidence
- Dynamic direction (rising / falling) noted for each force
- Strategic implications named
- Connects to a Where-to-Play / How-to-Win choice
