# Choice Cascade Template (Playing to Win)

Roger Martin & A.G. Lafley's five-question cascade. The most useful tool in the strategy canon for turning aspirations into enforceable choices. Every answer cascades into the next.

## The Five Questions

```
1. What is our Winning Aspiration?
        ↓
2. Where will we Play?
        ↓
3. How will we Win?
        ↓
4. What Capabilities must we have?
        ↓
5. What Management Systems do we need?
```

Every answer must support the one above it. If it does not, the strategy is incoherent.

## Question 1 — Winning Aspiration

What does winning look like? Not a mission statement; a measurable statement of success.

Template:
> *"To [verb] [what] for [whom] by [when], measured by [metric]."*

Example:
> *"To be the leading Thai provider of Oracle Cloud ERP implementations for mid-market manufacturers by FY28, measured by 30% market share in BOI-certified manufacturing accounts."*

## Question 2 — Where to Play

Choose segments, geographies, customer types, channels, product categories. Equally important: where you will **not** play.

Dimensions:
- **Geography** — which countries / regions / cities
- **Customer type** — which verticals / sizes / maturities
- **Product scope** — which offerings / modules
- **Channel** — direct / partner / platform / self-serve
- **Stage of buyer journey** — which you serve

Template:
> *"We will play in [specific segments / geos / channels] and NOT play in [segments / geos / channels we explicitly decline]."*

Example:
> *"We will play in Thai mid-market manufacturing (฿500M–฿10B revenue) needing Oracle Fusion Cloud ERP, sold direct with BOI-certified partner co-sell. We will NOT play in Thai public sector (served by dedicated government practice), SME under ฿500M (unprofitable), or Indochina cross-border (no delivery presence)."*

## Question 3 — How to Win

Given where we play, what is our **differentiated** way of winning? Porter's generic strategies (cost / differentiation / focus) are the starting point, but specificity matters more than framework labels.

Questions to answer:
- Why will customers in our Where-to-Play choose us over competitors?
- What is our sustainable advantage (7 Powers lens)?
- What trade-offs are we accepting to win here?

Template:
> *"We will win by [unique value] enabled by [source of advantage], accepting the trade-off of [what we give up]."*

Example:
> *"We will win by being the only Thai partner delivering Oracle Fusion Cloud with a fixed-price, audit-defensible BOI methodology, enabled by our Cloud Elite partnership and in-house BOI compliance IP, accepting the trade-off of not competing on technology variety — we do Oracle only."*

## Question 4 — Capabilities

What do we need to be uniquely good at to execute the Where / How?

Format:
- **Core capabilities** — the 3–5 must-have strengths
- **Table-stakes capabilities** — baseline needed to play
- **Capability gaps** — where we are short today
- **Capability plan** — how we will close the gaps (hire, partner, acquire, train)

Example capability set for the above strategy:
1. Oracle Fusion Cloud deep configuration expertise (core)
2. Thai accounting / BOI audit fluency (core)
3. Fixed-price methodology and estimation discipline (core)
4. Change management for mid-market manufacturing (core)
5. Local project leadership (core)
6. Baseline SAP / MSFT awareness for RFP response (table stakes)

## Question 5 — Management Systems

What processes, measures, and cultural norms will hold the strategy together?

Components:
- **Planning system** — how the strategy is updated
- **Metrics system** — what we measure and review
- **Incentive system** — how people are paid and promoted
- **Decision rights** — who decides what, with what input
- **Culture / values** — explicit norms reinforced in practice

Example:
- Monthly deal review anchored on BOI / Oracle pipeline
- Quarterly strategic fit review — are we drifting outside Where-to-Play?
- Partner compensation tied to Oracle Cloud Elite milestones
- Hiring bar: Oracle certified + Thai accounting / audit background

## Coherence Test

Walk the cascade top to bottom and bottom to top:
- Does Where-to-Play serve the Aspiration?
- Does How-to-Win give us advantage in our Where-to-Play?
- Do our Capabilities support How-to-Win?
- Do our Management Systems reinforce those Capabilities?

Any broken link = incoherent strategy. Fix or restart.

## Workshop Run (half-day)

1. Draft Aspiration (30 min)
2. Generate Where-to-Play options, narrow to 2–3 (60 min)
3. For each Where option, draft How-to-Win (60 min)
4. Assess coherence and pick the winning cascade (30 min)
5. Identify Capability gaps (30 min)
6. Draft Management Systems (30 min)
7. Commit and document (30 min)

## Quality Bar

- Aspiration is measurable and dated
- Where-to-Play names both where YES and where NO
- How-to-Win has a specific trade-off stated
- Capabilities are named, ranked, and gaps flagged
- Management Systems are concrete (not "we value excellence")
- Every level connects coherently to the one above
