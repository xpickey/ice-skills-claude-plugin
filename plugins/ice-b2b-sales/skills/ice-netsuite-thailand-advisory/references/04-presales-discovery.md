# Reference 04 — Pre-sales Discovery Playbook (Thai context)

Version: V01R01 | Date: 2026.05.23

## The qualifying question

Before you invest pre-sales time, ask yourself the question that matters: *what would have to be true for this customer to sign in <X> months at <Y> price?* If you cannot answer in one sentence, the deal is not yet qualified — keep discovering.

## Discovery session structure (90 minutes, 2-3 sessions)

### Session 1 — Frame and pain

Outcome: shared understanding of business context, current state pain, and trigger event.

Suggested flow:

1. Introductions and agenda alignment (5 min).
2. Customer's own description of their business — let them talk (15 min).
3. Current accounting/ERP environment — systems, integrations, who uses what (15 min).
4. Top three pain points, in customer's own words (20 min). Write them down verbatim.
5. Trigger event — why this conversation, why now (10 min).
6. Decision process — who, when, against what criteria (15 min).
7. Recap and confirm next session (10 min).

Do not pitch in Session 1. The customer will read it as "this consultant did not listen."

### Session 2 — Operational depth

Outcome: enough operational detail to scope a credible proposal.

Topics:

- Entity structure (Reference 02 patterns A-D).
- BOI promotions and special tax positions.
- Volume — transactions per month, users, integrations.
- Reporting requirements — statutory, management, board, regulatory.
- Pain points from Session 1 — quantify each one.
- Existing data — quality, completeness, willingness to migrate or restart clean.

Bring a process map or diagram. Customers respond well to a visual that shows you understood Session 1.

### Session 3 — Decision criteria and commercial frame

Outcome: enough commercial signal to decide whether to invest in a full proposal.

Topics:

- Confirmed budget envelope (or honest answer: aspirational, indicative, confirmed).
- Timeline — go-live ambition and drivers.
- Decision criteria — what the customer will weigh.
- Procurement process — direct, RFP, e-GP.
- Risk tolerance — appetite for SuiteSuccess vs custom, big-bang vs phased.
- Champion — who inside the customer is selling internally on your behalf.

If after Session 3 you cannot name a champion, the deal is not qualified yet.

## Red flags

When any of these appear, slow down or walk:

1. **"We have already decided we want NetSuite, just need help with the project."** Often means the technical decision was made without commercial pressure — the project will be underfunded and re-justified mid-flight.
2. **No named decision-maker.** "The committee will decide" — you will negotiate against an invisible chair.
3. **Reference-only request, no operational engagement.** Tire-kicking. Politely deprioritize.
4. **"We want a quick win in <X> weeks."** If <X> is less than 12 for a meaningful Thai NetSuite go-live, manage expectations or step away.
5. **Price asked before scope discussed.** Wrong order. Reframe.

## Champion development

A champion is not just an enthusiastic mid-level manager. A champion has:

- Authority or trusted access to the decision-maker.
- Personal upside if the project succeeds.
- Willingness to defend your proposal in rooms you are not in.

Develop the champion by giving them what they need to sell internally — a one-page summary they can forward, anticipated objections with answers, a tailored ROI calculation for their reporting line. Make them look good.

## Government and state enterprise context

If the customer is a ministry, state enterprise, or local government body, the discovery shape changes:

- TOR is the binding document, not a discovery transcript.
- e-GP rules constrain commercial structure — no off-cycle commercial creativity.
- The decision-maker is often a committee with rotating chair; champion development matters more.
- Reference architecture and prior experience with similar agencies carries disproportionate weight.

For these engagements, treat discovery as TOR analysis plus stakeholder mapping rather than open conversation.

## What to do with the discovery output

Convert each session's notes into the same six fields, every time:

```
Customer:           <name>
Date:               <date>
Session:            <1 / 2 / 3>
Attendees:          <names and roles>

Stated needs:       <what the customer said in their words>
Inferred needs:     <what you observed they need but did not say>
Open questions:     <what you still do not know>
Risks:              <what could derail this deal>
Next step:          <one concrete action, dated>
Decision:           <invest more / pause / decline>
```

The "Decision" field is the discipline that prevents endless pre-sales drift. After every session, write it down. If three sessions in a row land on "invest more" without commercial progress, reread Session 1's red flags.

## Bilingual handling during discovery

If the customer mixes Thai and English in conversation:

- Take notes in the language the customer uses most. Translate only when synthesizing the written summary.
- For technical terms, repeat in both languages on first use, then default to the customer's preference.
- If decision-makers are bilingual but operational team is Thai-primary, prepare two recap documents — Thai for the operational team, English for the decision-maker. Same content, different language. This is investment, not duplication.

## When to walk away

It is not weakness to decline a pre-sales opportunity. The opportunities you decline are the time you have to invest in the ones that will close. Walk when:

- The qualifying question stays unanswered after Session 3.
- The customer wants to engage on price before they will engage on scope.
- You cannot find a champion who has both authority and personal stake.
- The procurement structure (e-GP / TOR) is materially misaligned with what you can deliver.

Decline politely, professionally, and leave the door open. Markets are smaller than they look — the prospect you decline today may be the partner who refers you in 18 months.
