# Worked Example: Oracle Cloud ERP — Thai Manufacturing Group

> End-to-end walk-through showing how the WHY-to-MEDDPICC Bridge changed the outcome of a 180M-THB deal.

---

## Context

- **Customer:** large Thai manufacturing group (~50,000M THB revenue).
- **Scope:** Oracle Fusion Cloud ERP (Finance, Procurement, SCM).
- **Deal size:** 180M THB over 5 years.
- **Competition:** SAP S/4HANA, Workday.

---

## Before — MEDDPICC-only posture

| Element | What we had |
|---|---|
| Metrics | "-50% close cycle" |
| Economic Buyer | CFO (identified, lightly engaged) |
| Decision Criteria | Feature fit, price, timeline |
| Decision Process | RFP → Shortlist → Demo → BAFO |
| Paper Process | Board approval, legal review |
| Identify Pain | Legacy system, slow close |
| Champion | Finance Director |
| Competition | SAP, Workday |

**Position:** on the shortlist. Priced 15% below SAP — still losing. Deal at risk.

**Diagnosis:** Technical Fit archetype. High MEDDPICC, low WHY. Headed into a price war.

---

## WHY Discovery (what we found when we asked the right questions)

- **EB Personal WHY:** the new CFO wanted to prove in his first 18 months that he was a *strategic leader*, not "an accountant". He had been publicly labelled the latter.
- **Champion Personal WHY:** the Finance Director wanted to move her team from manual work to strategic analysis — she privately feared replacement if nothing changed within 18 months.
- **Organizational WHY:** the group was preparing to IPO in 24 months and needed audit-ready systems.

---

## Re-composed Why-Stack

### Why Change
> "The current system takes 15 days to close the books. The CFO cannot deliver board-ready data in time. This is not a technology problem — it is a question of whether finance becomes a **strategic function** or stays a back office."

### Why Now
> "Three forces converge in this quarter:
> 1. IPO in 24 months → audit-ready systems are non-negotiable.
> 2. The new CFO's 18-month window to prove impact.
> 3. Current Oracle license expires in Q3 — renewing it is wasted budget."

### Why Invest
- **Financial:** 230% ROI over 3 years; payback 18 months.
- **Strategic:** IPO readiness, M&A-ready architecture.
- **Personal:** the CFO will be remembered as "the CFO who modernized finance".

### Why Us (vs. SAP)
> "SAP can deliver what we can — but:
> 1. We IPO-enabled a Thai manufacturing group of 40,000M in 22 months (live reference).
> 2. We share your belief that finance is a strategic function — so we do not send a technical consultant to 'train' finance; we send a **former CFO** as advisor.
> 3. We accept a **20% fee-at-risk** clause on go-live milestones. SAP refused the same clause."

### Why Stay
> "A 5-year roadmap: ERP → EPM → Planning → AI-driven close. You are not buying an ERP — you are buying the journey to **CFO 4.0**."

---

## Outcome

- Deal won at **+8% over SAP**, not under.
- Expansion: Oracle EPM phase 2 = **+40M THB** in the following fiscal year.
- Reference customer for two subsequent deals.

---

## Why this flipped

1. **Price stopped being the battle.** Once the CFO saw himself in the story, the framing became "the CFO who modernized finance", not "the cheapest vendor".
2. **Champion gained political cover.** Her personal WHY was visible in the proposal — so her recommendation sounded strategic, not vendor-favoritism.
3. **Competition shifted from SAP to "do nothing".** Why Now (IPO + CFO window + license expiry) made delay the riskiest option.
4. **Risk moved onto us, where we could price it.** The 20% fee-at-risk clause was our Why Us proof — not a discount.

---

## Lessons for similar deals

- In regulated / IPO / M&A contexts, Why Now writes itself — find the window and name it.
- Always identify the *personal* WHY of the EB and Champion. Budgets unlock at the personal level, not the corporate level.
- A commercial risk-share clause is a Why Us proof point. Offer what competitors refuse.
- Frame Year 1 as the start of a multi-year journey (Why Stay). It changes the negotiating posture from "transaction" to "partnership".
